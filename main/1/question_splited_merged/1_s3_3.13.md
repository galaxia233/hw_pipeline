Problem 3.13. A two index "object" $\mathbf{X}^{\mu \nu}$ is defined by the "direct sum" of two vectors $\mathbf{X}^{\mu \nu} = \mathbf{A}^{\mu} + \mathbf{B}^{\nu}$ . Is $\mathbf{X}^{\mu \nu}$ a tensor? Is there a transformation law to take $\mathbf{X}$ to a new coordinate system, i.e. to obtain $\mathbf{X}^{\hat{\mu}\hat{\nu}}$ from $\mathbf{X}^{\mu \nu}$ ?

## Solution

Solution 3.13. The object cannot be a tensor. A $2^{\mathrm{nd}}$ rank covariant tensor is a functional of 2 covariant tensors, i.e. $\mathbf{X}^{\mu \nu}\mathbf{V}_{\mu}\mathbf{W}_{\nu}$ would have to be a scalar, but
$$
\mathbf {X} ^ {\mu \nu} := \mathbf {V} _ {\mu} \mathbf {W} _ {\nu} = (\mathbf {A} \cdot \mathbf {V}) \mathbf {W} _ {\nu} + (\mathbf {B} \cdot \mathbf {W}) \mathbf {V} _ {\mu}
$$
which is coordinate dependent.
If one defines the two index objects by the relation $\mathbf{X}^{\mu \nu} = \mathbf{A}^{\mu} + \mathbf{B}^{\nu}$ in any coordinate system, then the coordinate transformation law is $\mathbf{X}^{\mu^{\prime}\nu^{\prime}} = \Lambda_{a}^{\mu^{\prime}}\mathbf{A}^{a} + \Lambda_{\beta}^{\nu^{\prime}}\mathbf{B}^{\beta}$ . It is not possible to express this as
$$
\mathbf {X} ^ {\mu^ {\prime} \nu^ {\prime}} = \mathbf {T} _ {\alpha \beta} ^ {\mu^ {\prime} \nu^ {\prime}} \mathbf {X} ^ {\alpha \beta}
$$
where $\mathbf{T}$ is some transformation. This follows from the fact that there are many choices of $\mathbf{A}^{\mu}$ and $\mathbf{B}^{\nu}$ which give, in one coordinate system, the same $\mathbf{X}^{\mu \nu}$ . In particular, in a given coordinate system $\mathbf{A}^{\mu} + \mathbf{C}$ , $\mathbf{B}^{\nu} - \mathbf{C}$
give the same $\mathbf{X}^{\mu \nu}$ as $\mathbf{A}^{\mu}$ and $\mathbf{B}^{\nu}$ , but the two choices give very different $\mathbf{X}^{\mu^{\prime}\nu^{\prime}}$ .
