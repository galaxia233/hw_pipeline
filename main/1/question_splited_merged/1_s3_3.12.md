Problem 3.12. Prove that the general second-rank tensor in $n$ -dimensions cannot be represented as a simple direct product of two vectors, but can be expressed as a sum over many such products.

## Solution

Solution 3.12. A direct product of two vectors (see Problem 3.11) has all of its columns proportional to each other (similarly for all of its rows). This is not true for a general second rank tensor. If $\mathbf{e}_i$ $i = 1, n$ are the basis vectors $(0, 0, \dots, 1, \dots, 0, 0)$ where the 1 is in the $i^{th}$ slot, then the $n^2$ products $\mathbf{e}_i \otimes \mathbf{e}_j$ obviously span the space of second rank tensors, since there is one with a 1 uniquely in any row and column. Sums over these times constants obviously can give the general tensor. (Any one specific tensor can be represented as a sum of $n$ outer products, as the reader is invited to verify.)
