Problem 3.11. You are given a tensor $\mathbf{K}^{\alpha \beta}$ . How can you test whether it is a direct product of two vectors $\mathbf{K}^{\alpha \beta} = \mathbf{A}^{\alpha} \mathbf{B}^{\beta}$ ? Can you express the test in coordinate-free language?

## Solution

Solution 3.11. In a basis $\mathbf{K}$ is represented by a matrix $\mathbf{K}^{a\beta}$ . An obviously necessary and sufficient condition for $\mathbf{K}^{a\beta}$ to be expressible as $\mathbf{A}^{\alpha}\mathbf{B}^{\beta}$ is that all columns of the matrix are proportional to each other.
In basis-free language, $\mathbf{K}$ can be written as
$$
\mathbf {K} = \mathbf {A} \otimes \mathbf {B}
$$
if and only if the vector $\mathbf{W} = \mathbf{K} \cdot \mathbf{v}$ (i.e. $\mathbf{W}^{\alpha} = \mathbf{K}^{a\beta} \mathbf{v}_{\beta}$ ) is in the same direction for any choice of $\mathbf{v}$ . Proof: In some frame choose 4 basis vectors $\mathbf{e}^{0}, \mathbf{e}^{1}, \cdots$ to satisfy $\mathbf{e}^{\mu} \cdot \mathbf{e}_{\nu} = \delta_{\nu}^{\mu}$ . By linearity the direction of $\mathbf{W}$
is independent of any $\mathbf{v}$ if and only if it is independent of the 4 basis vectors $\mathbf{e}^{\mu}$ . Our condition then is
$$
\mathbf {K} \cdot \mathbf {e} ^ {0} = \lambda_ {0} \mathbf {W}, \quad \mathbf {K} \cdot \mathbf {e} ^ {1} = \lambda_ {1} \mathbf {W}, \dots
$$
or
$$
\mathrm {K} ^ {\alpha 0} = \lambda_ {0} \mathrm {W} ^ {\alpha}, \quad \mathrm {K} ^ {\alpha 1} = \lambda_ {1} \mathrm {W} ^ {\alpha} \dots .
$$
Our basis-free criterion then is equivalent to the columns being proportional.
