Problem 15.10. A radio commentator is describing his radial fall into a Schwarzschild black hole. Just before he crosses the Schwarzschild radius his broadcast frequency starts becoming redshifted enormously with a time dependence exp $(-t / \text{constant})$ , where $t$ measures proper time at infinity. From the constant deduce the mass of the hole.

## Solution

Solution 15.10. It is convenient to work in "outgoing Eddington-Finkelstein coordinates": Define $\mathbf{r}^*$ by $\mathrm{dr}^* / \mathrm{dr} = (1 - 2\mathbf{M} / \mathbf{r})^{-1}$ , that is
$$
\mathbf {r} ^ {*} = \mathbf {r} + 2 \mathbf {M} \log (\mathbf {r} / 2 \mathbf {M} - 1) + \text {c o n s t a n t}
$$
and define a retarded time coordinate
$$
\mathbf {u} = \mathbf {t} - \mathbf {r} ^ {*}
$$
The Schwarzschild metric in these coordinates is
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = - (1 - 2 \mathrm {M} / \mathrm {r}) \mathrm {d u} ^ {2} - 2 \mathrm {d u d r} + \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \mathrm {d} \Omega^ {2}. \tag {1}
$$
Putting $\mathrm{ds} = 0$ , we see that outgoing radial photons travel along lines $\mathbf{u} =$ constant. The redshift is calculated by comparing the time between reception of photons and emission of photons:
$$
\frac {\lambda_ {\infty}}{\lambda_ {\text {e m i t t e r}}} = \frac {(\Delta t) _ {\infty}}{(\Delta \tau) _ {\text {e m}}} = \frac {(\Delta u) _ {\infty}}{(\Delta \tau) _ {\text {e m}}} = \left. \frac {(\Delta u) _ {\text {e m}}}{(\Delta \tau) _ {\text {e m}}} = \frac {\mathrm {d} u}{\mathrm {d} \tau} \right| _ {\text {e m}}. \tag {2}
$$
Note how we can relate times at infinity to times at the emitter by using $u =$ constant along the rays.
We must therefore find the component $\mathrm{du} / \mathrm{d}\tau = \mathbf{U}^{\mathbf{u}}$ of the emitter's 4-velocity $\mathbf{U}$ as a function of t.
Now u and t are both ignorable coordinates and
$$
\mathrm {U} _ {\mathrm {t}} = \mathrm {U} _ {\mathrm {u}} = \text {c o n s t a n t} = - \tilde {\mathrm {E}} \tag {3}
$$
and $\mathbf{U}\cdot \mathbf{U} = -\mathbf{1}$ implies
![](images/cd41dda0fa8b84e6846d5afb92b49f93edb7e171ebda80df7eebe73c610b9a3f.jpg)
$$
U _ {r} = \frac {- \tilde {E} - \left(\tilde {E} ^ {2} - 1 + 2 M / r\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}}{1 - 2 M / r}, \tag {4}
$$
therefore
$$
\mathbf {U} ^ {\mathrm {u}} = \mathbf {g} ^ {\mathrm {u u}} \mathbf {U} _ {\mathrm {u}} + \mathbf {g} ^ {\mathrm {u r}} \mathbf {U} _ { r} = 0 + \left[ \tilde {\mathbf {E}} + \left(\tilde {\mathbf {E}} ^ {2} - 1 + 2 \mathbf {M} / \mathbf {r}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \right] (1 - 2 \mathbf {M} / \mathbf {r}) ^ {- 1} \qquad (5)
$$
and
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbf {U} ^ {\mathrm {r}} = \mathbf {g} ^ {\mathrm {u r}} \mathbf {U} _ {\mathrm {u}} + \mathbf {g} ^ {\mathrm {r r}} \mathbf {U} _ { \mathrm {r}} = \tilde {\mathbf {E}} + \left[ - \tilde {\mathbf {E}} - \left(\tilde {\mathbf {E}} ^ {2} - 1 + 2 \mathbf {M} / \mathbf {r}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \right] \\ = - \left(\tilde {\mathrm {E}} ^ {2} - 1 + 2 \mathrm {M} / \mathrm {r}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}. \tag {6} \\ \end{array}
$$
Thus
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {r}}{\mathrm {d} u} = \frac {\mathbf {U} ^ {r}}{\mathbf {U} ^ {u}} = \frac {- \left(\tilde {\mathbf {E}} ^ {2} - 1 + 2 \mathbf {M} / \mathbf {r}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} (1 - 2 \mathbf {M} / \mathbf {r})}{\tilde {\mathbf {E}} + \left(\tilde {\mathbf {E}} ^ {2} - 1 + 2 \mathbf {M} / \mathbf {r}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}} \tag {7}
$$
Near $\mathbf{r} = 2\mathbf{M}$ , Equation (7) gives
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathrm {d} \mathbf {u} \approx - 2 (1 - 2 \mathrm {M} / \mathrm {r}) ^ {- 1} \mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} \approx - 2 (\mathrm {r} / 2 \mathrm {M} - 1) ^ {- 1} \mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} \\ u \approx - 4 M \log (r / 2 M - 1) + c o n s t a n t \\ 1 - 2 \mathrm {M} / \mathrm {r} \sim \exp (- \mathrm {u} / 4 \mathrm {M}). \\ \end{array}
$$
Thus Equation (5) implies that, as $\mathbf{r} \to 2\mathbf{M}$
$$
U ^ {u} \sim e ^ {+ u / 4 M}.
$$
But $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{t} +$ constant for an observer at fixed (large) $\mathbf{r}$ , so finally
$$
\lambda_ {\text {e m i t t e r}} / \lambda_ {\infty} \sim \exp (- t / 4 M).
$$
