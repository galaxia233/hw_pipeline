Problem 2.13 (a) If a rocket has engines that give it a constant acceleration of $1 \, \text{g}$ (relative to its instantaneous inertial frame, of course), and the rocket starts from rest near the earth, how far from the earth (as measured in the earth's frame) will the rocket be in 40 years as measured on the earth? How far after 40 years as measured in the rocket?

## Solution

$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} ^ {2} \mathbf {u} ^ {\mathbf {x}}}{\mathrm {d} r ^ {2}} = \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {a} ^ {\mathbf {x}}}{\mathrm {d} r} = \textbf {g} \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {u} ^ {\mathbf {t}}}{\mathrm {d} r} = \textbf {g} \mathbf {a} ^ {\mathbf {t}} = \textbf {g} ^ {2} \mathbf {u} ^ {\mathbf {x}},
$$
with solutions $\mathbf{u}^{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{A}\sinh \mathbf{g}\tau +\mathbf{B}\cosh \mathbf{g}\tau$ . Since the initial conditions on the motion are $\mathbf{u}^{\mathbf{x}} = 0,\mathrm{d}\mathbf{u}^{\mathbf{x}} / \mathrm{d}\tau = \mathbf{g},$ at $\pmb {\tau} = 0$ , u must be
$$
\mathrm {u} ^ {\mathrm {x}} = \mathrm {d x} / \mathrm {d} r = \sinh \mathrm {g} r. \tag {6}
$$
$$
u ^ {t} = d t / d r = \cosh g r \quad (f r o m E q u a t i o n (1))
$$
The integrals of these equations (with $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{t} = 0$ at $\tau = 0$ ) give us
$$
\mathbf {x} = \mathbf {g} ^ {- 1} (\cosh \mathbf {g} \tau - 1) \quad \mathrm {t} = \mathbf {g} ^ {- 1} (\sinh \mathbf {g} \tau). \tag {7}
$$
In units with $c = 1$ , it turns out by numerical coincidence that $g(=980\mathrm{cm/sec}^2)$ is almost exactly equal to one inverse year (as a time) or one inverse light-year (as a distance). Thus for 40 years as measured on Earth ( $t = 40$ yr), (7) gives
$$
\tau \approx [ \sinh^ {- 1} (4 0) ] \mathrm {y r} \approx 4. 3 8 \mathrm {y r}
$$
and
$$
\mathbf {x} \approx [ \cosh [ \sinh^ {- 1} (4 0) ] - 1 ] L. Y. \approx 3 9. 0 1 L. Y. \tag {8}
$$
For 40 years in the rocket $\tau = 40$ yr and from (7)
$$
\mathbf {x} \approx [ \cosh (4 0) - 1 ] \mathrm {L . Y}. \approx 1 0 ^ {1 7} \mathrm {L . Y}. \tag {9}
$$
(b) To travel halfway requires $\mathbf{x} = 15,000$ L.Y., or
$$
\tau \approx \cosh^ {- 1} (1 5, 0 0 0 + 1) \mathrm {y r} \approx 1 0. 3 \mathrm {y r}. \tag {10}
$$
The deceleration half of the trip is identical, so the total time is 20.6 years.
(c) Denote the rest mass of the rocket (which changes) by M. The change in energy of the rocket equals the energy radiated
$$
\mathrm {d} \left(\mathrm {M u} ^ {0}\right) = - \mathrm {d E} _ {\text {r a d .}}. \tag {11}
$$
Since the energy is radiated as photons
$$
\mathrm {d E} _ {\text {r a d .}} = \mathrm {d P} _ {\text {r a d .}} \tag {12}
$$
and by conservation of momentum,
$$
\mathrm {d P} _ {\text {r a d .}} = \mathrm {d P} \tag {13}
$$
where $\mathrm{d}P$ is the change in momentum of the rocket. Combining Equations
(11)-(13) gives
$$
d \left(M u ^ {0}\right) = - d P = - d \left(M u ^ {x}\right)
$$
$$
\left(\mathrm {d M}\right) \mathbf {u} ^ {0} + \mathbf {M d u} ^ {0} = - (\mathrm {d M}) \mathbf {u} ^ {\mathbf {x}} - \mathbf {M d u} ^ {\mathbf {x}}
$$
$$
d M / M = - d \left(u ^ {0} + u ^ {x}\right) / \left(u ^ {0} + u ^ {x}\right)
$$
so that
$$
\mathrm {M} = \mathrm {M} _ {0} / \left(\mathrm {u} ^ {0} + \mathrm {u} ^ {\mathrm {x}}\right) = \mathrm {M} _ {0} \mathrm {e} ^ {- \mathrm {g} \tau} \tag {14}
$$
where we have used Equations (6) to give us $\mathfrak{u}^0 (\tau)$ and $\mathbf{u}^{\mathbf{x}}(\tau)$ .
From part (b), $e^{g^T} = 30,000$ for half the trip. Thus, $M_{\frac{1}{2}} = M_0 / 30,000$ and
$$
\mathbf {M} _ {\mathrm {f i n a l}} = \mathbf {M} _ {0} / (3 0, 0 0 0) ^ {2} \approx 1 0 ^ {- 9} \mathbf {M} _ {0}.
$$
