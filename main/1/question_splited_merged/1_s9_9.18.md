Problem 9.18. A beam of light has a circular cross section at some point along its path. Show that the beam experiences no shear (i.e. the cross-section is not deformed into an ellipse) when the Weyl tensor is zero.

## Solution

Solution 9.18. If the Weyl tensor vanishes, the metric can be written in the conformally flat form
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = \mathrm {e} ^ {2 \phi} \mathrm {d s} _ {0} ^ {2} = \mathrm {e} ^ {2 \phi} \eta_ {\mu \nu} \mathrm {d x} ^ {\mu} \mathrm {d x} ^ {\nu}
$$
where $\phi$ is a function of $\mathbf{x}^a$ . The null geodesics
$$
\mathrm {d t} = \underline {{\mathbf {n}}} \cdot \underline {{\mathrm {d x}}}, \quad \underline {{\mathbf {n}}} \cdot \underline {{\mathbf {n}}} = 1
$$
of $\mathbf{ds}_0^2$ remain null curves for $\mathbf{ds}^2$ . If we can show that they remain null geodesics, then clearly a cone of null geodesics, will remain a cone; there will be no preferred direction for squashing of the cross section.
To show the curves $\mathrm{dt} = \underline{\mathbf{n}}\cdot \underline{\mathbf{dx}}$ are geodesics we need only show that
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} ^ {2} \mathbf {x} ^ {\alpha}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda^ {2}} = \frac {\mathrm {d p} ^ {\alpha}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} = 0
$$
for null geodesics. Note that the Christoffel symbols are
$$
\Gamma_ {\gamma \beta} ^ {a} = \eta^ {a \mu} [ - \phi_ {, \mu} \eta_ {\gamma \beta} + \phi_ {, \beta} \eta_ {\gamma \mu} + \phi_ {, \gamma} \eta_ {\mu \beta} ]
$$
so that
$$
\mathbf {p} ^ {\gamma} \mathbf {p} ^ {\beta} \Gamma_ {\gamma \beta} ^ {\alpha} = 2 \mathbf {p} ^ {\alpha} (\nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf {p})
$$
and
$$
0 = \frac {\mathrm {d p} ^ {\alpha}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} + \Gamma^ {\alpha} _ {\beta \gamma} \mathrm {p} ^ {\beta} \mathrm {p} ^ {\gamma} = \frac {\mathrm {d p} ^ {\alpha}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} + 2 \mathrm {p} ^ {\alpha} (\nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf {p}) .
$$
By rescaling $\lambda$ , then we have $\mathrm{dp}^{\alpha} / \mathrm{d}\lambda = 0$ and the straight null lines of $\mathrm{ds}_0^2$ are null geodesics of $\mathrm{ds}^2$ .
Notice that, while beams of light have undistorted cross section the cross sectional area, like all areas, is affected by the scaling factor $e^{2\phi}$ .
Since $\phi$ is in general anisotropic, beams propagating in different directions will grow in cross section at different rates.
