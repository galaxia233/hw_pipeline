Problem 7.21. Show that the paths of light rays in a static, isotropic spacetime can be described by taking the space to have a certain spatially
varying “index of refraction” $\mathfrak{n}(\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{j}})$ . What is $\mathfrak{n}$ in terms of $\mathfrak{g}_{\alpha \beta}$ ? Assume $\mathfrak{g}_{\alpha \beta}$ has the form $\mathrm{ds}^2 = g_{\alpha \beta} \mathrm{dt}^2 - f(\mathrm{dx}^2 + \mathrm{dx}^2 + \mathrm{dx}^2)$ .

## Solution

Solution 7.21. The paths of light rays can be obtained from the geometrical optics limit of Maxwell's equations $\mathbf{F}^{\alpha \beta}$ ; $\beta = 0$ . With the identity in Problem 7.7(i), these equations can be written as
$$
\left[ \mathbf {g} ^ {\alpha \tau} \mathbf {g} ^ {\beta \mu} \mathbf {F} _ {\tau \mu} (\dot {-} \mathbf {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \right], \beta = 0.
$$
Using the spatially isotropic, diagonal form of the metric and noting that $\mathbf{E}_{\mathbf{i}} = \mathbf{F}_{0\mathbf{i}}, \mathbf{B}_{\mathbf{k}} = \varepsilon^{\mathbf{k}\mathbf{j}\ell} \mathbf{F}_{\mathbf{j}\ell}$ , one sees that the above equations have the form
$$
\nabla \cdot (\epsilon \underline {{\mathbf {E}}}) = 0
$$
$$
\nabla \times (\mu^ {- 1} \underline {{\mathbf {B}}}) = \partial (\epsilon \underline {{\mathbf {E}}}) / \partial \mathfrak {t}
$$
where $\epsilon = \mu = (\mathbf{f} / \mathbf{g}_{00})^{\frac{1}{2}}$ . Thus light moves as if in a medium with an effective index of refraction
$$
n = (\epsilon \mu) ^ {- \frac {1}{2}} = (g _ {0 0} / f) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}
$$
