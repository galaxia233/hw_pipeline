Problem 12.4. The position of the sun in the sky can in principle be measured by a sensitive tidal gravimeter. What is the angular difference between this position and its position as measured optically? If the actual position of the sun were at its optical position, there would be a force in the direction of the earth's motion. (Why?) If this were the case find the radius of the earth's orbit as a function of time.

## Solution

Solution 12.4. Laplace first realized that there must be a difference in positions. The position of the sun in the sky is displaced by the finite velocity of light; in other words, is aberrated by the velocity $\mathbf{v}$ of the earth in its orbit, by an angle $\theta = \mathbf{v} / \mathbf{c}$ . The Coulomb gravitational field is not aberrated: If the force of gravity pointed in this direction, there would be a component of solar acceleration $(\mathrm{GM}_{\odot} / r^{2})(\mathrm{v} / \mathrm{c})$ in the direction of the earth's motion, so the earth's energy would increase at a rate given by
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} (\mathrm {E} / \mathrm {M} _ {\oplus})}{\mathrm {d t}} = \frac {\mathrm {G M} _ {\odot}}{\mathrm {r} ^ {2}} \frac {\mathrm {v} ^ {2}}{\mathrm {c}}
$$
but $\mathrm{E} / \mathrm{M}_{\oplus} = -\frac{1}{2}\mathrm{GM}_{\odot} / \mathrm{r},$ so the energy loss rate implies $\mathrm{dr} / \mathrm{dt} = 2\mathrm{v}^2 /\mathrm{c}$ Since $\mathbf{v}^2 = \mathbf{G}\mathbf{M}_{\odot} / \mathbf{r}$ this is easily integrated to give
$$
\mathbf {t} - \mathbf {t} _ {0} = \frac {\mathbf {c}}{4 \mathbf {G M}} \left(\mathbf {r} ^ {2} - \mathbf {r} _ {0} ^ {2}\right) .
$$
In particular, the earth's orbit has $\mathbf{r} = 1.5 \times 10^{13}$ cm, $\mathbf{v} = 30$ km/sec, the radius of the sun is $\mathbf{r}_0 = 7 \times 10^{10}$ cm, so $\theta \approx 10^{-4}$ , and $t - t_0 \approx 1.3 \times 10^{10}$ sec $\approx 400$ years. This is much less than the known geological time during which the radius of the earth's orbit is known to have been constant.
