Problem 3.10. If $\Lambda_{\beta}^{a}$ and $\tilde{\Lambda}_{\beta}^{a}$ are two matrices which transform the components of a tensor from one coordinate basis to another, show that the matrix $\Lambda_{\gamma}^{a}\tilde{\Lambda}_{\beta}^{\gamma}$ is also a coordinate transformation.

## Solution

Solution 3.10. Suppose one coordinate transformation is
$$
\bar {\mathbf {x}} ^ {\mu} = \bar {\mathbf {x}} ^ {\mu} \left(\mathbf {x} ^ {\nu}\right), \quad \text {s o} \quad \Lambda_ {\beta} ^ {\alpha} = \partial_ {\bar {\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\alpha} / \partial_ {\mathbf {x}} ^ {\beta} \tag {1}
$$
and the other is
$$
\tilde {\mathbf {x}} ^ {\mu} = \tilde {\mathbf {x}} ^ {\mu} \left(\mathbf {x} ^ {\nu}\right), \quad \text {s o} \quad \tilde {\Lambda} _ {\beta} ^ {\alpha} = \partial_ {\tilde {\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\alpha} / \partial_ {\mathbf {x}} ^ {\beta}. \tag {2}
$$
The product matrix is then
$$
\Lambda_ {\gamma} ^ {\alpha} \tilde {\Lambda} _ {\beta} ^ {\gamma} = \frac {\partial_ {\overline {{\mathbf {x}}}} ^ {\alpha}}{\partial_ {\mathbf {x}} ^ {\gamma}} \frac {\partial_ {\tilde {\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\gamma}}{\partial_ {\mathbf {x}} \beta}. \tag {3}
$$
This looks suspiciously like the chain rule for partial derivatives, so one is led to consider the coordinate transformation
$$
\overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\mu} (\mathbf {x} ^ {\beta}) = \overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\mu} \left[ \tilde {\mathbf {x}} ^ {\gamma} (\mathbf {x} ^ {\beta}) \right], \tag {4}
$$
whose transformation matrix is obviously
$$
\bar {\Lambda} _ {\beta} ^ {\mu} = \frac {\partial_ {\overline {{\mathbf {x}}}} ^ {\mu}}{\partial_ {\mathbf {x}} \beta} = \frac {\partial_ {\overline {{\mathbf {x}}}} ^ {\mu}}{\partial_ {\widetilde {\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\gamma}} \frac {\partial_ {\widetilde {\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\gamma}}{\partial_ {\mathbf {x}} \beta}. \tag {5}
$$
This differs from (3) only by a twiddle. A little thought shows that the twiddle is not meaningful. Partial derivatives are taken with respect to argument variables; it makes no difference what symbols are used to represent these variables. Thus (4) is in fact the coordinate transformation of the product matrix.
