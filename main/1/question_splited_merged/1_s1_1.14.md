Problem 1.14. Show that an observer's 4-acceleration $\mathrm{du}^{\alpha} / \mathrm{d}\tau$ has only 3 independent components, and give the relation of these to the 3 components of ordinary acceleration that he would measure with a Newtonian accelerometer in his local frame.

## Solution

Solution 1.14. 4-velocities are normalized so that $\mathbf{u}^{\alpha}\mathbf{u}_{\alpha} = -\mathbf{1}$ , so
$$
0 = \frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d} \tau} \left(\mathrm {u} ^ {\alpha} \mathrm {u} _ {\alpha}\right) = 2 \frac {\mathrm {d u} ^ {\alpha}}{\mathrm {d} \tau} \mathrm {u} _ {\alpha} = 2 \mathrm {a} ^ {\alpha} \mathrm {u} _ {\alpha}.
$$
This is one constraint on the 4-components of acceleration $\mathbf{a}^{\alpha}$ . In the momentarily comoving frame $\mathfrak{u}_{\alpha} = (-1,0)$ , and the constraint requires $\mathsf{a}^{0} = 0$ , with $\mathsf{a}^{\mathrm{j}}(\mathrm{j} = 1,2,3)$ arbitrary. A Newtonian accelerometer can be modeled as follows: Have the observer release a particle in the comoving frame and see how much velocity $\underline{\mathrm{d}\nu}$ the observer picks up relative to it in a short time $\mathrm{d}\tau$ , then calculate $\underline{\mathsf{a}}_{\text{Newtonian}} = \underline{\mathrm{d}\nu} / \mathrm{d}\tau$ . Of course the particle is really stationary in the momentarily comoving inertial frame, and we accelerate relative to it by an amount $\mathrm{du}^{\mathrm{j}} = \mathsf{a}^{\mathrm{j}}\mathrm{d}\tau$ . Now $\mathbf{u}^{\mathrm{j}} = \mathbf{v}^{\mathrm{j}}(1 - \mathbf{v}^{2})^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ , so $\mathrm{du}^{\mathrm{j}} = (1 - \mathbf{v}^{2})^{-\frac{1}{2}}\mathrm{dv}^{\mathrm{j}} + \mathbf{v}^{\mathrm{j}}\mathrm{d}(1 - \mathbf{v}^{2})^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ . But $\mathbf{v} = 0$ since the frames are momentarily comoving, and thus $\mathrm{du}^{\mathrm{j}} = \mathrm{dv}^{\mathrm{j}}$ , so finally
$$
a _ {\text {N e w t o n i a n}} ^ {j} = d v ^ {j} / d \tau = d u ^ {j} / d \tau = a ^ {j}.
$$
The 3 independent components of 4-acceleration in a comoving frame are just 3 Newtonian accelerations.
