Problem 19.10. Suppose astronomers are able to find a family of objects whose absolute luminosities $L$ are known. Suppose their apparent luminosities $\ell$ (or equivalently their luminosity distance $d_{L}$ ) and their redshift $z$ are measured. Using the Robertson-Walker line element, find an expression for $\ell$ (or $d_{L}$ ) as a function of $L$ , $z$ , $H_{0}$ and $q_{0}$ for small $z$ .

## Solution

Solution 19.10. From the solution to Problem 19.9 we have $\mathsf{d}_{\mathbf{L}} = \mathsf{R}_0^2\mathsf{r}_1 / \mathsf{R}(\mathsf{t}_1)$ where $\mathbf{r}_1$ is the radial coordinate of the object and $\mathsf{R}(\mathsf{t}_1)$ is the scale factor at the time $\mathbf{t}_1$ at which the light was emitted. Since $\mathbf{H}_0 \equiv \dot{\mathbf{R}} / \mathbf{R}$ and $\mathbf{q}_0 \equiv -\ddot{\mathbf{R}}\mathbf{R} / \dot{\mathbf{R}}^2$ , the first terms in the power series expansion of $\mathbf{R}$ are
$$
\mathrm {R} (t) = \mathrm {R} _ {0} \left(1 + \mathrm {H} _ {0} \left(t - t _ {0}\right) - \frac {1}{2} \mathrm {q} _ {0} \mathrm {H} _ {0} ^ {2} \left(t - t _ {0}\right) ^ {2} + \dots\right). \tag {1}
$$
We can eliminate the factor $\mathbb{R}(t_1)$ from $d_L$ using $\mathbb{R}_0 / \mathbb{R}(t_1) = 1 + z$ , but we still need to find expressions for $\mathbb{R}_0$ and for $r_1$ . Setting $ds^2 = 0$ to get the path of light rays in the Robertson-Walker metric gives
$$
\int_ {t _ {1}} ^ {t _ {0}} \frac {\mathrm {d} t}{R (t)} = \int_ {0} ^ {r _ {1}} \frac {\mathrm {d} r}{(1 - \mathrm {k r} ^ {2}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}} \tag {2}
$$
Integrating Equation (2) to the lowest 2 orders in $(t_0 - t_1)$ and $r_1$ by means of Equation (1) gives
$$
r _ {1} = \frac {1}{R _ {0}} \left[ \left(t _ {0} - t _ {1}\right) + \frac {1}{2} H _ {0} \left(t _ {0} - t _ {1}\right) ^ {2} + \dots \right] \tag {3}
$$
and inverting Equation (1) to get $t - t_0$ in terms of $1 + z = R_0 / R(t_1)$
$$
\mathrm {g i v e s} \quad \mathrm {R} _ {0} \mathrm {r} _ {1} = \frac {1}{\mathrm {H} _ {0}} \left[ z - \frac {1}{2} (1 + \mathrm {q} _ {0}) z ^ {2} + \dots \right]
$$
so that
$$
\mathrm {d} _ {\mathbf {L}} = (1 + z) \mathrm {R} _ {0} \mathrm {r} _ {1} = \frac {1}{\mathrm {H} _ {0}} \left[ z + \frac {1}{2} \left(1 - \mathrm {q} _ {0}\right) z ^ {2} + \dots \right]
$$
or equivalently
$$
\ell = \frac {\mathrm {L}}{4 \pi \mathrm {d} _ {\mathrm {L}} ^ {2}} = \frac {\mathrm {L H} _ {0} ^ {2}}{4 \pi z ^ {2}} [ 1 + (\mathrm {q} _ {0} - 1) z + \dots ].
$$
