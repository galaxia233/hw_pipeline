Problem 15.4. Derive the equations of motion (equations relating $t$ , $r$ and $\tau$ ) for a particle falling radially in the Schwarzschild geometry. Consider the three cases (i) particle released from rest at $r = R$ (ii) particle released from rest at infinity (iii) particle projected inward from infinity with velocity $v_{\infty}$ .

## Solution

Solution 15.4. From the first integrals of the geodesic equation (see
Problem 15.3)
$$
u _ {0} = - \tilde {\mathbf {E}} = \text {c o n s t a n t}
$$
$$
\mathrm {g} ^ {0 0} \mathrm {u} _ {0} ^ {2} + \mathrm {g} _ {\mathrm {r r}} \left(\mathrm {u} ^ {\mathrm {r}}\right) ^ {2} = - 1
$$
we get
$$
u ^ {0} = \frac {\mathrm {d} t}{\mathrm {d} r} = \frac {\tilde {E}}{1 - 2 M / r} \tag {1}
$$
$$
u ^ {r} = \frac {d r}{d r} = - \left(\tilde {E} ^ {2} - 1 + 2 M / r\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}. \tag {2}
$$
The minus sign for the square root in Equation (2) corresponds to an infalling particle.
Case (i). For this case $\mathrm{dr} / \mathrm{d}\tau = 0$ at $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{R}$ implies $2\mathbf{M} / \mathbf{R} = 1 - \tilde{\mathbf{E}}^2$ , i.e. $\tilde{\mathbf{E}} < 1$ . Thus Equation (2) can be rewritten
$$
\mathrm {d} \tau = \frac {\mathrm {d r}}{\left(\frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {r}} - \frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {R}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}} \tag {3}
$$
This can be integrated in closed form to give
$$
\tau = \left(\frac {\mathrm {R} ^ {3}}{8 \mathrm {M}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \left[ 2 \left(\frac {\mathrm {r}}{\mathrm {R}} - \frac {\mathrm {r} ^ {2}}{\mathrm {R} ^ {2}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} + \cos^ {- 1} \left(\frac {2 \mathrm {r}}{\mathrm {R}} - 1\right) \right] \tag {4}
$$
where the constant of integration has been chosen so that $\tau = 0$ at $r = R$ . It is convenient to rewrite Equation (4) in parametric form by introducing the "cycloid parameter"
$$
\eta = \cos^ {- 1} \left(\frac {2 r}{R} - 1\right)
$$
so that we have
$$
r = \frac {1}{2} R (1 + \cos \eta) \quad (\eta = 0 \text {a t} r = R) \tag {5}
$$
$$
\tau = \left(\frac {\mathrm {R} ^ {3}}{8 \mathrm {M}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} (\eta + \sin \eta). \tag {6}
$$
Equation (1) gives
$$
\begin{array}{l} t = \int \frac {\tilde {E} d \tau}{\left(1 - \frac {2 M}{r}\right)} = \tilde {E} \int \frac {d \tau}{d \eta} \frac {1}{1 - \frac {2 M}{r}} d \eta \\ = \left(1 - \frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {R}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \int \frac {\left(\frac {\mathrm {R} ^ {3}}{8 \mathrm {M}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} (1 + \cos \eta) \mathrm {d} \eta}{1 - 4 \mathrm {M} [ \mathrm {R} (1 + \cos \eta) ] ^ {- 1}}. \\ \end{array}
$$
From a table of integrals, we find
$$
\frac {t}{2 \mathrm {M}} = \log \left| \frac {\left(\frac {\mathrm {R}}{2 \mathrm {M}} - 1\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} + \tan \left(\frac {\eta}{2}\right)}{\left(\frac {\mathrm {R}}{2 \mathrm {M}} - 1\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} - \tan \left(\frac {\eta}{2}\right)} \right| + \left(\frac {\mathrm {R}}{2 \mathrm {M}} - 1\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \left[ \eta + \frac {\mathrm {R}}{4 \mathrm {M}} (\eta + \sin \eta) \right] \tag {7}
$$
where the constant of integration has been chosen so that $t = 0$ at $\eta = 0$ i.e. at $r = R$ . Note that $t \to \infty$ when $\tan(\eta/2) \to (R/2M - 1)^{\frac{1}{2}}$ i.e. when $r \to 2M$ .
Case (ii). Here $\tilde{\mathbf{E}} = 1$ and Equation (2) gives
$$
\tau = - \frac {2}{3} \left(\frac {\mathrm {r} ^ {3}}{2 \mathrm {M}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} + \text {c o n s t a n t} \tag {8}
$$
and since
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} t}{\mathrm {d} r} = \frac {\mathrm {d} t / \mathrm {d} \tau}{\mathrm {d} r / \mathrm {d} \tau}
$$
we get
$$
t = - \frac {2}{3} \left(\frac {r ^ {3}}{2 M}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} - 4 M \left(\frac {r}{2 M}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} + 2 M \log \left| \frac {\left(\frac {r}{2 M}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} + 1}{\left(\frac {r}{2 M}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} - 1} \right| + \text {c o n s t a n t}. \tag {9}
$$
Case (iii). By analogy with case (i), choose R such that
$$
\frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {R}} = \tilde {\mathrm {E}} ^ {2} - 1 = \left(1 - \mathrm {v} _ {\infty} ^ {2}\right) ^ {- 1} - 1 = \frac {\mathrm {v} _ {\infty} ^ {2}}{1 - \mathrm {v} _ {\infty} ^ {2}} .
$$
Then changing the sign of $\mathbf{R}$ in Equation (4) gives
$$
\tau = - \left(\frac {\mathrm {R} ^ {3}}{8 \mathrm {M}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \left[ 2 \left(\frac {\mathrm {r}}{\mathrm {R}} + \frac {\mathrm {r} ^ {2}}{\mathrm {R} ^ {2}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} - \cosh^ {- 1} \left(\frac {2 \mathrm {r}}{\mathrm {R}} + 1\right) \right]. \tag {10}
$$
Note that $\tau = 0$ at $\mathbf{r} = 0$ , and $\tau = -\infty$ at $\mathbf{r} = \infty$ . Put
$$
\eta = \cosh^ {- 1} \left(\frac {2 r}{R} + 1\right)
$$
and get
$$
r = \frac {R}{2} (\cosh \eta - 1) \quad (r = 0 \text {a t} \eta = 0) \tag {11}
$$
$$
\tau = - \left(\frac {\mathrm {R} ^ {3}}{8 \mathrm {M}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} (\sinh \eta - \eta). \tag {12}
$$
The equation corresponding to Equation (7) is
$$
\frac {t}{2 \mathbb {M}} = \log \left| \frac {\left(\frac {\mathrm {R}}{2 \mathbb {M}} + 1\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} + \coth \frac {\eta}{2}}{\left(\frac {\mathrm {R}}{2 \mathbb {M}} + 1\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} - \coth \frac {\eta}{2}} \right| - \left(\frac {\mathrm {R}}{2 \mathbb {M}} + 1\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \left[ \eta + \frac {\mathrm {R}}{4 \mathbb {M}} (\sinh \eta - \eta) \right]. \tag {13}
$$
Note that $t$ goes from $-\infty$ to $+\infty$ as $r$ goes from $\infty$ to $2M$ .
