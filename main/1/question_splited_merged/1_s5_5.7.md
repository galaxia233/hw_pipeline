Problem 5.7. An infinitesimally thin rod of length $2a$ has a point mass $m$ at each of its ends. The center of the rod is fixed in the laboratory and the rod rotates about this point with a relativistic angular velocity $\omega$ . (i.e. $\omega \ell$ is comparable with $c$ ). Assume the rod is massless. What is $\mathbf{T}^{\mu \nu}$ for the rod and particle system?

## Solution

Solution 5.7. Let $S'$ be the instantaneous rest frame of an element of

the rod. In this rest frame the only nonzero components are $\mathbf{T}^{0^{\prime}0^{\prime}} = \rho$ , $\mathbf{T}^{\mathbf{x}'\mathbf{x}'} = \mathfrak{p}$ . If we Lorentz transform to the lab frame we find the nonzero components: $\mathbf{T}^{\mathbf{xx}} = \mathfrak{p}$ , $\mathbf{T}^{\mathbf{yy}} = \gamma^{2}\beta^{2}\rho$ , $\mathbf{T}^{00} = \gamma^{2}\rho$ , $\mathbf{T}^{0y} = \gamma^{2}\beta\rho$ , where $\beta = \omega r$ and $\gamma \equiv (1 - \beta^{2})^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ . If spherical polar coordinates are used the nonzero components are
$$
\mathbf {T} ^ {\mathbf {r r}} = \mathbf {p}, \quad \mathbf {T} ^ {\phi \phi} = \gamma^ {2} \beta^ {2} \rho / \mathbf {r} ^ {2}, \quad \mathbf {T} ^ {0 \phi} = \gamma^ {2} \beta \rho / \mathbf {r}, \quad \mathbf {T} ^ {0 0} = \gamma^ {2} \rho .
$$
To find $\mathfrak{p}(\mathbf{r})$ we can use the $\mathbf{r}$ component of the equation of motion
$$
\begin{array}{l} 0 = \mathrm {T} ^ {\mathrm {r} \nu} _ {; \nu} = \mathrm {T} _ {, r} ^ {\mathrm {r r}} + \mathrm {T} ^ {\phi \phi} \Gamma_ {\phi \phi} ^ {\mathrm {r}} + \mathrm {T} ^ {\mathrm {r r}} \left[ \log (- \mathrm {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \right] _ {, r} \\ \mathbf {\tau} = \left(\mathrm {T} ^ {\mathrm {r r}} \mathrm {r} ^ {2}\right) _ {, \mathrm {r}} / \mathrm {r} ^ {2} - \mathrm {r} \sin^ {2} \theta \mathrm {T} ^ {\phi \phi} \quad , \\ \end{array}
$$
so that
$$
\left(\operatorname {p r} ^ {2}\right) _ {, \mathrm {r}} = \mathrm {r} \sin^ {2} \theta \gamma^ {2} \beta^ {2} \rho .
$$
Since the only mass density is in the point masses we have
$$
\rho = \mathrm {m} \delta (\mathrm {r} - \mathrm {a}) \delta (\cos \theta) [ \delta (\phi - \omega \mathrm {t}) + \delta (\phi - \omega \mathrm {t} - \pi) ] / \mathrm {r} ^ {2}.
$$
We can now integrate the equation of motion with the boundary condition $\mathfrak{p} = 0$ for $\mathbf{r} > \mathbf{a}$ , to find $\mathfrak{p}$ . When this is done the components of the stress-energy tensor (in the lab, described by coordinates $t, r, \theta, \phi$ ) are seen to be
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbf {T} ^ {\mu \nu} = \frac {\mathfrak {m}}{1 - \omega^ {2} \mathfrak {a} ^ {2}} \delta (\cos \theta) \left[ \delta (\phi - \omega \mathfrak {t}) + \delta (\phi - \omega \mathfrak {t} - \pi) \right] \\ \times \left[ \begin{array}{c c c c} \frac {\delta (\mathrm {r} - \mathrm {a})}{\mathrm {a} ^ {2}} & 0 & 0 & \frac {\omega \delta (\mathrm {r} - \mathrm {a})}{\mathrm {a} ^ {2}} \\ & - \frac {\omega^ {2} \mathrm {a}}{\mathrm {r} ^ {2}} & 0 & 0 \\ & & 0 & 0 \\ \text {s y m m e t r i c} & & & \frac {\omega^ {2} \delta (\mathrm {r} - \mathrm {a})}{\mathrm {a} ^ {2}} \end{array} \right] \\ \end{array}
$$
