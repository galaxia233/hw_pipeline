Problem 3.3. Show that the only non-spacelike vectors orthogonal to a given nonzero null vector are multiples of it.

## Solution

Solution 3.3. Without loss of generality, choose coordinates to make the null vector $\mathbf{V} = \mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{x}} + \mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{t}}$ . A general vector is $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{t}} + \mathbf{B}\mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{x}} + \mathbf{C}\mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{y}} + \mathbf{D}\mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{z}}$ . The dot product is $\mathbf{S} \cdot \mathbf{V} = \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{t}} \cdot \mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{t}}) + \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{x}} \cdot \mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{x}}) = \mathbf{B} - \mathbf{A}$ so A must equal B if S is orthogonal to V. Now the vector S is spacelike unless
$\mathbf{A}^2 \geq \mathbf{B}^2 + \mathbf{C}^2 + \mathbf{D}^2$ which means $\mathbf{C}^2 + \mathbf{D}^2 = 0$ and hence $\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{D} = 0$ , therefore $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{x}} + \mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{t}})$ , which is a multiple of $\mathbf{V}$ .
