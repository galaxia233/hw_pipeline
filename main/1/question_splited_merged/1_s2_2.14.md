Problem 2.14. What is the maximum energy one could get out of a fixed frequency electron cyclotron with accelerating potential $V$ .

## Solution

Solution 2.14. A cyclotron applies an accelerating potential with a fixed frequency, to the gap between two “dees.” The frequency is chosen so that the potential is always in the right direction when the electron crosses the gap, i.e. it is the cyclotron frequency $\omega_0 = \mathrm{eB} / \mathrm{mc}$ . The reason that there is a maximum energy is that the electron actually circles at the “synchrotron frequency” $\omega = \mathrm{eB} / \gamma \mathrm{mc} = \omega_0 \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{E}$ (where $\mathbf{E} =$ electron energy), and as the electron becomes relativistic this gets out of phase with the fixed cyclotron frequency. Eventually the electron arrives at the gap when the potential is $90^\circ$ out of phase and the accelerating process breaks down. Quantitatively if the phase of the accelerating potential is $\alpha$ when the electron passes through the gap, the electron receives energy V cos $\alpha$ , where V is the maximum accelerating potential expressed in electron volts. In dN cycles of the electron motion then
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d (e l e c t r o n e n e r g y)}}{\mathrm {d N}} = \frac {\mathrm {d E}}{\omega \mathrm {d t} / 2 \pi} = 2 \mathrm {V} \cos (\phi - \omega_ {0} \mathrm {t}).
$$
[Note that the electron passes through the gap twice per cycle, hence the factor of 2.] Here $\phi$ is the angular distance traveled by the electron
$$
\phi = \int \omega d t = \omega_ {0} \int_ {0} ^ {t} (m / E) d t
$$
and
$$
\begin{array}{l} \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {E}}{\mathrm {d} t} = \frac {\omega}{2 \pi} 2 \mathrm {V} \cos \left[ \omega_ {0} \int_ {0} ^ {t} \frac {\mathrm {m}}{\mathrm {E}} \mathrm {d} t - \omega_ {0} t \right] \\ = \frac {V}{\pi E} \omega_ {0} m \cos \left[ \omega_ {0} \int_ {0} ^ {t} \left(\frac {m}{E} - 1\right) d t \right]. \\ \frac {d E ^ {2}}{d t} = a \cos \left[ \omega_ {0} \int_ {0} ^ {t} \left(\frac {m}{E} - 1\right) d t \right] \\ \end{array}
$$
where $\mathbf{a} \equiv 2\mathbf{V}\omega_0\mathfrak{m} / \pi$ . Differentiating we have
$$
\begin{array}{l} \frac {d ^ {2} \left(E ^ {2}\right)}{d t ^ {2}} = - a \sin \left[ \omega_ {0} \int_ {0} ^ {t} \left(\frac {m}{E} - 1\right) d t \right] \omega_ {0} \left(\frac {m}{E} - 1\right) \\ = \left[ a ^ {2} - \left(\frac {\mathrm {d E} ^ {2}}{\mathrm {d t}}\right) ^ {2} \right] ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \omega_ {0} \left(\frac {\mathrm {m}}{\mathrm {E}} - 1\right). \\ \end{array}
$$
The negative square root has been used because the sine function above is clearly negative.
We now find a first integral of this equation. Let $\mathbf{q} \equiv \mathrm{d}\mathbf{E}^2/\mathrm{d}t$ so that $\mathrm{d}^2\mathbf{E}^2/\mathrm{d}t^2 = \mathbf{qd}\mathbf{q}/\mathrm{d}\mathbf{E}^2$ , then our differential equation becomes
$$
- q \frac {d q}{d E ^ {2}} = [ a ^ {2} - q ^ {2} ] ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \omega_ {0} \left[ 1 - \frac {m}{(E ^ {2}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}} \right].
$$
This can be integrated with the initial condition that $\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{a}$ at $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{m}$ (i.e. at $t = 0$ ):
$$
\left(a ^ {2} - q ^ {2}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} = \omega_ {0} (E - m) ^ {2}.
$$
The energy keeps increasing until $\mathbf{q} = 0$ , at which time the energy must be
$$
\mathbf {E} _ {\mathrm {m a x}} = \mathbf {m} + \mathbf {a} / \omega_ {0} = \mathbf {m} + (2 \mathbf {V m} / \pi) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}.
$$
