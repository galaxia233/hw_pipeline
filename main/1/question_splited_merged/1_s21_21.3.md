Problem 21.3. If $\mathbf{L}$ is a Lagrangian density as in Problem 21.2, the stress-energy tensor can be defined by a variation of $\mathbf{g}_{\mu \nu}$ in $S$ :
$$
\delta S = \intop \frac {\delta (\mathrm {L} (- \mathrm {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}})}{\delta \mathbf {g} _ {\mu \nu}} \delta \mathbf {g} _ {\mu \nu} \mathrm {d} ^ {4} \mathbf {x} \equiv \frac {1}{2} \intop \mathrm {T} ^ {\mu \nu} \delta \mathbf {g} _ {\mu \nu} (- \mathbf {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \mathrm {d} ^ {4} \mathbf {x} .
$$
Show that $\mathbf{T}^{\mu \nu}_{;\nu} = 0$ follows from the equation of motion of the field and the fact that S is a scalar.

## Solution

Solution 21.3. Since $\mathbf{L}(-\mathbf{g})^{\frac{1}{2}}\mathbf{d}^{4}\mathbf{x}$ is a scalar, it is unchanged under the infinitesimal coordinate transformation
$$
\overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\mu} = \mathbf {x} ^ {\mu} + \xi^ {\mu}.
$$
By renaming the dummy variable of integration, $\mathsf{d}^4\overline{\mathbf{x}}$ can be changed back to $\mathsf{d}^4\mathbf{x}$ . The net change in $\mathbf{L}(-\mathbf{g})^{\frac{1}{2}}$ is therefore the Lie derivative (see Problem 8.17), so that
$$
\begin{array}{l} 0 = \delta S = \int \mathcal {L} _ {\xi} (L (- g) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}) d ^ {4} x \\ = \int \left[ \frac {\delta \underline {{\mathrm {L}}}}{\delta \Phi^ {\mathrm {A}}} \left(\mathcal {Q} _ {\xi} \Phi^ {\mathrm {A}}\right) (- \mathrm {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} + \frac {\delta (\mathrm {L} (- \mathrm {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}})}{\delta \mathrm {g} _ {\mu \nu}} \mathcal {Q} _ {\xi} \mathrm {g} _ {\mu \nu} \right] d ^ {4} x. \tag {1} \\ \end{array}
$$
The first term in the integrand vanishes by the equation of motion (Problem
21.2), thus
$$
0 = \frac {1}{2} \int \mathrm {T} ^ {\mu \nu} \mathcal {L} _ {\pmb {\xi}} \mathbf {g} _ {\mu \nu} (- \mathbf {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \mathrm {d} ^ {4} \mathbf {x} .
$$
Since
$$
\mathcal {L} _ {\xi} \mathrm {g} _ {\mu \nu} = \xi_ {\mu ; \nu} + \xi_ {\nu ; \mu}
$$
we get
$$
\begin{array}{l} 0 = \int \mathrm {T} ^ {\mu \nu} \xi_ {\mu ; \nu} (- \mathrm {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \mathrm {d} ^ {4} \mathrm {x} \tag {2} \\ = \int \left(\mathrm {T} ^ {\mu \nu} \xi_ {\mu}\right) _ {; \nu} (- \mathrm {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \mathrm {d} ^ {4} \mathrm {x} - \int \mathrm {T} _ {; \nu} ^ {\mu \nu} \xi_ {\mu} (- \mathrm {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \mathrm {d} ^ {4} \mathrm {x}. \\ \end{array}.
$$
The first term is
$$
\int \left(\mathrm {T} ^ {\mu \nu} \xi_ {\mu}\right) _ {; \nu} (- \mathrm {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \mathrm {d} ^ {4} \mathrm {x} = \int \left((- \mathrm {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \mathrm {T} ^ {\mu \nu} \xi_ {\mu}\right) _ {, \nu} \mathrm {d} ^ {4} \mathrm {x}
$$
and gives a surface integral which vanishes. Since $\xi_{\mu}$ is arbitrary,
Equation (2) then gives
$$
\mathrm {T} _ {; \nu} ^ {\mu \nu} = 0.
$$
