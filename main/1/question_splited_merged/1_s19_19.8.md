Problem 19.8. Show by an explicit coordinate transformation that the Robertson-Walker metric is conformally flat. Write $\mathbf{R}_{\mu \nu a\beta}$ in terms of $\mathbf{g}_{\mu \nu}$ and $\rho, p$ and the 4-velocity $\mathfrak{u}^{\mu}$ of the matter.

## Solution

Solution 19.8. Start with the $\eta, \chi$ form of the metric
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = \mathrm {R} ^ {2} (\eta) \left[ - \mathrm {d} \eta^ {2} + \mathrm {d} \chi^ {2} + \Sigma^ {2} (\chi) \mathrm {d} \Omega^ {2} \right] \tag {1}
$$
(3rd form in Problem 19.5), where $\Sigma(\chi) = \sinh \chi$ , $\chi$ , $\sin \chi$ for $k = -1, 0, +1$ . Since conformal transformations preserve light cones, a natural first step is to go to null coordinates, e.g.
$$
u = \frac {1}{2} (\eta + \chi) \tag {2}
$$
$$
v = \frac {1}{2} (\eta - \chi)
$$
The metric now becomes
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = \mathrm {R} ^ {2} (\mathrm {u} + \mathrm {v}) [ - 4 \mathrm {d u d v} + \Sigma^ {2} (\mathrm {u} - \mathrm {v}) \mathrm {d} \Omega^ {2} ]. \tag {3}
$$
We now try a transformation which preserves the “‘nullness’” of the coordinates, and also preserves the symmetry between u and v:
$$
\begin{array}{l} a = \mathrm {g} (\mathrm {u}) \quad \mathrm {u} = \mathrm {f} (\alpha) \tag {4} \\ \beta = \mathbf {g} (\mathbf {v}) \quad \mathbf {v} = \mathbf {f} (\beta) \\ \end{array}
$$
where $g$ is the inverse function to $f$ . With this transformation, Equation (3) becomes
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = \mathrm {R} ^ {2} (\mathrm {u} + \mathrm {v}) \left[ - 4 \mathrm {f} ^ {\prime} (\alpha) \mathrm {f} ^ {\prime} (\beta) \mathrm {d} \alpha \mathrm {d} \beta + \Sigma^ {2} (\mathrm {u} - \mathrm {v}) \mathrm {d} \Omega^ {2} \right] \\ = \mathrm {R} ^ {2} (\mathrm {u} + \mathrm {v}) \mathrm {f} ^ {\prime} (\alpha) \mathrm {f} ^ {\prime} (\beta) \left[ - 4 \mathrm {d} \alpha \mathrm {d} \beta + \frac {\sum^ {2} (\mathrm {u} - \mathrm {v})}{\mathrm {f} ^ {\prime} (\alpha) \mathrm {f} ^ {\prime} (\beta)} \mathrm {d} \Omega^ {2} \right]. \tag {5} \\ \end{array}
$$
If the metric is to be conformally flat, the term in brackets must have the flat space form $[-4\mathrm{d}\alpha \mathrm{d}\beta + (\alpha - \beta)^2\mathrm{d}\Omega^2]$ . Using $\mathbf{f}'(\alpha) = \frac{\mathrm{d}\mathbf{u}}{\mathrm{d}\alpha} = \left(\frac{\mathrm{d}\alpha}{\mathrm{d}\mathbf{u}}\right)^{-1} = [\mathbf{g}'(\mathbf{u})]^{-1}$ , we get the condition
$$
\mathbf {g} ^ {\prime} (\mathbf {u}) \mathbf {g} ^ {\prime} (\mathbf {v}) [ \boldsymbol {\Sigma} (\mathbf {u} - \mathbf {v}) ] ^ {2} = [ \mathbf {g} (\mathbf {u}) - \mathbf {g} (\mathbf {v}) ] ^ {2}. \tag {6}
$$
For the case $k = 0$ , we have $\Sigma(u - v) = u - v$ , so $g(x) = x$ is a solution by inspection. For the other cases Equation (6) can be solved by first finding the solution for $v$ close to $u$ ; that is, for $v = u + \epsilon$ . Expanding Equation (6) in a Taylor series gives
$$
\begin{array}{l} \left(\mathrm {g} ^ {\prime}\right) ^ {2} \left[ 1 + \epsilon \mathrm {g} ^ {\prime \prime} / \mathrm {g} ^ {\prime} + \epsilon^ {2} \mathrm {g} ^ {\prime \prime \prime} / (2 \mathrm {g} ^ {\prime}) \dots \right] \epsilon^ {2} \left(1 - k \epsilon^ {2} / 6 \dots\right) ^ {2} \\ = \epsilon^ {2} (\mathrm {g} ^ {\prime}) ^ {2} \left[ 1 + \epsilon \mathrm {g} ^ {\prime \prime} / (2 \mathrm {g} ^ {\prime}) + \epsilon^ {2} \mathrm {g} ^ {\prime \prime \prime} / (6 \mathrm {g} ^ {\prime}) \dots \right] ^ {2}, \\ \end{array}
$$
so that with $\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{g}'$ , $\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{p}'$ and $\mathbf{k} = +1$ , we must solve
$$
2 q d q / d p - 4 p = 3 q ^ {2} / p.
$$
This Bernoulli differential equation gives
$$
q = p (A p - 4) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}, \quad A = c o n s t a n t,
$$
and integrating twice we get
$$
\begin{array}{l} u + B = \tan^ {- 1} (A p / 4 - 1) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \\ \mathbf {g} (\mathfrak {u}) = \mathbf {C} \tan (\mathfrak {u} + \mathbf {B}) + \mathbf {D}. \\ \end{array}
$$
We can now verify by substitution that this is in fact the general solution to Equation (6). Without loss of generality we can take $\mathbf{g} = \tan u$ . For $k = -1$ , we can similarly find $\mathbf{g} = \tanh u$ . With these solutions, Equation (6) turns into the trigonometric identities
$$
\sec u \sec v \sin (u - v) = \tan u - \tan v
$$
sech u sech v sinh (u-v) = tanh u - tanh v
and the metric of Equation (5) becomes
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = \frac {\mathrm {R} ^ {2} \left[ \tan^ {- 1} (\alpha) + \tan^ {- 1} (\beta) \right]}{\left(1 + \alpha^ {2}\right) \left(1 + \beta^ {2}\right)} \{- 4 \mathrm {d} \alpha \mathrm {d} \beta + (\alpha - \beta) ^ {2} \mathrm {d} \Omega^ {2} \}
$$
or
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = \frac {\mathrm {R} ^ {2} \left[ \tanh  ^ {- 1} (\alpha) + \tanh  ^ {- 1} (\beta) \right]}{\left(1 - \alpha^ {2}\right) \left(1 - \beta^ {2}\right)} \{- 4 \mathrm {d} \alpha \mathrm {d} \beta + (\alpha - \beta) ^ {2} \mathrm {d} \Omega^ {2} \}
$$
which is manifestly conformally flat. A conformally flat metric has zero Weyl tensor, so the Riemann tensor is only composed of the Ricci tensor and scalar curvature (see the introduction to Chapter 9).
$$
\mathsf {R} _ {\gamma \delta} ^ {\alpha \beta} = 2 \delta_ {[ \gamma} ^ {[ \alpha} \mathsf {R} _ {\delta ]} ^ {\beta ]} - \frac {1}{3} \delta_ {[ \gamma} ^ {[ \alpha} \delta_ {\delta ]} ^ {\beta ]} \mathsf {R}. \tag {8}
$$
With this the Einstein equation
$$
\mathrm {R} _ {\beta} ^ {\alpha} = 8 \pi \left(\mathrm {T} _ {\beta} ^ {\alpha} - \frac {1}{2} \mathrm {g} _ {\beta} ^ {\alpha} \mathrm {T}\right)
$$
and the stress energy tensor
$$
\mathbf {T} _ {\beta} ^ {\alpha} = (\rho + \mathrm {p}) \mathbf {u} ^ {\alpha} \mathbf {u} _ {\beta} + \mathrm {p g} _ {\beta} ^ {\alpha}
$$
give $\mathbf{R}^{\alpha \beta}_{\gamma \delta}$ in terms of $\mathbf{g}_{\mu \nu},\mathfrak{u}^{\mu},\rho ,\mathfrak{p}$
