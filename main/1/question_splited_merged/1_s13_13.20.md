Problem 13.20. A hypothetical particle of negative gravitational mass $-\mathbf{M}$ is released from rest at a distance $\ell >> \mathbf{M}$ from another fixed particle of equal positive mass $+\mathbf{M}$ . As seen by a static observer, what is the magnitude and direction of the acceleration of each particle? Calculate the motion of the particles after they are released, making any reasonable approximations necessary.

## Solution

Solution 13.20. By the principle of equivalence, the acceleration of a body is independent of the magnitude of its mass, and depends only on other masses with which it gravitates. Thus a positive mass attracts both positive and negative masses, and a negative mass repels them both.
Here, then, the acceleration of the negative mass is toward the positive mass, and of magnitude $\mathbf{GM} / \ell^2$ . (Since $\ell >> \mathbf{M}$ , we are in the Newtonian limit.) The acceleration of the positive mass is in the same direction and of the same magnitude: the two masses try to chase each other.
The problem is more complicated once the particles are moving, because each sees the retarded field of the other.
Since we are given $\mathbf{M} \ll \ell$ , we shall solve the problem using linearized theory which will be justified as long as their distance in a frame comoving with either remains much greater than $\mathbf{M}$ . The equation
$$
\square \bar {h} _ {\mu \nu} = - 1 6 \pi T _ {\mu \nu} \tag {1}
$$
has the retarded integral solution
$$
\bar {h} _ {\mu \nu} (t, \underline {{x}}) = 4 \int \frac {T _ {\mu \nu} \left(t ^ {\prime} , \underline {{x}} ^ {\prime}\right)}{\left| \underline {{x}} - \underline {{x}} ^ {\prime} \right|} \delta \left(t ^ {\prime} + \left| \underline {{x}} - \underline {{x}} ^ {\prime} \right| - t\right) d ^ {3} x ^ {\prime} d t ^ {\prime}. \tag {2}
$$
In linearized theory we can superpose the $\mathbf{T}_{\mu \nu}$ 's (that is the $\mathbf{h}_{\mu \nu}$ 's) of each particle. For a particle of mass $m$ moving along a world-line
$$
\mathbf {x} ^ {\mu} = \mathbf {z} ^ {\mu} (\tau) \tag {3}
$$
we have
$$
T _ {\mu \nu} (t, \underline {{x}}) = m \int u _ {\mu} u _ {\nu} \delta^ {4} (x - z (r)) d r = m u _ {\mu} u _ {\nu} \delta^ {3} (\underset {\sim} {x} - \underset {\sim} {z} (t)) / u ^ {0} \tag {4}
$$
where we have used $\mathrm{d}r = \mathrm{d}t / \mathrm{u}^0$ to do the integral. Substitute Equation (4) in Equation (2):
$$
\begin{array}{l} \bar {h} _ {\mu \nu} (t, \underline {{x}}) = 4 m \int \frac {u _ {\mu} u _ {\nu} \delta^ {3} [ \underline {{x}} ^ {\prime} - \underline {{z}} (\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime}) ] \delta (\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime} + | \underline {{x}} - \underline {{x}} ^ {\prime} | - t) d ^ {3} x ^ {\prime} d t ^ {\prime}}{u ^ {0} | \underline {{x}} - \underline {{x}} ^ {\prime} |} \\ = 4 \mathrm {m} \int \frac {\mathrm {u} _ {\mu} \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime}\right) \mathrm {u} _ {\nu} \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime}\right) \delta \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime} + \left| \underline {{\mathrm {x}}} - \underline {{z}} \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime}\right) \right| - \mathrm {t}\right) \mathrm {d t} ^ {\prime}}{\mathrm {u} ^ {0} \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime}\right) \left| \underline {{\mathrm {x}}} - \underline {{z}} \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime}\right) \right|}. \tag {5} \\ \end{array}
$$
By symmetry everything depends only on one spatial coordinate, say x.
Let
$$
\mathrm {R} \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime}\right) = | \mathrm {x} - \mathrm {z} \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime}\right) | = \epsilon [ \mathrm {x} - \mathrm {z} \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime}\right) ] \tag {6}
$$
where $\epsilon = \pm 1$ for $x \gtrless z$ . Then
$$
\bar {h} _ {\mu \nu} (t, x) = 4 m \int^ {\infty} \frac {u _ {\mu} u _ {\nu} \delta \left(t ^ {\prime} + R - t\right) d t ^ {\prime}}{u ^ {0} R}. \tag {7}
$$
The 4-velocity $\mathbf{u}$ is determined from the equation of motion
$$
\mathrm {d} \mathbf {u} ^ {\mu} / \mathrm {d} \tau = - \Gamma_ {\alpha \beta} ^ {\mu} \mathbf {u} ^ {\alpha} \mathbf {u} ^ {\beta}
$$
i.e.
$$
\mathrm {d} u ^ {\mu} / \mathrm {d} \tau + \eta^ {\mu \nu} u ^ {\alpha} u ^ {\beta} \left(h _ {\nu a, \beta} + h _ {\nu \beta , a} - h _ {a \beta , \nu}\right) / 2 = 0. \tag {8}
$$
Here the $\mathbf{h}_{\alpha \beta}$ of particle 2 evaluated at the position of particle 1 is used to find $\mathfrak{u}^{\mu}$ of particle 1 and vice versa. By symmetry $\mathfrak{u}^{\mathbf{y}} = \mathfrak{u}^{\mathbf{z}} = 0$ for both particles. Write out Equation (8) for $\mu = 0$ and $\mu = \mathbf{x}$ , using $\mathbf{h}_{00} = \mathbf{h}_{\mathbf{xx}}$ (easily verified from Equation (7)):
$$
\mathrm {d} u ^ {0} / \mathrm {d} \tau = \frac {1}{2} h _ {0 0, 0} \left(u ^ {0 ^ {2}} - u ^ {x ^ {2}}\right) + h _ {0 0, x} u ^ {0} u ^ {x} + h _ {0 x, x} u ^ {x ^ {2}} \tag {10}
$$
$$
\mathrm {d} u ^ {x} / \mathrm {d} \tau = \frac {1}{2} h _ {0 0, x} \left(u ^ {0 ^ {2}} - u ^ {x ^ {2}}\right) - h _ {0 x, 0} u ^ {0 ^ {2}} - h _ {0 0, 0} u ^ {0} u ^ {x}. \tag {11}
$$
Now evaluate the derivatives of the $\mathfrak{h}_{\alpha \beta}$ from Equation (7):
$$
\overline {{\mathbf {h}}} _ {0 0, 0} = 4 \mathrm {m} \frac {\partial}{\partial t} \int \frac {\mathbf {u} ^ {0} (\mathbf {t} ^ {\prime}) \delta (\mathbf {t} ^ {\prime} + \mathbf {R} (\mathbf {t} ^ {\prime}) - \mathbf {t}) \mathrm {d t} ^ {\prime}}{\mathbf {R} (\mathbf {t} ^ {\prime})} = - 4 \mathrm {m} \int \frac {\mathbf {u} ^ {0} \delta^ {\prime} (\mathbf {t} ^ {\prime} + \mathbf {R} - \mathbf {t}) \mathrm {d t} ^ {\prime}}{\mathbf {R}}
$$
where $\delta'$ denotes differentiation with respect to the argument of the delta function. Let
$$
\mathbf {f} \left(\mathbf {t} ^ {\prime}\right) = \mathbf {t} ^ {\prime} + \mathbf {R} \left(\mathbf {t} ^ {\prime}\right)
$$
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} f}{\mathrm {d} t ^ {\prime}} = 1 - \epsilon v (t ^ {\prime}), \quad v = \frac {\mathrm {d} z}{\mathrm {d} t ^ {\prime}}.
$$
Then
$$
\begin{array}{l} \overline {{{\mathsf {h}}}} _ {0 0, 0} = - 4 \mathrm {m} \int \frac {\mathsf {u} ^ {0}}{\mathsf {R}} \left[ \frac {\mathsf {d}}{\mathsf {d f}} \delta (\mathsf {f} - \mathsf {t}) \right] \frac {\mathsf {d t} ^ {\prime}}{\mathsf {d f}} \mathsf {d f} \\ = 4 \mathrm {m} \int \frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d f}} \left[ \frac {\mathrm {u} ^ {0}}{\mathrm {R}} \frac {\mathrm {d t} ^ {\prime}}{\mathrm {d f}} \right] \delta (\mathrm {f} - \mathrm {t}) \mathrm {d f} \\ = 4 m \left[ \frac {\mathrm {d} t ^ {\prime}}{\mathrm {d} f} \frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d} t ^ {\prime}} \left(\frac {u ^ {0}}{R} \frac {\mathrm {d} t ^ {\prime}}{\mathrm {d} f}\right) \right] _ {f = t} \\ = 4 \mathrm {m} \left[ \frac {1}{1 - \epsilon \mathrm {v}} \frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d t} ^ {\prime}} \frac {\mathrm {u} ^ {0}}{(1 - \epsilon \mathrm {v}) \mathrm {R}} \right] _ {\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime} = \mathrm {t} - \mathrm {R} \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime}\right)}. \tag {12} \\ \end{array}
$$
Since $\mathrm{du}^0/\mathrm{dt}' \sim \mathfrak{m}$ , to linear order in $\mathfrak{m}$ we need not differentiate $\mathbf{u}^0$ and $\mathbf{v}$ in Equation (12). Thus
$$
\overline {{\mathrm {h}}} _ {0 0, 0} = 4 \mathrm {m} \left[ \frac {\epsilon \mathrm {v u} ^ {0}}{(1 - \epsilon \mathrm {v}) ^ {2} \mathrm {R} ^ {2}} \right] _ {\text {r e t .}} \tag {13}
$$
where ret. means evaluated at the retarded time $t' = t - R(t')$ . But
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbf {v} \left(t ^ {\prime}\right) = \mathbf {v} (t) + (\mathrm {d v} / \mathrm {d t}) \left(t ^ {\prime} - t\right) + \dots \\ = \mathbf {v} (t) + \mathcal {O} (m) \\ \end{array}
$$
and similarly for $\mathbf{u}^0 = (1 - \mathbf{v}^2)^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ and
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbf {R} \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime}\right) = \epsilon \left[ \mathrm {x} - \mathrm {z} \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime}\right) \right] \\ = \epsilon [ \mathbf {x} - \mathbf {z} (t) - \mathbf {v} (t) (t ^ {\prime} - t) ] + \mathcal {O} (m) \\ = \epsilon [ x - z (t) + v (t) R \left(t ^ {\prime}\right) ], \\ \end{array}
$$
thus
$$
R (t ^ {\prime}) (1 - \epsilon v) = \epsilon [ x - z (t) ].
$$
Since $\overline{\mathbf{h}}_{00,0}$ is being evaluated at the position of the other particle, we have
$$
\overline {{\mathrm {h}}} _ {0 0, 0} = 4 \mathrm {m} \frac {\epsilon \mathrm {v u} ^ {0}}{\left(z _ {1} - z _ {2}\right) ^ {2}} \tag {14}
$$
where the quantities are evaluated at the instantaneous time $t$ . Similarly
$$
\overline {{\mathrm {h}}} _ {0 \mathbf {x}, 0} = \frac {- \mathrm {u} ^ {\mathbf {x}}}{\mathrm {u} ^ {0}} \overline {{\mathrm {h}}} _ {0 0, 0} \tag {15}
$$
$$
\overline {{\mathrm {h}}} _ {\mathbf {x x}, 0} = \left(\frac {\mathrm {u} ^ {\mathbf {x}}}{\mathrm {u} ^ {0}}\right) ^ {2} \overline {{\mathrm {h}}} _ {0 0, 0}. \tag {16}
$$
The spatial derivatives can be evaluated by noting that $\mathbf{x}$ occurs only in $\mathbb{R}$ in Equation (7). Thus
$$
\begin{array}{l} \bar {h} _ {0 0, x} = \epsilon \frac {\partial}{\partial R} 4 m \int \frac {u ^ {0} \delta \left(t ^ {\prime} + R - t\right) d t ^ {\prime}}{R} \\ = 4 \mathrm {m} \epsilon \left[ - \int \frac {\mathrm {u} ^ {0} \delta \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime} + \mathrm {R} - \mathrm {t}\right) \mathrm {d t} ^ {\prime}}{\mathrm {R} ^ {2}} + \int \frac {\mathrm {u} ^ {0} \delta^ {\prime} \left(\mathrm {t} ^ {\prime} + \mathrm {R} - \mathrm {t}\right) \mathrm {d t} ^ {\prime}}{\mathrm {R}} \right] \\ = 4 \mathrm {m} \epsilon \left[ - \int \frac {\mathrm {u} ^ {0}}{\mathrm {R} ^ {2}} \delta (\mathrm {f} - \mathrm {t}) \frac {\mathrm {d t} ^ {\prime}}{\mathrm {d f}} \mathrm {d f} + \int \frac {\mathrm {u} ^ {0}}{\mathrm {R}} \frac {\mathrm {d t} ^ {\prime}}{\mathrm {d f}} \frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d f}} \delta (\mathrm {f} - \mathrm {t}) \mathrm {d f} \right] \\ = - 4 \mathrm {m} \epsilon \left[ \frac {\mathrm {u} ^ {0}}{(1 - \epsilon \mathrm {v}) \mathrm {R} ^ {2}} + \frac {1}{(1 - \epsilon \mathrm {v})} \frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d t} ^ {\prime}} \left\{\frac {\mathrm {u} ^ {0}}{(1 - \epsilon \mathrm {v}) \mathrm {R}} \right\} \right] _ {\text {r e t .}} \\ = - 4 \mathrm {m} \epsilon \left[ \frac {\mathrm {u} ^ {0}}{(1 - \epsilon \mathrm {v}) \mathrm {R} ^ {2}} + \frac {\mathrm {u} ^ {0} \epsilon \mathrm {v}}{(1 - \epsilon \mathrm {v}) ^ {2} \mathrm {R} ^ {2}} \right] _ {\text {r e t .}} \\ = - \frac {4 \mathrm {m} \epsilon \mathrm {u} ^ {0}}{\left(z _ {1} - z _ {2}\right) ^ {2}}. \tag {17} \\ \end{array}
$$
Similarly
$$
\overline {{h}} _ {0 x, x} = - \frac {u ^ {x}}{u ^ {0}} \bar {h} _ {0 0, x} \tag {18}
$$
$$
\overline {{\mathrm {h}}} _ {\mathbf {x x}, \mathbf {x}} = \left(\frac {\mathrm {u} ^ {\mathbf {x}}}{\mathrm {u} ^ {0}}\right) ^ {2} \overline {{\mathrm {h}}} _ {0 0, \mathbf {x}}. \tag {19}
$$
Note that the gauge condition $\mathbf{h}_{\mu, \alpha}^{\alpha} = 0$ is satisfied by Equations (14)-(19). Write out the equations of motion, Equations (10) and (11), for particle 1 letting $\mathbf{u}^{0} = \gamma$ , $\mathbf{u}^{\mathbf{x}} = \gamma \mathbf{v}$ , $\mathbf{g} = \mathbf{m} / (\mathbf{z}_1 - \mathbf{z}_2)^2$ . Take particle 1 to be the positive mass particle at $z_1 > z_2$ , so that $\epsilon = +1$ and the source mass in Equations (14)-(19) is negative.
$$
\mathrm {d} \gamma_ {1} / \mathrm {d} \tau = - \mathbf {g} \gamma_ {2} v _ {2} \left(1 + v _ {2} ^ {2}\right) + 2 \mathbf {g} \gamma_ {2} \left(1 + v _ {2} ^ {2}\right) \gamma_ {1} ^ {2} v _ {1} - 4 \mathbf {g} \gamma_ {2} v _ {2} \gamma_ {1} ^ {2} v _ {1} ^ {2} \tag {20}
$$
$$
d \left(\gamma_ {1} v _ {1}\right) / d \tau = g \gamma_ {2} \left(1 + v _ {2} ^ {2}\right) - 4 g \gamma_ {2} v _ {2} ^ {2} \gamma_ {1} ^ {2} + 2 g \gamma_ {2} v _ {2} \left(1 + v _ {2} ^ {2}\right) \gamma_ {1} ^ {2} v _ {1}. \tag {21}
$$
Now the equations for $\mathrm{d}\gamma_{2} / \mathrm{d}\tau_{2}$ and $\mathrm{d}(\gamma_2\mathbf{v}_2) / \mathrm{d}\tau_2$ have the same right-hand sides as Equations (20)-(21), with $\mathbf{v}_2\leftrightarrow \mathbf{v}_1$ . ( $\varepsilon \mathfrak{m}$ keeps the same sign.) Since $\mathbf{v}_1 = \mathbf{v}_2$ at $t = 0$ , then $\mathbf{v}_1 = \mathbf{v}_2$ for all t. Thus $z_{1} - z_{2}$ is constant for all t, i.e. $z_{1} - z_{2} = \ell$ . We can drop the subscripts 1 and 2 in Equations (20) and (21) and get, after some simplification
$$
\mathrm {d} \gamma / \mathrm {d} \tau = \mathrm {g v} / \gamma \tag {22}
$$
$$
\mathrm {d} (\gamma \mathrm {v}) / \mathrm {d} \tau - \mathrm {g} / \gamma \tag {23}
$$
These equations are not independent, because of the relation $\gamma = (1 - v^2)^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ . We find then that the differential equation for the motion is
$$
\mathrm {d} \mathbf {v} / \mathrm {d} \tau = \mathbf {g} \left(1 - \mathbf {v} ^ {2}\right) ^ {2}, \tag {24}
$$
and hence
$$
2 \mathrm {g} \tau = \mathrm {v} / \left(1 - \mathrm {v} ^ {2}\right) + \tanh  ^ {- 1} \mathrm {v} \tag {25}
$$
where $\tau = 0$ when $\mathbf{v} = 0$ . Thus
$$
\frac {d z}{d v} = \frac {d z}{d t} \frac {d t}{d r} \frac {d r}{d v} = \frac {v}{g} (1 - v ^ {2}) ^ {- 5 / 2}
$$
implies
$$
z _ {2} = \frac {1}{3 \mathrm {g}} \left[ (1 - v ^ {2}) ^ {- \frac {3}{2}} - 1 \right] \tag {26}
$$
$$
\mathbf {z} _ {1} = \mathbf {z} _ {2} + \ell
$$
and similarly
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} t}{\mathrm {d} v} = \frac {\mathrm {d} t}{\mathrm {d} r} \frac {\mathrm {d} r}{\mathrm {d} v} = \frac {1}{g} (1 - v ^ {2}) ^ {- 5 / 2} \tag {27}
$$
implies
$$
t = \frac {v}{g} \left[ \left(1 - v ^ {2}\right) ^ {- \frac {1}{2}} + \frac {v ^ {2}}{3} \left(1 - v ^ {2}\right) ^ {- \frac {3}{2}} \right]. \tag {28}
$$
Equations (26) and (28) are parametric equations for the trajectories with $\mathbf{v}$ as parameter. Note that while the coordinate separation of the particles remains constant, the proper separation as measured by an observer comoving with one of the particles increases, approximately as $\gamma \ell$ . Proof: Suppose particle 1 has velocity $\mathbf{v}$ at some instant $t_1$ at position $z_1(t_1)$ . The Lorentz transformation to the locally comoving inertial frame is
$$
\mathbf {x} ^ {\prime} = \gamma [ \mathbf {x} - \mathbf {z} _ {1} (t _ {1}) - \mathbf {v} (t - t _ {1}) ]
$$
$$
\mathbf {t} ^ {\prime} = \gamma \left[ \mathbf {t} - \mathbf {t _ {1}} \right. - \mathbf {v} \left\{\mathbf {x} - \mathbf {z _ {1}} (\mathbf {t _ {1}}) \right\} \mathrm {.}
$$
Thus the trajectory of particle 2 in these coordinates is $z_2'(t_2')$ where
$$
z _ {2} ^ {\prime} = \gamma \left[ z _ {2} (t _ {2}) - z _ {1} (t _ {1}) - v (t _ {2} - t _ {1}) \right]
$$
$$
\mathsf {t} _ {2} ^ {\prime} = \gamma \left[ \mathsf {t} _ {2} - \mathsf {t} _ {1} \right. - \mathrm {v} \{\mathsf {z} _ {2} (\mathsf {t} _ {2}) - \mathsf {z} _ {1} (\mathsf {t} _ {1}) \} ].
$$
The distance to particle 2 is $-z_2'(t_2' = 0)$ . Let
$$
\beta = \mathbf {z} _ {2} (\mathfrak {t} _ {2}) - \mathbf {z} _ {1} (\mathfrak {t} _ {1}).
$$
Now $t_2' = 0$ implies
$$
\mathsf {t} _ {2} = \mathsf {t} _ {1} + \mathsf {v} \beta
$$
so
$$
\begin{array}{l} \beta = z _ {2} \left(t _ {1} + v \beta\right) - z _ {1} \left(t _ {1}\right) \\ \approx \left. z _ {2} (t _ {1}) + v \beta \mathrm {d} z _ {2} / \mathrm {d} t \right| _ {t = t _ {1}} + \frac {v ^ {2} \beta^ {2}}{z} \left. \frac {\mathrm {d} ^ {2} z _ {2}}{\mathrm {d} t ^ {2}} \right| _ {t = t _ {1}} - z _ {1} (t _ {1}) \\ = - \ell + v ^ {2} \beta + \mathcal {O} (m / L). \\ \end{array}
$$
Solve for $\beta$ :
$$
\beta = \frac {- \ell}{(1 - v ^ {2})}.
$$
Thus
$$
- \mathbf {z} _ {2} ^ {\prime} (\mathbf {t} _ {2} ^ {\prime} = 0) = - \gamma (\beta - \mathbf {v} ^ {2} \beta) \approx \gamma \ell .
$$
