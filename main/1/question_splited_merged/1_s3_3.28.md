Problem 3.28. Show that $\delta_{\lambda \kappa}^{\mu \nu} = -\frac{1}{2}\varepsilon^{\mu \nu \rho \sigma}\varepsilon_{\lambda \kappa \rho \sigma},$ and generalize to $\delta_{\lambda \dots \kappa}^{\mu \dots \nu}$ of other ranks.

## Solution

Solution 3.28. The indices $\mu, \nu, \lambda, \kappa$ must take on different values from $\rho, \sigma$ . For given $\rho, \sigma$ , we can have $\mu$ equal to $\kappa$ (with $\nu$ then equal to $\lambda$ ) or $\mu$ equal to $\lambda$ (with $\nu$ then equal to $\kappa$ ). All other possibilities vanish because of the antisymmetry of $\varepsilon^{\mu \nu \rho \sigma}$ . Furthermore, because of that antisymmetry, the $\mu = \kappa$ , $\nu = \lambda$ case has a minus sign. Thus
$$
\varepsilon^ {\mu \nu \rho \sigma} _ {\varepsilon_ {\lambda \kappa \rho \sigma}} = \mathsf {C} (\delta_ {\lambda} ^ {\mu} \delta_ {\kappa} ^ {\nu} - \delta_ {\kappa} ^ {\mu} \delta_ {\lambda} ^ {\nu}) \equiv \mathsf {C} \delta_ {\lambda \kappa} ^ {\mu \nu}
$$
for some C. Summing on $\mu, \lambda$ and $\nu$ , k and using the results of Problem 3.22 gives $C = -2$ . Thus
$$
\delta_ {\lambda \kappa} ^ {\mu \nu} = - \frac {1}{2} \varepsilon^ {\mu \nu \rho \sigma} \varepsilon_ {\lambda \kappa \rho \sigma}.
$$
In general
$$
\begin{array}{l} \varepsilon^ {\mu \nu \lambda \tau} _ {\iota \kappa \rho \sigma} = - \delta_ {\iota \kappa \rho \sigma} ^ {\mu \nu \lambda \tau} \\ \varepsilon^ {\mu \nu \lambda \tau} \varepsilon_ {\iota \kappa \rho \tau} = - \delta_ {\iota \kappa \rho} ^ {\mu \nu \lambda}. \\ \end{array}
$$
