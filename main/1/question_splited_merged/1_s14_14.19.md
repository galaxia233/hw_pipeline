Problem 14.19. Show that $\mathrm{H} = \frac{1}{2}\mathrm{g}^{\mu \nu}(\pi_{\mu} - \mathrm{eA}_{\mu})(\pi_{\nu} - \mathrm{eA}_{\nu})$ is a Hamiltonian giving the equations of motion of a test particle of charge e. Here $\pi_{\mu}$ is the canonical momentum. (The canonical momentum $\pi^{\mu}$ is not equal to $\mathfrak{p}^{\mu}$ , the particles 4-momentum unless the 4-potential $\mathbf{A}^{\mu}$ is zero.)

## Solution

Solution 14.19. First note that $\mathbf{H}$ has been normalized to have dimensions of $(\text{mass})^2$ ; this means that Hamilton's equations are
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} ^ {\mu} / \mathrm {d} \lambda = \partial \mathrm {H} / \partial \pi_ {\mu} (1) \\ \partial \pi_ {\mu} / \mathrm {d} \lambda = - \partial \mathrm {H} / \partial \mathbf {x} ^ {\mu} (2) \\ \end{array}
$$
where $\lambda$ is an affine parameter ( $= \tau / m$ for a particle of mass $m$ where $\tau$ is proper time). If $H$ were divided by $m$ , one would replace $d / d\lambda$ by $d / d\tau$ in Equations (1) and (2); $d / d\lambda$ has the advantage of being valid for massless particles as well.
Since the particle's 4-momentum is $\mathfrak{p}^{\mu} = \mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}^{\mu} / \mathrm{d}\lambda$ , and since $\mathbf{g}^{\mu \nu}$ and $\mathbf{A}_{\mu}$ are functions of $\mathbf{x}^{\alpha}$ not $\pi_{\alpha}$ , Equation (1) gives
$$
\mathrm {p} ^ {\mu} = \mathrm {g} ^ {\mu \nu} \left(\pi_ {\nu} - \mathrm {e A} _ {\nu}\right). \tag {3}
$$
Equation (2) gives
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} \pi_ {\alpha}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} = - \frac {1}{2} \mathbf {g} _ {, \alpha} ^ {\mu \nu} (\pi_ {\mu} - \mathsf {e A} _ {\mu}) (\pi_ {\nu} - \mathsf {e A} _ {\nu}) + \mathbf {g} ^ {\mu \nu} \mathsf {e A} _ {\mu , \alpha} (\pi_ {\nu} - \mathsf {e A} _ {\nu}).
$$
But from Equation (3) we get
$$
\begin{array}{l} \frac {\mathrm {d} \pi_ {a}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} = \frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} \left(\mathrm {g} _ {a \mu} \mathrm {p} ^ {\mu} + \mathrm {e A} _ {a}\right) \\ = \mathrm {g} _ {\alpha \mu} \frac {\mathrm {d p} ^ {\mu}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} + \mathrm {g} _ {\alpha \mu , \beta} \mathrm {p} ^ {\beta} \mathrm {p} ^ {\mu} + \mathrm {e A} _ {\alpha , \beta} \mathrm {p} ^ {\beta} \\ \end{array}
$$
(where we have used $\mathrm{d} / \mathrm{d}\lambda = \mathrm{p}^{\beta}\partial /\partial \mathbf{x}^{\beta})$ . Multiplying by $\mathbf{g}^{\alpha \gamma}$ , we find
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d p} ^ {\gamma}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} + \mathbf {g} ^ {\alpha \gamma} \mathbf {g} _ {\alpha \mu , \beta} \mathbf {p} ^ {\beta} \mathbf {p} ^ {\mu} + \frac {1}{2} \mathbf {g} ^ {\alpha \gamma} \mathbf {g} _ {, \alpha} ^ {\mu \nu} \mathbf {p} _ {\mu} \mathbf {p} _ {\nu} =. \mathbf {g} ^ {\alpha \gamma} \mathbf {e} (\mathbf {A} _ {\mu , \alpha} - \mathbf {A} _ {\alpha , \mu}) \mathbf {p} ^ {\mu}.
$$
Now rearrange the derivatives of $\mathfrak{g}_{\alpha \beta}$ to form a Christoffel symbol.
Since
$$
0 = (\mathbf {g} ^ {\mu \beta} \mathbf {g} _ {\beta \gamma}) _ {, a} = \mathbf {g} ^ {\mu \beta} _ {, a} \mathbf {g} _ {\beta \gamma} + \mathbf {g} ^ {\mu \beta} \mathbf {g} _ {\beta \gamma , a}
$$
we have on multiplying by $\mathbf{g}^{\gamma \nu}$
$$
\mathbf {g} _ {, a} ^ {\mu \nu} = - \mathbf {g} ^ {\gamma \nu} \mathbf {g} ^ {\mu \beta} \mathbf {g} _ {\beta \gamma , a}.
$$
Thus
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d p} ^ {\gamma}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} + \mathbf {g} ^ {\alpha \gamma} \Bigl (\mathbf {g} _ {\alpha \mu , \beta} - \frac {1}{2} \mathbf {\Gamma} \mathbf {g} _ {\beta \mu , a} \Bigr) \mathbf {p} ^ {\beta} \mathbf {p} ^ {\mu} = \mathbf {g} ^ {\alpha \gamma} \mathbf {e F} _ {\alpha \mu} \mathbf {p} ^ {\mu} .
$$
Since
$$
\mathbf {g} _ {\alpha \mu , \beta} \mathrm {p} ^ {\beta} \mathrm {p} ^ {\mu} = \frac {1}{2} \left(\mathbf {g} _ {\alpha \mu , \beta} + \mathbf {g} _ {\alpha \beta , \mu}\right) \mathrm {p} ^ {\beta} \mathrm {p} ^ {\mu},
$$
we get, finally
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d p} ^ {\gamma}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} + \Gamma_ {\beta \mu} ^ {\gamma} \mathsf {p} ^ {\beta} \mathsf {p} ^ {\mu} = \mathsf {e F} _ {\mu} ^ {\gamma} \mathsf {p} ^ {\mu}
$$
which is the equation of motion.
