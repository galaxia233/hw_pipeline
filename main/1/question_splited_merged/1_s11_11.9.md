Problem 11.9. Calculate the period of precession of the Earth's axis due to the coupling of tidal forces from the sun and moon with the quadrupole moment of the (slightly nonspherical) Earth.

## Solution

Solution 11.9. We shall use two coordinate systems: the XYZ coordinates are the spatial coordinates of a local Lorentz frame with spatial origin at the center of mass of the Earth. The ecliptic plane is the XY plane, and we shall assume that the Sun and the Moon move in circular orbits in this plane as seen from the Earth. The xyz coordinates are the spatial coordinates of a similar Lorentz frame, but with the $z$ -axis parallel to the Earth's angular momentum $\underline{\mathbf{J}}$ . Choose the $x$ -axis parallel to the $X$ -axis. The relation between basis vectors for the two coordinate systems is
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathfrak {E} _ {\mathbf {x}} = \mathfrak {E} _ {\mathbf {X}} \\ \underline {{\mathbf {e}}} _ {\mathbf {Y}} = \cos \psi \underline {{\mathbf {e}}} _ {\mathbf {Y}} - \sin \psi \underline {{\mathbf {e}}} _ {\mathbf {Z}} \\ \underline {{\mathbf {e}}} _ {\mathbf {z}} = \sin \psi \underline {{\mathbf {e}}} _ {\mathbf {Y}} + \cos \psi \underline {{\mathbf {e}}} _ {\mathbf {Z}}. \tag {1} \\ \end{array}
$$
![](images/0480b8c388be7b86069d1daaff3dd4074426821c3c9f50cf2f99b7799b964b0c.jpg)
Here $\psi \approx 23\frac{1}{2}^0$ is the angle between $\underline{\mathbf{J}}$ and the $Z$ -axis, which remains constant if we ignore the small nutation. As time goes on, $\underline{\mathbf{J}}$ precesses about the $Z$ -axis with a period $\mathbf{T}$ which we wish to find.
Let $\underline{\mathbf{e}}_{\mathrm{s}}$ be a unit vector pointing to the Sun. If the sun has polar coordinates $(\mathbf{r}_{\mathrm{s}},\theta ,\phi)$ with respect to the xyz coordinates and $(\mathbf{r_s},\frac{\pi}{2},\phi_{\mathrm{s}})$ with respect to the XYZ coordinates, then
$$
\begin{array}{l} \cos \theta = \underline {{\mathbf {e}}} _ {\mathrm {z}} \cdot \underline {{\mathbf {e}}} _ {\mathrm {s}} \\ = (\sin \psi \underline {{\mathrm {e}}} _ {\mathbf {Y}} + \cos \psi \underline {{\mathrm {e}}} _ {\mathbf {Z}}) \cdot (\cos \phi_ {\mathrm {s}} \underline {{\mathrm {e}}} _ {\mathbf {X}} + \sin \phi_ {\mathrm {s}} \underline {{\mathrm {e}}} _ {\mathbf {Y}}) \\ = \sin \psi \sin \phi_ {s} \\ \end{array}
$$
and similarly,
$$
\sin \theta \sin \phi = \underline {{e}} _ {\mathbf {y}} \cdot \underline {{e}} _ {\mathbf {s}} = \cos \psi \sin \phi_ {\mathbf {s}}
$$
$$
\sin \theta \cos \phi = \underline {{\mathbf {e}}} _ {\mathbf {x}} \cdot \underline {{\mathbf {e}}} _ {\mathbf {s}} = \cos \phi_ {\mathbf {s}}.
$$
From Problem 11.8, the equation of motion of the spin vector $\underline{\mathbf{J}}$ of the Earth in an external gravitational field is
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} \underline {{J}}}{\mathrm {d} t} = \underline {{N}}
$$
where the torque is
$$
\mathrm {N} _ {\mathrm {i}} = - \varepsilon_ {\mathrm {i j k}} \mathrm {t} _ {\mathrm {j m}} \mathrm {R} _ {\mathrm {k 0 m 0}}.
$$
Here the "reduced quadrupole moment tensor" is
$$
\mathbf {t} _ {\mathrm {j m}} = \int \rho \left(\mathbf {x} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \mathbf {x} ^ {\mathrm {m}} - \frac {1}{3} \mathbf {\nabla r} ^ {2} \delta^ {\mathrm {j m}}\right) \mathbf {d} ^ {3} \mathbf {x} .
$$
Take the Earth to be spheroidal, with moment of inertia C about the rotation axis and A about an equatorial axis. Then in xyz coordinates
$$
C = \int \rho (x ^ {2} + y ^ {2}) d ^ {3} x
$$
$$
A = \int \rho (x ^ {2} + z ^ {2}) d ^ {3} x = \int \rho (y ^ {2} + z ^ {2}) d ^ {3} x.
$$
The nonzero components of $t_{\mathrm{jm}}$ are
$$
t _ {z z} = - \frac {2}{3} (C - A)
$$
$$
t _ {x x} = t _ {y y} = \frac {1}{2} (C - A)
$$
so that
$$
N _ {x} = - (C - A) R _ {z 0 y 0}
$$
$$
N _ {y} = (C - A) R _ {z 0 x 0}
$$
$$
\mathrm {N} _ {\mathrm {z}} = 0.
$$
In the weak-field, slow-motion limit,
$$
R _ {z 0 i 0} = \frac {\partial^ {2} \Phi}{\partial z \partial x ^ {i}}
$$
where $\Phi$ is the Newtonian gravitational potential:
$$
\Phi = \Phi_ {\mathrm {S U N}} + \Phi_ {\mathrm {M O O N}}.
$$
Since
$$
\Phi_ {\mathrm {S U N}} (\mathrm {x}, \mathrm {y}, z) = \frac {\mathrm {M} _ {\mathrm {s}}}{[ (\mathrm {x} - \mathrm {x} _ {\mathrm {s}}) ^ {2} + (\mathrm {y} - \mathrm {y} _ {\mathrm {s}}) ^ {2} + (\mathrm {z} - z _ {s} ) ^ {2} ] ^ { \frac {1 } {2}}}
$$
we find
$$
\begin{array}{l} \left. \frac {\partial^ {2} \Phi}{\partial z \partial x} \right| _ {0} = - \frac {3 M _ {s} \sin \theta \cos \theta \cos \phi}{r _ {s} ^ {3}} \\ \left. \frac {\partial^ {2} \Phi}{\partial z \partial y} \right| _ {0} = - \frac {3 M _ {s} \sin \theta \cos \theta \sin \phi}{r _ {s} ^ {3}}. \\ \end{array}
$$
Thus $\underline{\mathbf{N}}_{\mathrm{SUN}}$ has $(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y},\mathbf{z})$ components
$$
\begin{array}{l} \underset {\sim} {N} _ {\mathrm {S U N}} = 3 \mathrm {a} (\mathrm {C} - \mathrm {A}) \sin \theta \cos \theta (\sin \phi , - \cos \phi , 0) \\ = 3 a (C - A) \sin \psi \sin \phi_ {s} (\cos \psi \sin \phi_ {s}, - \cos \phi_ {s}, 0) \\ \end{array}
$$
where $a \equiv M_{s} / r_{s}^{3}$ . Since the Sun's angular velocity about the Earth is much greater than the precession angular velocity we wish to find, we can average $N_{\text{SUN}}$ over the Sun's orbit. The term $\sin^2 \phi_{\text{s}}$ gives $\frac{1}{2}$ , while $\sin \phi_{\text{s}} \cos \phi_{\text{s}}$ averages to zero. Thus the only nonzero component of
$\underline{\mathbf{N}}_{\mathrm{SUN}}$ is
$$
\mathrm {N} _ { \mathbf {x } } ^ { \text {S U N } } = \mathrm {N} _ { \mathbf {x } } ^ { \text {S U N } } = \frac { 3 } { 2 } \mathrm {a } ( \mathrm {C } - \mathrm {A } ) \sin \psi \cos \psi .
$$
A similar expression holds for the average torque exerted by the Moon, with a replaced by $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{M}_{\mathbf{M}} / \mathbf{r}_{\mathbf{M}}^{3}$ . Thus, at $t = 0$ , there is a torque in the X direction of magnitude
$$
\mathbf {N} = \frac {3}{2} (a + b) (C - A) \sin \psi \cos \psi .
$$
In a small time dt therefore $\underline{\mathbf{J}}$ changes by an amount $\mathrm{d}\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{N}\mathrm{dt}$ perpendicular to itself. Thus $\underline{\mathbf{J}}$ rotates about the $Z$ axis by an amount
$$
\mathrm {d} \chi = \frac {\mathrm {d J}}{\mathrm {J} \sin \psi} = \frac {\mathrm {N d t}}{\mathrm {J} \sin \psi}.
$$
The precession period is the time for $\chi$ to change by $2\pi$ :
$$
\mathrm {T} = \frac {2 \pi \mathrm {J} \sin \psi}{\mathrm {N}}.
$$
Put $\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{C}\omega$ , where $\omega$ is the angular velocity of rotation of the Earth.
Then
$$
T = \frac {4 \pi}{3} \frac {C}{C - A} \frac {1}{\cos \psi} \frac {\omega}{a + b}.
$$
Putting
$$
C / (C - A) = 3 0 5. 3
$$
$$
\psi = 2 3. 4 5 ^ {0}
$$
$$
\omega = 7. 2 9 2 \times 1 0 ^ { - 5 } \sec ^ { - 1 }
$$
$$
\mathrm {M} _ { \mathrm {s } } = 1. 9 8 9 \times 1 0 ^ { 3 3 } \mathrm {g }
$$
$$
r _ { s } = 1. 4 9 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 3 } c m
$$
$$
M _ { M } = 7. 3 4 9 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 5 } g
$$
$$
\mathrm {r} _ { \mathrm {M } } = 3. 8 4 4 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 0 } \mathrm {c m }
$$
we find
$$
\mathrm {T} = 2 5 , 6 0 0 \text {y e a r s }.
$$
This agrees with observation to better than $1\%$ ; some error in the calculation arises because we treated the orbits as circular and because the Moon's orbit is not exactly in the ecliptic.
