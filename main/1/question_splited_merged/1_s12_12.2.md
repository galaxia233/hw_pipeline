Problem 12.2. Estimate the height of spring tides and neap tides.

## Solution

Solution 12.2. Spring tides occur when the sun and the moon are in the same line as the earth (new moon or full moon); neap tides occur when the sun and the moon are at right angles. Let the height of the tide be $h$ . For a rough estimate, take the sun and the moon to be in the equatorial plane. An ocean element on the equator at high tide is in equilibrium between the earth's gravitational acceleration $g(r) = -M_{\oplus} / r^2$ at a height $r = r_{\oplus} + h$ ( $M_{\oplus} =$ mass of earth, $r_{\oplus} =$ radius of earth) and the tidal accelerations of the sun and moon relative to the center of the earth:
$$
0 = \mathrm {g} \left(\mathrm {r} _ {\oplus} + \mathrm {h}\right) + \mathrm {r} _ {\oplus} \mathrm {R} _ {2 0 2 0} ^ {\text {s u n}} + \mathrm {r} _ {\oplus} \mathrm {R} _ {2 0 2 0} ^ {\text {m o o n}}. \tag {1}
$$
An element $90^{\circ}$ away in longitude experiences low tide:
$$
0 = \mathrm {g} \left(\mathrm {r} _ {\oplus} - \mathrm {h}\right) + \mathrm {r} _ {\oplus} \mathrm {R} _ {1 0 1 0} ^ {\text {s u n}} + \mathrm {r} _ {\oplus} \mathrm {R} _ {1 0 1 0} ^ {\text {m o o n}}. \tag {2}
$$

The difference of these two equations gives us
$$
0 = 2 \mathrm {h} \mathrm {g} ^ {\prime} (\mathrm {r} _ {\oplus}) + \mathrm {r} _ {\oplus} \left(\mathrm {R} _ {2 0 2 0} ^ {\text {s u n}} - \mathrm {R} _ {1 0 1 0} ^ {\text {s u n}} + \mathrm {R} _ {2 0 2 0} ^ {\text {m o o n}} - \mathrm {R} _ {1 0 1 0} ^ {\text {m o o n}}\right). \tag {3}
$$
Note that if we had put in centrifugal forces, they would have cancelled in Equation (3). Compute the Riemann tensor in the Newtonian limit (see Problem 12.12):
$$
\mathrm {R} _ {1 0 1 0} ^ {\mathrm {s u n}} = \left. \frac {\partial^ {2} \Phi^ {\mathrm {s u n}}}{\partial^ {2} x ^ {2}} \right| _ {x = y \approx 0} = - \frac {\partial^ {2}}{\partial x ^ {2}} \left[ \frac {\mathrm {M} _ {\odot}}{\left[ (\mathrm {x} - \mathrm {x} _ {\mathrm {s u n}}) ^ {2} + (\mathrm {y} - \mathrm {y} _ {\mathrm {s u n}}) ^ {2} + (\mathrm {z} - \mathrm {z} _ {\mathrm {s u n}}) ^ {2} \right] ^ {\frac {1}{2}}} \right]
$$
and similarly for the other components. This gives
$$
R _ {1 0 1 0} ^ {\text {s u n}} \left(x _ {\text {s u n}} = z _ {\text {s u n}} = 0, y _ {\text {s u n}} = R\right) = M _ {O} / R ^ {3}
$$
$$
\mathrm {R} _ {1 0 1 0} ^ {\text {s u n}} \left(\mathbf {y} _ {\text {s u n}} = \mathbf {z} _ {\text {s u n}} = 0, \mathbf {x} _ {\text {s u n}} = \mathrm {R}\right) = - 2 \mathrm {M} _ {\odot} / \mathrm {R} ^ {3}
$$
$$
R _ {2 0 2 0} ^ {\text {s u n}} \left(x _ {\text {s u n}} = z _ {\text {s u n}} = 0, y _ {\text {s u n}} = R\right) = - 2 M _ {\odot} / R ^ {3}
$$
$$
R _ {2 0 2 0} ^ {\text {s u n}} \left(y _ {\text {s u n}} = z _ {\text {s u n}} = 0, x _ {\text {s u n}} = R\right) = M _ {\Theta} / R ^ {3}
$$
and similar expressions for the moon. Spring tides occur when the sun and the moon are in a line, say both on the y-axis. Equation (3) then gives
$$
h _ {\text {s p r i n g}} = \frac {3}{4} \left(\frac {M _ {\odot}}{R _ {\odot} ^ {3}} + \frac {M _ {\text {m o o n}}}{R _ {\text {m o o n}} ^ {3}}\right) \frac {r _ {\oplus} ^ {4}}{M _ {\oplus}} = 3 9 \mathrm {c m}.
$$
Neap tides occur when e.g. the moon is on the y-axis and the sun on the x-axis. This gives
$$
h _ {n e a p} = \frac {3}{4} \left(\frac {M _ {m o o n}}{R _ {m o o n} ^ {3}} - \frac {M _ {\odot}}{R _ {\odot} ^ {3}}\right) \frac {r _ {\oplus} ^ {4}}{M _ {\oplus}} = 1 5 c m.
$$
The actual tides are, of course, considerably larger than this in many places because of hydrodynamical effects (like "sloshing" of water in shallow seas and against irregular coastlines).
