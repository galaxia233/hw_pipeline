Problem 1.13. Show that the curve
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbf {x} = \int \mathbf {r} \cos \theta \cos \phi d \lambda \\ \mathbf {y} = \int \mathbf {r} \cos \theta \sin \phi d \lambda \\ z = \int r \sin \theta d \lambda \\ \mathrm {t} = \int r d \lambda , \\ \end{array}
$$
where $\mathbf{r}$ , $\theta$ and $\phi$ are arbitrary functions of $\lambda$ , is a null curve in special relativity. Under what conditions is it a null geodesic?

## Solution

Solution 1.13. Along the curve we have
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathrm {d} x ^ {2} + \mathrm {d} y ^ {2} + \mathrm {d} z ^ {2} - \mathrm {d} t ^ {2} = (\mathrm {r} ^ {2} \cos^ {2} \theta \cos^ {2} \phi + \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \cos^ {2} \theta \sin^ {2} \phi + \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \sin^ {2} \theta - \mathrm {r} ^ {2}) \mathrm {d} \lambda^ {2} \\ = r ^ {2} d \lambda^ {2} \left(\cos^ {2} \theta + \sin^ {2} \theta - 1\right) = 0, \\ \end{array}
$$
so the curve is null.
To be a geodesic (i.e. a straight line) we must have $\mathrm{dz} / \mathrm{dt} = \text{constant}$ , but $\mathrm{dz} / \mathrm{dt} = \sin \theta$ , so $\theta$ must be a constant. From the expression for $\mathrm{dy} / \mathrm{dt}$ or $\mathrm{dx} / \mathrm{dt}$ we see that $\phi$ must be constant. These are the requirements for a geodesic; there is no restriction on the function $r(\lambda)$ . If, however, $\lambda$ is to be an affine parameter (e.g. proper time) we must have $\mathrm{dt} / \mathrm{d}\lambda$ a constant, and hence $r$ a constant.
