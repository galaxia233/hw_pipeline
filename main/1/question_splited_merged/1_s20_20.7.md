Problem 20.7. Calculate the expected perihelion shift of the planet Mercury in terms of the semimajor axis of its orbit $\mathbf{a}$ , the eccentricity $\mathbf{e}$ , and the mass $\mathbf{M}$ of the sun.

## Solution

Solution 20.7. In the Schwarzschild metric, in curvature coordinates, the equation for noncircular orbits may be put into the form (see e.g. solution to Problem 15.9)
$$
u ^ {\prime \prime} + u = \frac {M}{\tilde {L} ^ {2}} + 3 M u ^ {2} \equiv a + b u ^ {2} \tag {1}
$$
where $\mathbf{u} \equiv 1 / \mathbf{r}$ , $\mathbf{u}'' \equiv \mathrm{d}^2\mathbf{u} / \mathrm{d}\phi^2$ , and $\tilde{\mathbf{L}} \equiv$ angular momentum per unit mass of the orbiting particle (= constant). The solution to the lowest order, the linear portion of Equation (1), is
$$
u _ {0} = a + A \cos \phi \tag {2}
$$
where $A$ is a constant. The second order solution to Equation (1), $u_1$ , then satisfies the equation
$$
\begin{array}{l} u _ {1} ^ {\prime \prime} + u _ {1} \approx b u _ {0} ^ {2} = b (a ^ {2} + 2 a A \cos \phi + A ^ {2} \cos^ {2} \phi) \\ = \mathrm {b a} ^ {2} + \frac {1}{2} \mathrm {b A} ^ {2} + 2 \mathrm {a b A} \cos \phi + \frac {1}{2} \mathrm {b A} ^ {2} \cos 2 \phi . \tag {3} \\ \end{array}
$$
The inhomogeneous solution to Equation (3) is
$$
u _ {1} = b \left(a ^ {2} + \frac {1}{2} A ^ {2}\right) + a b A \phi \sin \phi - (b A ^ {2} / 6) \cos 2 \phi \tag {4}
$$
and
$$
u = \frac {1}{r} \approx u _ {0} + u _ {1}. \tag {5}
$$
The second term in $\mathbf{u}_1$ causes the nonperiodicity; we may put it into more useful form by use of the identity
$$
\epsilon \phi \sin \phi = \cos (\phi - \epsilon \phi) - \cos \phi + \mathcal {O} \left(\epsilon^ {2}\right). \tag {6}
$$
Since ab is a small quantity according to Equation (1), Equation (6) may be used to put Equation (4) into the form
$$
u _ {1} \approx b \left(a ^ {2} + \frac {1}{2} A ^ {2}\right) + A \cos (\phi - a b \phi) - A \cos \phi - (b A ^ {2} / 6) \cos 2 \phi \tag {7}
$$
and the perihelion shift, per revolution, is
$$
\delta \phi = 2 \pi \mathbf {a b} = 6 \pi \mathrm {M} ^ {2} / \tilde {\mathrm {L}} ^ {2}. \tag {8}
$$
We may express $\tilde{\mathbf{L}}$ in terms of the semimajor axis $a$ and eccentricity $e$ of the orbit using standard classical mechanics
$$
\tilde {\mathbf {L}} ^ {2} = \operatorname {M a} \left(1 - e ^ {2}\right). \tag {9}
$$
Equation (8) then becomes
$$
\delta \phi = \frac {6 \pi \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {a} \left(1 - \mathrm {e} ^ {2}\right)}, \tag {10}
$$
which in the limit of small e agrees with the solution of Problem 15.7.
