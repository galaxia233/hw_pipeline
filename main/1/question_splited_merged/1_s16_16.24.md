Problem 16.24. Show that the integral for M given in Problem 16.23, in the case of a static spherical star made of perfect fluid, is
$$
\mathrm {M} = \int_ {0} ^ {\mathrm {R}} (\rho + 3 \mathrm {p}) \mathrm {e} ^ {\Phi + \lambda} 4 \pi \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \mathrm {d r}
$$
in curvature coordinates $(\mathbf{g}_{00} = \mathrm{e}^{2\Phi}, \mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{rr}} = \mathrm{e}^{2\lambda})$ . Show that this is the same as the expression derived from the equation of stellar structure,
$$
\mathbf {M} = \int_ {0} ^ {\mathbf {R}} \rho 4 \pi \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \mathrm {d r}.
$$

## Solution

Solution 16.24. In curvature coordinates, $\xi_{(t)}^{\nu} = \delta_{t}^{\nu}$ and $\mathrm{d}^3\Sigma_\mu = \delta_\mu^t |\mathbf{g}|^\frac{1}{2}\mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}\mathrm{d}\theta \mathrm{d}\phi$ , thus the integral is
$$
\begin{array}{l} I = - \int (2 T _ {\nu} ^ {\mu} - \delta_ {\nu} ^ {\mu} T) \xi_ {(t)} ^ {\nu} d ^ {3} \Sigma_ {\mu} \\ = - \int \left(2 T _ {t} ^ {t} - T\right) e ^ {\Phi + \lambda} r ^ {2} \sin \theta d \theta d \phi d r. \\ \end{array}
$$
But
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbf {T} _ {t} ^ {t} = (\rho + p) u ^ {t} u _ {t} + p \delta_ {t} ^ {t} = - \rho \\ T = - (\rho + p) + 4 p = 3 p - \rho . \\ \end{array}
$$
Since everything is independent of $\theta$ and $\phi$ , the integration over angles gives $4\pi$ and therefore
$$
I = \int_ {0} ^ {R} (\rho + 3 p) e ^ {\Phi + \lambda} 4 \pi r ^ {2} d r. \tag {1}
$$
We can transform this by integration by parts. Since
$$
\mathrm {m} (\mathrm {r}) = \int_ {0} ^ {\mathrm {r}} 4 \pi \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \rho \mathrm {d r} \tag {2}
$$
we have
$$
\int_ {0} ^ {\mathbf {R}} \rho e ^ {\Phi + \lambda} 4 \pi r ^ {2} d r = [ m e ^ {\Phi + \lambda} ] _ {0} ^ {\mathbf {R}} - \int_ {0} ^ {\mathbf {R}} m (e ^ {\Phi + \lambda}) ^ {\prime} d r \tag {3}
$$
where a prime denotes $\mathrm{d} / \mathrm{dr}$ . The stellar structure equations tell us $\mathfrak{p}'$ so we write
$$
\int_ {0} ^ {\mathrm {R}} 3 \mathrm {p e} ^ {\Phi + \lambda} 4 \pi \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \mathrm {d r} = [ \mathrm {p e} ^ {\Phi + \lambda} 4 \pi \mathrm {r} ^ {3} ] _ {0} ^ { \mathrm {R } } - \int_ {0} ^ { \mathrm {R } } 4 \pi \mathrm {r} ^ {3} \left(\mathrm {pe} ^ { \Phi + \lambda }\right)^{ \prime} \mathrm{d} \mathrm{r}. \tag {4}
$$
The first term on the right in Equation (3) is $\mathfrak{m}(\mathbb{R}) = \mathbb{M}$ , since $\mathfrak{m} = 0$ at $\mathbf{r} = 0$ and $\Phi = -\lambda$ at $\mathbf{r} = \mathbb{R}$ . The first term on the right in Equation (4) is zero since $\mathfrak{p} = 0$ at $\mathbf{r} = \mathbb{R}$ . Thus, $\mathbf{I} = \mathbf{M}$ provided the sum of the remaining terms in Equations (3) and (4) vanish. This sum is
$$
\begin{array}{l} \int_ {0} ^ { \mathrm {R } } \mathrm {m } \left(\mathrm {e } ^ { \Phi + \lambda }\right)^{ \prime} \mathrm{d} \mathrm{r} + \int_ {0} ^ { \mathrm {R } } 4 \pi \mathrm {r} ^ {3} \left(\mathrm {pe } ^ { \Phi + \lambda }\right)^{ \prime} \mathrm{d} \mathrm{r} \\ = \int_ {0} ^ { \mathrm {R } } \mathrm {dr } \mathrm {e } ^ { \Phi + \lambda } \left[ \mathrm {m } \left(\Phi^ { \prime} + \lambda^ { \prime} \right)+4 \pi \mathrm {r} ^ {3} \left\{\mathrm {p} ^ { \prime}+\mathrm {p } \left(\Phi^ { \prime} + \lambda^ { \prime} \right)\right\}\right]. \tag {5}\\ \end{array}
$$
Now
$$
e ^ {2 \lambda } = \left(1-2 m / r\right)^{-1}
$$
so we get
$$
\lambda^{\prime}=\frac{(4 \pi r^{3} \rho-m)}{r(r-2 m)}.
$$
If this result and the relations
$$
\Phi^{\prime}=\frac{\mathrm{m}+4 \pi \mathrm{r}^{3} \rho}{\mathrm{r}(\mathrm{r}-2 \mathrm{m})}
$$
$$
p^{\prime}=\frac{-(\rho+p)(m+4 \pi r^{3} p)}{r(r-2 m)}
$$
are substituted in the expression in Equation (5), all terms cancel and it reduces to zero as required.
