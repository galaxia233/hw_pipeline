Problem 13.14. In linearized theory one often uses the "trace reversed" form of the metric perturbations $\overline{\mathbf{h}}_{\alpha}\beta \equiv \mathbf{h}_{\alpha}\beta -\frac{1}{2}\eta_{\alpha \beta}\mathbf{h}_{\sigma}^{\sigma}$ . Show that there always exists a gauge transformation to the "Lorentz gauge", in which the divergence of $\overline{\mathbf{h}}_{\alpha}\beta$ vanishes. Is this gauge transformation unique?

## Solution

Solution 13.14. Under a gauge transformation (see Problem 13.12) the metric perturbations become
$$
\mathsf {h} _ {\alpha \beta} ^ {\prime} = \mathsf {h} _ {\alpha \beta} - 2 \xi_ {(\alpha , \beta)} .
$$
If we contract this we see that the trace $(\mathbf{h} \equiv \mathbf{h}_{\sigma}^{\sigma} \equiv \eta^{\sigma \lambda} \mathbf{h}_{\sigma \lambda})$ transforms
as $\mathbf{h}' = \mathbf{h} - 2\xi_{, \sigma}^{\sigma}$ , (where $\xi^{\sigma} \equiv \eta^{\sigma \lambda} \xi_{\lambda}$ ). The transformation law for $\overline{\mathbf{h}}_{\alpha \beta}$ is then
$$
\begin{array}{l} \overline {{\mathrm {h}}} _ {\alpha} ^ {\prime} \beta = \mathrm {h} _ {\alpha} ^ {\prime} \beta - \frac {1}{2} \eta_ {\alpha} \beta \mathrm {h} ^ {\prime} = \mathrm {h} _ {\alpha} \beta - 2 \xi_ {(\alpha , \beta)} - \frac {1}{2} \eta_ {\alpha} \beta (\mathrm {h} - 2 \xi_ {, \sigma} ^ {\sigma}) \\ = \overline {{\mathsf {h}}} _ {\alpha \beta} - \xi_ {\alpha , \beta} - \xi_ {\beta , \alpha} + \eta_ {\alpha \beta} \xi^ {\sigma} _ {, \sigma} \\ \end{array}
$$
and
$$
\begin{array}{l} \overline {{\mathfrak {h}}} _ {\alpha} ^ {\prime} \beta^ {\prime} ^ {\beta} = \eta^ {\lambda \beta} \overline {{\mathfrak {h}}} _ {\alpha} ^ {\prime} \beta , \lambda = \overline {{\mathfrak {h}}} _ {\alpha} \beta^ {\prime} ^ {\beta} - \xi_ {\alpha ,} \beta^ {\prime} ^ {\beta} - \xi_ {\beta ,} ^ {\prime} ^ {\beta} + \xi_ {\sigma ,} ^ {\prime} ^ {\sigma}, \\ = \bar {h} _ {\alpha} \beta^ {\prime} ^ {\beta} - \xi_ {\alpha}, \beta^ {\prime} ^ {\beta} = \bar {h} _ {\alpha} \beta^ {\prime} ^ {\beta} - \square \xi_ {\alpha}. \\ \end{array}
$$
So we need only choose the four functions $\xi_{\alpha}$ such that they satisfy the familiar wave equation $\square \xi_{\alpha} = \overline{h}_{\alpha} \beta^{\prime \beta}$ .
Such a solution can always be found (e.g. the retarded integral of classical electromagnetic wave theory). The solution, of course, is not unique. We can always add to $\xi_{a}$ any solution of $\square \xi_{a} = 0$ .
