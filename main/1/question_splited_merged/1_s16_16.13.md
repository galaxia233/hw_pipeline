Problem 16.13. A static, spherically symmetric star is made out of a zero-temperature Fermi gas with Fermi energies much larger than the particle rest masses. Show that the equations of stellar structure have a solution $\mathbf{m}(\mathbf{r}) = 3\mathbf{r} / 14$ . Find $\rho(\mathbf{r})$ , $\mathbf{p}(\mathbf{r})$ , and $\mathbf{n}(\mathbf{r})$ . Although $\mathbf{n}$ is infinite at $\mathbf{r} = 0$ , show that the number of particles out to any radius is finite. Make an embedding diagram of the 3-surface, $\mathbf{t} =$ constant. What kind of singularity is at $\mathbf{r} = 0$ ?

## Solution

Solution 16.13. A relativistic zero-temperature Fermi gas has the equation of state $p = 1/3 \rho$ , so the equations of structure (see Problem 16.12) read
$$
\mathrm {d} \rho / \mathrm {d r} = - \frac {4 \rho (\mathrm {m} + 4 \pi \mathrm {r} ^ {3} \rho / 3)}{\mathrm {r} (\mathrm {r} - 2 \mathrm {m})} \tag {1}
$$
$$
\mathrm {d m} / \mathrm {d r} = 4 \pi \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \rho . \tag {2}
$$
Substitution of the suggested solution $\mathfrak{m}(\mathbf{r}) = 3\mathbf{r} / 14$ into Equation (2) gives $\rho (\mathbf{r}) = (3 / 14)(4\pi \mathbf{r}^2)^{-1}$ ; substitution of this $\rho$ and $\mathfrak{m}$ into Equation (1) verifies that they are a solution. From the equation of state $\mathfrak{p}(\mathbf{r}) = (1 / 14)(4\pi \mathbf{r}^2)^{-1}$ . To find $\mathfrak{n}(\mathbf{r})$ we use the relations for a relativistic Fermi gas (see Problem 5.24)
$$
n = \int_ {0} ^ {p _ {F}} \left(\frac {2}{h ^ {3}}\right) 4 \pi p ^ {2} d p = \frac {8 \pi}{3 h ^ {3}} p _ {F} ^ {3} \tag {3}
$$
$$
\rho = \int_ {0} ^ {\mathrm {P} _ {\mathrm {F}}} \mathrm {p} \left(\frac {2}{\mathrm {h} ^ {3}}\right) 4 \pi \mathrm {p} ^ {2} \mathrm {d p} = \frac {8 \pi}{4 \mathrm {h} ^ {3}} \mathrm {p} _ {\mathrm {F}} ^ {3}. \tag {4}
$$
Eliminating $p_{F}$ (the Fermi momentum) from Equations (3) and (4) gives
$$
\mathbf {n} (\mathbf {r}) = \frac {8 \pi}{3 h ^ {3}} \left(\frac {4 h ^ {3} \rho}{8 \pi}\right) ^ {\frac {3}{4}}
$$
and substitution of $\rho (\mathbf{r})$ gives
$$
n (r) = \frac {K}{r ^ {\frac {3}{2}}} , \quad K = \frac {8 \pi}{3 h ^ {3}} \left(\frac {4 h ^ {3}}{8 \pi} \cdot \frac {3}{1 4} \cdot \frac {1}{4 \pi}\right) ^ {\frac {3}{4}}. \tag {5}
$$
The total number of particles inside radius $r$ is
$$
\begin{array}{l} N (r) = \int_ {0} ^ {r} n (r) d (\text {p r o p e r v o l u m e}) \\ = \int_ {0} ^ {r} n (r) e ^ {\Lambda} 4 \pi r ^ {2} d r \\ = \frac {1 4 \pi}{3} \mathrm {K r} ^ {\frac {3}{2}} \tag {6} \\ \end{array}
$$
which is finite for all $r$ . [Note: In deriving Equation (6) we have used
$$
\mathrm {e} ^ {2 \Lambda} \equiv \mathrm {g} _ {\tau \mathrm {r}} = \left(1 - \frac {2 \mathrm {m (r)}}{\mathrm {r}}\right) ^ {- 1} = 7 / 4 ].
$$
The 3-geometry of the $t =$ constant hypersurface has the metric
$$
\left(^ {3}\right) d s ^ {2} = g _ {r r} d r ^ {2} + r ^ {2} d \Omega^ {2} = (7 / 4) d r ^ {2} + r ^ {2} d \Omega^ {2}.
$$
This obviously has a conical singularity at the origin, because a 2-sphere of circumference $2\pi r$ will have a radius of $(7/4)^{\frac{1}{2}}r$ which is greater than $r$ .
The embedding equation for a radial cut is
$$
\left(3\right) d s ^ {2} = (7 / 4) d r ^ {2} = d r ^ {2} + d z ^ {2}
$$
which implies
$$
z = \pm (3 / 4) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} r
$$
