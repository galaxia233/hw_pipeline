Problem 6.4. Mercator's projection is defined as follows: The map coordinates are rectangular Cartesian coordinates $(x, y)$ such that a straight line on the map is a line of constant compass bearing on the globe.
(c) Show that the great circles are given by $\sinh y = a\sin (x + \beta)$ (except for the special cases $y = 0$ or $x =$ constant).

## Solution

(c) The great circles are the geodesics of the 2-sphere. The geodesic equations are easy to solve, since we have two first integrals. Let a dot denote $\mathrm{d} / \mathrm{ds}$ ; then Equation (8) gives
$$
\operatorname {s e c h} ^ {2} \mathcal {Y} (\dot {\mathbf {x}} ^ {2} + \dot {\mathbf {y}} ^ {2}) = 1. \tag {9}
$$
Since $\mathbf{x}$ is an ignorable coordinate in the metric (8), $\mathbf{g}_{\mathbf{x}\mathbf{x}}\dot{\mathbf{x}}$ is constant (see Solution 7.13), i.e.
$$
\left(\operatorname {s e c h} ^ {2} \mathrm {y}\right) \dot {\mathrm {x}} = \gamma . \tag {10}
$$
When neither $\dot{\mathbf{x}}$ nor $\dot{\mathbf{y}}$ vanishes, we can eliminate $s$ from Equations (9) and (10):
$$
\left(\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathcal {Y}}{\mathrm {d} x}\right) ^ {2} = \frac {\dot {\mathcal {Y}} ^ {2}}{\dot {x} ^ {2}} = \frac {\lambda^ {2} - \cosh^ {2} \mathcal {Y}}{\cosh^ {2} \mathcal {Y}} \tag {11}
$$
where $\lambda = 1 / \gamma$ . Equation (11) is easily integrated by putting $z = \sinh y$ . The result is
$$
\sinh y = \alpha \sin (x + \beta)
$$
where $\alpha = (\lambda^2 - 1)^{\frac{1}{2}}$ and $\beta$ is another constant of integration.
