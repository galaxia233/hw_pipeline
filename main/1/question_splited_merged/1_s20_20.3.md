Problem 20.3. Derive the general expression for the angular deflection of light by the sun's gravitational field, if the light comes from a star which is at an angle $\alpha$ from the sun as seen from earth. Take the earth-sun distance to be R. Do not assume that $\alpha$ is small, but show that in the
limit of small $\alpha$ the answer reduces to the conventional result (Problem 15.6) $\delta \alpha = 4\mathsf{M} / \mathsf{b}$ .

## Solution

Solution 20.3. We may consider purely equatorial motion with no loss of generality. In the gravitational field of the sun, the metric may then be approximated by
$$
d s ^ {2} \approx - \left(1 - \frac {2 M}{r}\right) d t ^ {2} + \left(1 + \frac {2 M}{r}\right) \left(d r ^ {2} + r ^ {2} d \phi^ {2}\right). \tag {1}
$$
Null geodesics for this metric have the form
$$
\frac {\mathrm {b}}{\mathrm {r}} = \sin \phi + \frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {r}} (1 - \cos \phi) \tag {2}
$$
![](images/0e1a3ed941bf65ccbb19fdc99de86879b95d10c69cbf5a6b2c0330ac4fb8b481.jpg)
The angle $\alpha$ between the sun and the star (see figure), as measured by an astronomer on earth, satisfies
$$
\begin{array}{l} \tan \alpha = \tan (\pi - \phi_ {\mathbf {E}} + \delta a) \approx - \tan \phi_ {\mathbf {E}} + \frac {\delta a}{\cos^ {2} \phi_ {\mathbf {E}}} \\ = \frac {\mathrm {u} \hat {\phi}}{\mathrm {u} ^ {\hat {\mathrm {r}}}} = \left[ \frac {\left(1 + \frac {\mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {r}}\right) \mathrm {r} \mathrm {d} \phi / \mathrm {d} \lambda}{\left(1 + \frac {\mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {r}}\right) \mathrm {d r} / \mathrm {d} \lambda} \right] _ {\mathrm {E}} = \left[ \mathrm {r} \frac {\mathrm {d} \phi}{\mathrm {d r}} \right] _ {\mathrm {E}} \tag {3} \\ \end{array}
$$
where $\mathbf{u}^{\hat{\phi}}$ and $\mathbf{u}^{\hat{\mathbf{r}}}$ are the orthonormal components of the photon's 4-velocity, and we have taken advantage of the isotropic form of the metric in Equation (1) to define angles. If Equation (2) is now used, Equation (3) becomes
$$
\begin{array}{l} \tan \phi_ {\mathrm {E}} - \frac {\delta a}{\cos^ {2} \phi_ {\mathrm {E}}} = \frac {\sin \phi_ {\mathrm {E}} \cdot \frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {b}} (1 - \cos \phi_ {\mathrm {E}})}{\cos \phi_ {\mathrm {E}} + \frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {b}} \sin \phi_ {\mathrm {E}}} \\ \approx \tan \phi_ {\mathrm {E}} - \frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {b}} \frac {(1 - \cos \phi_ {\mathrm {E}})}{\cos^ {2} \phi_ {\mathrm {E}}} \tag {4} \\ \end{array}
$$
and hence
$$
\delta \alpha \cong \frac {2 M}{b} (1 + \cos \alpha) \approx \frac {2 M}{R _ {E}} \left(\frac {1 + \cos \alpha}{1 - \cos \alpha}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}. \tag {5}
$$
It is $\delta \alpha$ which is the deflection angle measured by an astronomer at earth. [See also MTW, Section 40.3, and references given therein.]
