Problem 20.4. Show that the sun's angular momentum $\underline{\mathbf{J}}$ modifies the light deflection formula (Problem 20.3) from $\delta \phi = 4\mathsf{M} / \mathsf{b}$ to
$$
\delta \phi = \frac {4 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {b}} \left(1 - \frac {\mathrm {J} \cdot \mathrm {n}}{\mathrm {M b}}\right)
$$
where $\underline{\mathfrak{n}}\equiv$ unit vector in direction of angular momentum of the photons about the center of sun.

## Solution

Solution 20.4. Since we wish to compute only the lowest order deflection due to $\underline{\mathbf{J}}$ , we can look for terms linear in $\underline{\mathbf{J}}$ and set $\mathbf{M} = \mathbf{0}$ in the line element. The total first order deflection will then be the sum of the usual term linear in $\mathbf{M}$ and a term linear in $\underline{\mathbf{J}}$ . Since the metric (for our purposes) is (see Problem 17.1),
$$
d s ^ {2} = - d t ^ {2} - \frac {4 J}{r} \sin^ {2} \theta d t d \phi + d r ^ {2} + r ^ {2} d \Omega^ {2} \tag {1}
$$
(J points along z-axis) the equations of motion may be deduced from the variational principle (Problem 7.25)
$$
\delta \int \left(- \dot {t} ^ {2} - \frac {4 J}{r} \sin^ {2} \theta \dot {t} \dot {\phi} + r ^ {2} + r ^ {2} \dot { \theta } ^ {2} + r ^ {2} \sin^ {2} \theta \dot {\phi} ^ {2}\right) d \lambda = 0 \tag {2}
$$
where $\dot{\mathbf{t}}\equiv \mathrm{dt} / \mathrm{d}\lambda$ etc., and $\lambda$ is affine parameter. The Euler equations for Equation (2), to first order in J, are
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} \left(2 \dot {\mathrm {t}} + \frac {4 \mathrm {J}}{\mathrm {r}} \sin^ {2} \theta \dot {\phi}\right) = 0 \tag {3a}
$$
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} \left(2 \dot {\phi} r ^ {2} \sin^ {2} \theta - \frac {4 J}{r} \sin^ {2} \theta \dot {t}\right) = 0, \tag {3b},
$$
which imply that
$$
\mathbf {P} _ {0} \equiv \dot {\mathbf {t}} + \frac {2 \mathrm {J}}{\mathrm {r}} \sin^ {2} \theta \dot {\phi} \tag {3c}
$$
$$
\mathrm {P} _ {\phi} \equiv \dot {\phi} \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \sin^ {2} \theta - \frac {2 \mathrm {J}}{\mathrm {r}} \sin^ {2} \theta \mathrm {P} _ {0} \tag {3d}
$$
are constants, and
$$
\ddot {\mathbf {r}} = \mathbf {r} \dot {\theta} ^ {2} + \mathbf {r} \sin^ {2} \theta \dot {\phi} ^ {2} + \frac {4 \mathrm {J}}{\mathbf {r} ^ {2}} \sin^ {2} \theta \dot {\phi} \mathbf {P} _ {0} \tag {3e}
$$
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} \left(\mathrm {r} ^ {2} \dot {\theta}\right) = \mathrm {P} _ {\phi} ^ {2} \cos \theta / \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \sin^ {3} \theta . \tag {3f}
$$
From Equation (3f), one may deduce a third constant of the motion (in addition to $\mathbf{P}_0$ and $\mathbf{P}_{\phi}$ ):
$$
\mathrm {P} _ {\theta} ^ {2} + \frac {\mathrm {P} _ {\phi} ^ {2}}{\sin^ {2} \theta} \equiv \mathrm {L} ^ {2} = \text {c o n s t a n t} \left(\mathrm {P} _ {\theta} \equiv \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \dot {\theta}\right). \tag {4}
$$
We will now decompose the bending into three contributions, each defined by the relationship (see figure) of the ray trajectory (a straight line to lowest order) to $\underline{\mathbf{J}}$ . (Since we are working only to linear order, the total bending must be the sum of such contributions.)
In both cases (a) and (b), the angular momentum of the light beam about the sun's center has zero projection onto $\underline{\mathbf{J}}$ , in case (c) it has unit projection.

First we consider cases (a) and (b): In both cases (a) and (b) $\mathbf{P}_{\phi} = 0$ (since $\mathbf{P}_{\phi}$ is a constant of the motion). We wish to calculate $\mathbf{r}(\theta)$ . From Equation (3d) we have
$$
\dot {\phi} = \frac {2 \mathrm {J P} _ {0}}{\mathrm {r} ^ {3}} = \mathcal {O} (\mathrm {J}) \tag {5a}
$$
and from Equation (4)
$$
\mathbf {P} _ {\theta} = \mathbf {r} ^ {2} \dot {\theta} = \mathbf {L} = \text {c o n s t a n t}. \tag {5b}
$$
Equations (3e) and (5a), to $\mathcal{O}(\mathbf{J})$ , now give us
$$
\ddot {\mathbf {r}} = \mathbf {r} \dot {\theta} ^ {2}, \tag {5c}
$$
but this result and Equation (5b) guarantee straight-line motion! Thus in cases (a) and (b) $J$ makes no contribution to the bending of light.
Now we consider case (c): In case (c), we may set $\theta = \pi / 2$ , and $\dot{\theta} = 0$ (Equation (3f) guarantees that $\ddot{\theta}$ will always then be 0) and solve our equations accordingly. Using
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} = \frac {\mathrm {d} \phi}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} \frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d} \phi} = \left(\frac {\mathbf {P} _ {\phi}}{\mathbf {r} ^ {2}} + \frac {2 \mathbf {J P} _ {0}}{\mathbf {r} ^ {3}}\right) \frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d} \phi},
$$
and defining $\mathfrak{u} \equiv 1 / \mathfrak{r}$ , $\ell \equiv \mathrm{P}_0 / \mathrm{P}_{\phi}$ , we may put Equation (3e) in the form
$$
u ^ {\prime \prime} + u = - 6 u ^ {2} J \ell - 2 J \ell u ^ {\prime 2} - 4 J \ell u u ^ {\prime \prime} \tag {6}
$$
where $\mathbf{u}' \equiv \mathrm{du} / \mathrm{d}\phi$ . We will solve Equation (6) by perturbation techniques; we define
$$
u = u _ {0} + u _ {1} \tag {7}
$$
with $\mathbf{u_1} = \mathcal{O}(\mathbf{J})$ . To lowest order, the equation is $\mathbf{u}_0^{\prime \prime} + \mathbf{u}_0 = 0$ which implies
$$
u _ {0} = \frac {1}{b} \cos \phi \tag {8}
$$
where $\mathbf{b}$ is the impact parameter and where we have chosen our coordinate system such that the ray moves parallel to the $y$ -axis. For the second order solution, $u_{1}$ must satisfy the equation
$$
\mathrm {u} _ {1} ^ {\prime \prime} + \mathrm {u} _ {1} = - \frac {2 \mathrm {J} \ell}{\mathrm {b} ^ {2}} \left(\sin^ {2} \phi + \cos^ {2} \phi\right) = - \frac {2 \mathrm {J} \ell}{\mathrm {b} ^ {2}} \tag {9a}
$$
or
$$
u _ {1} = - \frac {2 J \ell}{b ^ {2}}. \tag {9b}
$$
Thus, from Equations (7), (8), (9b), the solution for $\mathbf{u}$ is
$$
u \approx \frac {1}{b} \cos \phi - \frac {2 J \ell}{b ^ {2}}. \tag {10}
$$
The bending at each asymptote, $a$ , is found by setting $u = 0$ ( $r = \infty$ ). At one asymptote $\phi \approx \pi / 2 + a$ so that $\cos \phi \approx -a$ and
$$
a \approx - \frac {2 J \ell}{b}. \tag {11}
$$
There is an equal contribution at the other asymptote, so the total bending is
$$
\delta \phi = 2 a = - \frac {4 J}{b ^ {2}} \tag {12}
$$
(where we have used the fact that $\ell = \mathbf{P}_0 / \mathbf{P}_{\phi} \approx \mathfrak{b}^{-1}$ ).
From this result, and the results from cases (a) and (b), we may write the deflection of light by $\mathbf{J}$ as
$$
\delta \phi = - \frac {4 J \cdot n}{b ^ {2}} \tag {13}
$$
where $\mathbf{n}$ is a unit vector in the direction of the angular momentum of the ray about the Sun's center.
Note that the dimensionless small parameter in which we have been expanding is
$$
\left(\frac {\mathrm {J}}{\mathrm {b} ^ {2}}\right) _ {\text {s u n}} = \frac {1 . 7 \times 1 0 ^ {4 8} \mathrm {g m - c m} ^ {2} / \sec}{(7 \times 1 0 ^ {1 0} \mathrm {c m}) ^ {2}} \approx 1 0 ^ {- 1 2} \approx 2 \times 1 0 ^ {- 7} \operatorname {a r c s e c}.
$$
