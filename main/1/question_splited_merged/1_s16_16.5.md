Problem 16.5. In the Brans-Dicke theory of gravity (see MTW p. 1070 or Weinberg p. 160 for field equations), show that the only static spherically
vacuum solution which is regular at the origin is the flat-space metric $\pmb{\eta}$ and constant scalar field $\Phi$ .

## Solution

Solution 16.5. The vacuum field equations for the scalar field $\Phi$ and metric $\mathbf{g}_{\mu \nu}$ in the Brans-Dicke theory are
$$
\begin{array}{l} R _ {\alpha \beta} - \frac {1}{2} g _ {\alpha \beta} R = \frac {\omega}{\Phi^ {2}} \left(\Phi_ {, \alpha} \Phi_ {, \beta} - \frac {1}{2} g _ {\alpha \beta} \Phi_ {, \gamma} \Phi^ {, \gamma}\right) (1) \\ + \Phi^ {- 1} \left(\Phi_ {, a; \beta} - \mathrm {g} _ {a \beta} \square \Phi\right) \\ \square \Phi = 0. (2) \\ \end{array}
$$
If the standard form of the spherically symmetric static metric $(-e^{2U}dt^2 + e^{2\lambda}dr^2 + r^2 d\Omega^2)$ is used, $\square \Phi = 0$ becomes
$$
\left(\mathrm {e} ^ {\mathrm {U} - \lambda} _ {\mathrm {r}} ^ {2} \Phi_ {, \mathrm {r}}\right) _ {, \mathrm {r}} = 0. \tag {3}
$$
The general solution is
$$
\Phi = a \int_ {r} ^ {\infty} \frac {e ^ {\lambda - U}}{r ^ {2}} d r + b. \tag {4}
$$
Near $\mathbf{r} = 0$ the metric functions $e^{2\mathbf{U}}$ and $e^{2\lambda}$ approach unity. (The metric is Lorentzian near $\mathbf{r} = 0$ .) The integral in Equation (4) therefore blows up near $\mathbf{r} = 0$ and $\Phi$ takes the form
$$
\Phi \approx a / r + b \tag {5}
$$
near the origin. But if $a$ is nonzero, the right hand side of Equation (1) is of order $a^2 / r^2$ . (This is not a coordinate effect; the trace of the right hand side is of order $a^2 / r^2$ so by Equation (1) the scalar curvature blows up as $a^2 / r^2$ .) For a solution regular at the origin, then, a must be zero, i.e. $\Phi$ must be constant. For $\Phi$ constant, Equation (1) reduces to the vacuum static spherically symmetric field equations of general relativity. From Birkhoff's theorem we know that the only solution to these equations which is well behaved at $r = 0$ is $g_{\mu \nu} = \eta_{\mu \nu}$ .
