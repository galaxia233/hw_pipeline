Problem 18.18. Calculate in detail the lifetime of a hydrogen atom in the 3d state against decaying to the 1s state by gravitational radiation.

## Solution

Solution 18.18. To linearized order in the gravitational field, the interaction Hamiltonian with a system whose stress-energy tensor is $\mathbf{T}_{\mu \nu}$ is
$$
\mathrm {H} = (8 \pi \mathrm {G}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \mathrm {T} _ {\mu \nu} \mathrm {h} ^ {\mu \nu}. \tag {1}
$$
We shall use units with $c = \overline{n} = 1$ , so $G^{\frac{1}{2}} = 1.616 \times 10^{-33}$ cm. For the electron in the hydrogen atom,
$$
T _ {\mu \nu} = m _ {e} u _ {\mu} u _ {\nu} = \frac {p _ {\mu} p _ {\nu}}{m _ {e}}; \tag {2}
$$
we work in the transverse-traceless gauge for $\mathfrak{h}^{\mu \nu}$ , so
$$
\mathrm {h} _ {0 \mu} = 0, \quad \mathrm {h} _ {, j} ^ {\mathrm {i j}} = 0 \tag {3}
$$
and thus
$$
\mathrm {H} = (8 \pi \mathrm {G}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \frac {\mathrm {p} _ {\mathrm {i}} \mathrm {p} _ {\mathrm {j}} \mathrm {h} ^ {\mathrm {i j}}}{\mathrm {m} _ {\mathrm {e}}} \tag {4}
$$
Since a graviton has spin 2, the decay must be to an s state. The transition matrix element from a 3d state to a 1s state with the emission of a graviton with wave-vector $\underline{\mathbf{k}}$ and polarization $\lambda$ (for right-hand circular polarization $\lambda = 1$ and for left-hand circular polarization $\lambda = -1$ ) is
$$
\mathrm {T} = <   1 \mathrm {s}; \underline {{\mathrm {k}}}, \lambda | \mathrm {H} | 3 \mathrm {d}; 0 >. \tag {5}
$$
We now expand
$$
h ^ {i j} = \frac {1}{(2 \pi) ^ {\frac {3}{2}}} \int \frac {d ^ {3} k}{(2 \omega) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}} \sum_ {\lambda} \left(e ^ {- i k \cdot r} e _ {k, \lambda} ^ {i j} a _ {k, \lambda} + e ^ {i k \cdot r} e _ {k, \lambda} ^ {i j *} a _ {k, \lambda} ^ {\dagger}\right). \tag {6}
$$
Here $\omega$ is the frequency of the wave and $e^{ij}$ the polarization tensor. The creation and annihilation operators satisfy the equal-time commutation relations
$$
\left[ \mathbf {a} _ {\mathbf {k}, \lambda}, \mathbf {a} _ {\mathbf {k} ^ {\prime}, \lambda^ {\prime}} ^ {\dagger} \right] = \delta^ {3} (\underline {{\mathbf {k}}} - \underline {{\mathbf {k}}} ^ {\prime}) \delta_ {\lambda \lambda^ {\prime}}
$$
and so
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbf {a} _ {\mathbf {k}, \lambda} | 0 > = 0 \\ <   \underline {{\mathrm {k}}}, \lambda | a _ {\mathbf {k} ^ {\prime}, \lambda^ {\prime}} ^ {\dagger} | 0 > = \delta^ {3} \left(\underline {{\mathrm {k}}} - \underline {{\mathrm {k}}} ^ {\prime}\right) \delta_ {\lambda \lambda^ {\prime}}. \tag {7} \\ \end{array}
$$
Using Equations (4), (6), and (7) in Equation (5), we get
$$
T = \frac {(8 \pi G) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}}{m _ {e}} \frac {1}{(2 \pi) ^ {\frac {3}{2}}} \frac {1}{(2 \omega) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}} <   1 s | e ^ {i k \cdot r} p _ {i} p _ {j} e ^ {i j ^ {*}} | 3 d >. \tag {8}
$$
Since $\mathrm{kr} \sim (1\mathrm{eV}) \times 10^{-8}\mathrm{cm} \sim 10^{-4}$ , we can make the approximation of putting $\mathbf{e}^{\mathbf{i}\mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{r}} = 1$ (cf. the "dipole" approximation for electromagnetic transitions). The transition rate is
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathrm {d} \Gamma = 2 \pi | \mathrm {T} | ^ {2} \times (\text {d e n s i t y o f f i n a l s t a t e s f o r g r a v i t o n}) \\ = 2 \pi | \mathbf {T} | ^ {2} \omega^ {2} d \Omega \tag {9} \\ \end{array}
$$
where $\mathbf{d}\Omega$ is the solid angle into which the graviton is emitted. Hence
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} \Gamma}{\mathrm {d} \Omega} = \frac {\mathrm {G} \omega}{\pi \mathrm {m} _ {\mathrm {e}} ^ {2}} | <   1 \mathrm {s} | \mathrm {p} _ {i} \mathrm {p} _ {j} \mathrm {e} _ {\mathrm {k}, \lambda} ^ {\mathrm {i j} ^ {*}} | 3 \mathrm {d} > | ^ {2}. \tag {10}
$$
If we use the explicit form of the wave functions, the matrix element in this equation can be evaluated. Since we do not observe the spins of the initial or final states of the atom, we will sum over the final spin states and average over the initial spin states. This is most easily done by introducing the pure spin-2 operator
$$
Q _ {i j} = p _ {i} p _ {j} - \frac {1}{3} \delta_ {i j} p ^ {2}. \tag {11}
$$
Since $\mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{i}}^{\mathbf{i}} = 0$ , we can replace $\mathbf{p}_{\mathbf{i}} \mathbf{p}_{\mathbf{j}}$ by $Q_{ij}$ in Equation (10). The spherical components of $Q_{ij}$ are
$$
\begin{array}{l} Q _ {2} \equiv \frac {1}{2} \left(Q _ {\mathbf {x x}} - Q _ {\mathbf {y y}} + 2 \mathrm {i} Q _ {\mathbf {x y}}\right) \\ Q _ {1} \equiv Q _ {z x} + i Q _ {z y} \\ Q _ {0} \equiv \left(\frac {2}{3}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \left(Q _ {z z} - \frac {1}{2} Q _ {x x} - \frac {1}{2} Q _ {y y}\right) \tag {12} \\ Q _ {- 1} \equiv Q _ {z x} - i Q _ {z y} \\ Q _ {- 2} \equiv \frac {1}{2} \left(Q _ {x x} - Q _ {y y} - 2 i Q _ {x y}\right). \\ \end{array}
$$
Since $\mathbf{e}_{\lambda}^{\mathrm{ij}}$ has x-y components (for $\lambda = \pm 1$ ):
$$
2 ^ {- \frac {1}{2}} \left[ \begin{array}{c c} 1 & \pm \mathbf {i} \\ \pm \mathbf {i} & - 1 \end{array} \right]
$$
we find
$$
Q _ {i j} e _ {\lambda} ^ {i j *} = 2 ^ {\frac {1}{2}} Q _ {- 2 \lambda}. \tag {13}
$$
Since $\mathbf{Q}_{\sigma}$ has spin two, the Wigner-Eckart theorem says
$$
<   \mathrm {j} _ {j} \mathrm {m} _ {j} | \mathrm {Q} _ {\sigma} | \mathrm {j} _ {i} \mathrm {m} _ {i} > = <   \mathrm {j} _ {j} \| \mathrm {Q} \| \mathrm {j} _ {i} > <   2 \sigma \mathrm {j} _ {i} \mathrm {m} _ {i} | \mathrm {j} _ {j} \mathrm {m} _ {j} > \tag {14}
$$
where the "reduced" matrix is defined as
$$
<   j _ {j} \| Q \| j _ {i} > = \sum_ {m _ {i} m _ {j} \sigma} \frac {1}{2 j _ {j} + 1} <   2 \sigma j _ {i} m _ {i} | j _ {j} m _ {j} > <   j _ {j} m _ {j} | Q _ {\sigma} | j _ {i} m _ {i} > \tag {15}
$$
and where $< \mathrm{jm} j_{\mathrm{i}} m_{\mathrm{i}} | j_{\mathrm{j}} m_{\mathrm{j}} >$ denotes a Clebsch-Gordon coefficient. Thus Equations (13) and (14) (with the n quantum number added) give
$$
\left| <   n _ {j} j _ {j} m _ {j} \right| Q _ {i j} e ^ {i j ^ {*}} \left| n _ {i} j _ {i} m _ {i} > \right| ^ {2} = 2 \left| <   n _ {j} j _ {j} \| Q \| n _ {i} j _ {i} > \right| ^ {2} <   2 - 2 \lambda \left. j _ {i} m _ {i} \right| j _ {j} m _ {j} > \left| ^ {2} \right..
$$
Now we sum over $\mathfrak{m}_{\mathbf{j}}$ and average over $\mathfrak{m}_{\mathbf{i}}$ , using
$$
\begin{array}{l} \frac {1}{2 \mathrm {j} _ {\mathrm {i}} + 1} \sum_ {\mathrm {m} _ {\mathrm {i}} \mathrm {m} _ {j}} | <   2 - 2 \lambda \mathrm {j} _ {i} \mathrm {m} _ {i} | \mathrm {j} _ {i} \mathrm {m} _ {j} > | ^ {2} = \frac {1}{2 \mathrm {j} _ {i} + 1} \sum_ {\mathrm {m} _ {i} \mathrm {m} _ {j}} \frac {(2 \mathrm {j} _ {j} + 1)}{5} | <   \mathrm {j} _ {j} - \mathrm {m} _ {j} \mathrm {j} _ {i} \mathrm {m} _ {i} | 2 - 2 \lambda > | ^ {2} \\ = \frac {1}{5} \frac {2 j _ {j} + 1}{2 j _ {i} + 1} \\ \end{array}
$$
and thus
$$
\frac {d \Gamma}{d \Omega} = \frac {G \omega}{\pi m _ {e} ^ {2}} \frac {2}{5} \frac {2 j _ {j} + 1}{2 j _ {i} + 1} \left| <   n _ {j} j _ {j} \| Q \| n _ {i} j _ {i} > \right| ^ {2}. \tag {16}
$$
This is independent of the polarization of the graviton and of the angle of emission. The total rate is obtained by multiplying by 2 (for the two polarization states) and by $4\pi$ (solid angle), giving
$$
\Gamma = \frac {\mathrm {G} \omega}{\mathrm {m} _ {\mathrm {e}} ^ {2}} \frac {1 6}{5} \frac {2 \mathrm {j} _ {\mathrm {j}} + 1}{2 \mathrm {j} _ {i} + 1} | <   \mathrm {n} _ {j} \mathrm {j} _ {j} \| \mathrm {Q} \| \mathrm {n} _ {i} \mathrm {j} _ {i} > | ^ {2}. \tag {17}
$$
The reduced matrix element can be evaluated from Equation (14) and any convenient choice of $\mathfrak{m}_{\mathbf{i}}$ and $\mathfrak{m}_{\mathbf{j}}$ . For example,
$$
<   1 \mathbf {s} \| Q \| 3 d > = \frac {<   1 0 0 | Q _ {0} | 3 2 0 >}{<   2 0 2 0 | 0 0 >}. \tag {18}
$$
The Clebsch-Gordon coefficient in Equation (18) is unity. From Equations (11) and (12),
$$
Q _ {0} = \left(\frac {3}{2}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \left(p _ {z} p _ {z} - \frac {1}{3} p ^ {2}\right).
$$
The $p^2$ term does not contribute to the matrix element in Equation (18) because it cannot couple $j = 0$ to $j = 2$ . Thus
$$
<   \mathbf {1 s} \| \mathrm {Q} \| 3 \mathrm {d} > = \left(\frac {3}{2}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} <   1 0 0 | _ {\mathrm {p} _ {z} \mathrm {p} _ {z}} | 3 2 0 >. \tag {19}
$$
In general one would introduce a complete set of states to evaluate the matrix element in Equation (19) as a sum of terms of the form $< 100|\mathfrak{p}_z|\mathfrak{n}> <   \mathfrak{n}|\mathfrak{p}_z|320>$ . Since the $|100\rangle$ wave function is so simple, however, we can use
$$
\mathbf {p} _ {z} \rightarrow - \mathrm {i} \partial / \partial z
$$
so
$$
\begin{array}{l} p _ {z} ^ {2} \rightarrow - \partial^ {2} / \partial z ^ {2} = - \left(\cos \theta \frac {\partial}{\partial r} - \frac {\sin \theta}{r} \frac {\partial}{\partial \theta}\right)\left(\cos \theta \frac {\partial}{\partial r} - \frac {\sin \theta}{r} \frac {\partial}{\partial \theta}\right) \\ = - \left(\cos^ {2} \theta \frac {\partial^ {2}}{\partial r ^ {2}} + \frac {\sin^ {2} \theta}{r} \frac {\partial}{\partial r}\right) \\ \end{array}
$$
when acting on the spherically-symmetric $|100\rangle$ state. Thus
$$
\begin{array}{l} <   3 2 0 | p _ {z} ^ {2} | 1 0 0 > = - \int_ {0} ^ {\infty} r ^ {2} d r \int_ {0} ^ {\pi} \sin \theta d \theta \int_ {0} ^ {2 \pi} d \phi \left(\frac {5}{1 6 \pi}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \frac {(3 \cos^ {2} \theta - 1)}{a ^ {\frac {3}{2}}} \frac {2 (3 0) ^ {1/2}}{9 5 5} \left(\frac {r}{a}\right) ^ {3} \\ \times \mathrm {e} ^ {- \mathrm {r} / 3 \mathrm {a}} \left(\cos^ {2} \theta \frac {\partial^ {2}}{\partial \mathrm {r} ^ {2}} + \frac {\sin^ {2} \theta}{\mathrm {r}} \frac {\partial}{\partial \mathrm {r}}\right) \frac {1}{(4 \pi) ^ {1/2}} \frac {2}{\mathrm {a} ^ {3/2}} \mathrm {e} ^ {- \mathrm {r} / \mathrm {a}} \\ = \frac {- 6 ^ {1/2}}{1 9 1 a ^ {6}} \int_ {0} ^ {\infty} d r r ^ {5} e ^ {- \frac {4 r}{3 a}} \int_ {0} ^ {\pi} \sin \theta d \theta (3 \cos^ {2} \theta - 1) \left(\frac {\cos^ {2} \theta}{a ^ {2}} - \frac {\sin^ {2} \theta}{r a}\right) \\ = \frac {6 ^ {1/2}}{1 9 1 a ^ {6}} \frac {8}{1 5} \int_ {0} ^ {\infty} d r e ^ {- \frac {4 r}{3 a}} \left(\frac {r ^ {5}}{a ^ {2}} + \frac {r ^ {4}}{a}\right) \\ = - \frac {1}{a ^ {2}} \frac {6 ^ {1/2} \times 8 \times 2 4 3 \times 5 7}{1 9 1 \times 1 5 \times 2 \times 2 5 6} \\ = \frac {- . 1 9}{a ^ {2}} \\ \end{array}
$$
where $\mathfrak{a} = (\mathfrak{m}_{\mathbf{e}}\alpha)^{-1}$ is the Bohr radius and $\alpha = 1 / 137$ is the fine structure constant.
$$
\begin{array}{l} \Gamma = \frac {G \omega}{m _ {e} ^ {2}} \cdot \frac {1 6}{5} \cdot \frac {1}{5} \cdot \frac {3}{2} \cdot \frac {(. 1 9) ^ {2}}{a ^ {4}} \\ = . 3 6 \mathrm {G m} _ {e} ^ {2} \omega a ^ {4}. \\ \end{array}
$$
Now $\mathbf{G} \mathbf{m}_{\mathbf{e}}^{2} = 1.75 \times 10^{-45}$ and $\omega = 12 \mathrm{eV}$ for $3 \mathrm{p} \rightarrow 1 \mathrm{s}$ . Thus the lifetime is $\Gamma^{-1} = 1.9 \times 10^{38}$ sec.
