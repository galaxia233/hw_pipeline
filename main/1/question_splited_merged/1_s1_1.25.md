Problem 1.25. Let $\mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{x}}, \mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{y}}, \mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{z}}$ be infinitesimal rotation operators defined so that $1 + \mathrm{i}\mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{j}}\theta / 2$ is a rotation by a small angle $\theta$ around the j-axis. Let $\mathbf{K}_{\mathbf{x}}, \mathbf{K}_{\mathbf{y}}, \mathbf{K}_{\mathbf{z}}$ be infinitesimal boost operators defined so that $1 + \mathrm{i}\mathbf{K}_{\mathbf{j}}\mathbf{v} / 2$ is a boost by a small velocity $\mathbf{v}$ in the j-direction. Show that the following relationships, and all their cyclic permutations, are true:
$$
\begin{array}{l} [ J _ {x}, J _ {y} ] = 2 i J _ {z} \\ [ J _ {x}, K _ {y} ] = 2 i K _ {z} \\ \left[ \mathrm {K} _ {\mathbf {x}}, \mathrm {K} _ {\mathbf {y}} \right] = - 2 \mathrm {i} \mathrm {J} _ {z}. \\ \end{array}
$$
Find a representation of the Lorentz group in terms of Pauli spin matrices $\sigma_{\mathbf{x}}$ , $\sigma_{\mathbf{y}}$ , $\sigma_{\mathbf{z}}$ , and the unit matrix.

## Solution

Solution 1.25. Let us first derive the forms of the infinitesimal operators by considering their action on a spacetime function $f(x, y, z, t)$ :
(i) Rotations about $\mathbf{z}$ axis:
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbf {x} ^ {\prime} \approx \mathbf {x} - \mathbf {y} \theta \\ \mathbf {y} ^ {\prime} \approx \mathbf {y} + \mathbf {x} \theta \tag {1} \\ z ^ {\prime} = z \\ t ^ {\prime} = t, \\ \end{array}
$$
$$
f \left(x ^ {\prime}, y ^ {\prime}, z ^ {\prime}\right) - f (x, y, z) = \theta \left[ - y \partial_ {x} + x \partial_ {y} \right] f , \tag {2}
$$
thus
$$
J _ {z} = - 2 \mathrm {i} \left(\mathrm {x} \partial_ {\mathrm {y}} - \mathrm {y} \partial_ {\mathrm {x}}\right), \tag {3}
$$
since
$$
\mathbf {f} (\mathbf {x} ^ {\prime}, \mathbf {y} ^ {\prime}, z ^ {\prime}) = (1 + \mathrm {i} \mathbf {J} _ {z} \theta / 2) \mathbf {f} (\mathbf {x}, \mathbf {y}, z).
$$
$\mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{y}}$ $\mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{x}}$ are obtained by cyclical permutations of Equation (3).
(ii) Boosts in $\mathbf{z}$ direction:
$$
\begin{array}{l} z ^ {\prime} \approx z - v t \\ t ^ {\prime} \approx t - v x \\ \mathbf {y} ^ {\prime} = \mathbf {y} \\ z ^ {\prime} = z \\ \end{array}
$$
$$
f (x ^ {\prime}, y ^ {\prime}, z ^ {\prime}, t ^ {\prime}) - f (x, y, z, t) = \left[ - t \partial_ {z} - z \partial_ {t} \right] v f (x, y, z, t)
$$
thus
$$
\mathrm {K} _ {z} = 2 \mathrm {i} \left(\mathrm {t} \frac {\partial}{\partial z} + z \frac {\partial}{\partial t}\right), \text {e t c .} \tag {4}
$$
(iii) Now, commutation relations may be worked out from Equations (3) and (4) by the usual procedure, for example:
$$
\left[ J _ {x}, J _ {y} \right] = - 4 \left[ y \partial_ {z} - z \partial_ {y}, z \partial_ {x} - x \partial_ {z} \right] = 4 \left(x \partial_ {y} - y \partial_ {x}\right) = 2 i J _ {z}, \tag {5}
$$
and similarly for all other commutators.
(iv) A representation of the Lorentz group is specified by giving explicit matrices for the generators. We can associate the operators with the Pauli spin matrices
$$
\sigma_ {\mathbf {x}} = \left[ \begin{array}{c c} 0 & 1 \\ \mathbf {1} & 0 \end{array} \right] \quad \text {e t c .}
$$
according to
$$
\begin{array}{l} \underline {{\mathbf {J}}} \rightarrow \underline {{\sigma}} \\ \underline {{\mathbf {K}}} \rightarrow \underline {{\mathrm {i} \sigma}}. \\ \end{array}
$$
The commutation relations for $\underline{\mathbf{J}}$ and $\underline{\mathbf{K}}$ are satisfied since $[\sigma_{\mathbf{x}},\sigma_{\mathbf{y}}] = 2\mathrm{i}\sigma_{z}$ etc. To find the matrices corresponding to finite transformations $\mathbf{L}$ , note that for example $\mathbf{J}_{z}$ is defined by
$$
\left. \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {L}}{\mathrm {d} \theta} \right| _ {\theta = 0} = \frac {\mathrm {i} \mathbf {J} _ {z}}{2} ,
$$
so that
$$
\mathrm {L} (\theta) = \exp \left(\mathrm {i} \theta \mathrm {J} _ {z} / 2\right).
$$
An arbitrary finite transformation $\mathbf{L}$ will have six parameters; in the general case the rotation parameters $\underline{\theta}^{*}$ (here treated as a 3-vector) will be different from the physical rotation angle $\underline{\theta}$ and the parameters representing boosts, $\underline{\mathbf{v}}^{*}$ , will differ from the physical relative velocity $\underline{\mathbf{v}}$ . These are details; the Lorentz group is defined completely by its generators $\underline{\mathbf{J}}, \underline{\mathbf{K}}$ ; its parametrization is largely arbitrary. The infinitesimal version of an arbitrary transformation $\mathbf{L}$ is
$$
\delta \mathrm {L} = \mathrm {i} \left(\theta_ {\mathrm {x}} ^ {*} \mathrm {J} _ {\mathrm {x}} + \theta_ {\mathrm {y}} ^ {*} \mathrm {J} _ {\mathrm {y}} + \theta_ {\mathrm {z}} ^ {*} \mathrm {J} _ {\mathrm {z}} + \mathrm {v} _ {\mathrm {x}} ^ {*} \mathrm {K} _ {\mathrm {x}} + \mathrm {v} _ {\mathrm {y}} ^ {*} \mathrm {K} _ {\mathrm {y}} + \mathrm {v} _ {\mathrm {z}} ^ {*} \mathrm {K} _ {\mathrm {z}}\right) / 2 = (\mathrm {i} \underline {{\theta}} ^ {*} - \underline {{\mathrm {v}}} ^ {*}) \cdot \underline {{\sigma}} / 2.
$$
Let us define
$$
\underline {{\mathbf {q}}} \equiv (\underline {{\mathbf {i} \theta}} ^ {*} - \underline {{\mathbf {v}}} ^ {*}) / 2
$$
$$
q ^ {2} \equiv \underset {\sim} {q} \cdot \underset {\sim} {q} = \frac {1}{4} \left(- \theta^ {* 2} - 2 i \underset {\sim} {\theta} ^ {*} \cdot v ^ {*} + v ^ {2}\right).
$$
(Note that pure boosts and pure rotations correspond respectively to pure real and pure imaginary $\underline{\mathbf{q}}$ .) We have then, for the general finite transformation:
$$
\begin{array}{l} L (\underline {{\theta}}, \underline {{v}}) = \exp (\underline {{q}} \cdot \underline {{\sigma}}) = \sum_ {n = 0} ^ {\infty} (\underline {{q}} \cdot \underline {{\sigma}}) ^ {n} / n! \\ = \sum_ {n = 0, 2, 4 \dots} ^ {\infty} q ^ {n} / n! + \sum_ {n = 1, 3, 5 \dots} ^ {\infty} (\underline {{q}} \cdot \underline {{\sigma}}) q ^ {n - 1} / n! \\ = \cosh q + (\underline {{q}} \cdot \underline {{\sigma}} / q) \sinh q. \\ \end{array}
$$
Here we have used
$$
(\underset {\sim} {q} \cdot \underset {\sim} {\sigma}) (\underset {\sim} {q} \cdot \underset {\sim} {\sigma}) = q _ {i} q _ {j} (\sigma_ {i}, \sigma_ {j}) = q _ {i} q _ {j} \delta^ {i j} = q ^ {2}.
$$
