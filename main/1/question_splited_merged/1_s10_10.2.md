Problem 10.2. Show that Killing's equation $\xi_{\alpha;\beta} + \xi_{\beta;\alpha} = 0$ is equivalent to $\mathcal{L}_{\pmb{\xi}}\mathbf{g} = 0$ , where $\mathbf{g}$ is the metric tensor. Interpret this result geometrically.

## Solution

Solution 10.2. It is straightforward to prove this statement in component notation. Alternately, “dot” $\mathfrak{L}_{\pmb{\xi}}$ with two arbitrary vector fields A and B:
$$
\begin{array}{l} 0 = \mathbf {A} \cdot \mathcal {L} _ {\xi} \mathbf {g} \cdot \mathbf {B} = \mathcal {L} _ {\xi} (\mathbf {A} \cdot \mathbf {B}) - \mathbf {B} \cdot \mathcal {L} _ {\xi} \mathbf {A} - \mathbf {A}, \mathcal {L} _ {\xi} \mathbf {B} \\ = \nabla_ {\xi} (\mathbf {A} \cdot \mathbf {B}) - \mathbf {B} \cdot \left(\nabla_ {\xi} \mathbf {A} - \nabla_ {\mathbf {A}} \xi\right) - \mathbf {A} \cdot \left(\nabla_ {\xi} \mathbf {B} - \nabla_ {\mathbf {B}} \xi\right) \\ = \mathbf {B} \cdot \nabla_ {\mathbf {A}} \boldsymbol {\xi} + \mathbf {A} \cdot \nabla_ {\mathbf {B}} \boldsymbol {\xi} = 2 \mathbf {A} ^ {\alpha} \mathbf {B} ^ {\beta} \xi_ {(a; \beta)}. \\ \end{array}
$$
Since $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ are arbitrary, $\xi_{(a;\beta)}$ must vanish. To interpret this result geometrically recall that the Lie derivative of any geometrical quantity, $\phi_{\mathbf{A}}$ , is the functional change of that quantity, $\delta \phi_{\mathbf{A}} \equiv \phi_{\mathbf{A}}^{\mathrm{new}}(\mathbf{x}^{a}) - \phi_{\mathbf{A}}^{\mathrm{old}}(\mathbf{x}^{a})$ under a displacement of coordinates by $\xi$ . This is equivalent to the change in $\phi_{\mathbf{A}}$ upon moving it in the manifold along $\xi$ . For the Lie derivative of $\mathbf{g}$ along $\xi$ to vanish, therefore, requires that the geometry to be unchanged as one moves in the $\xi$ direction, i.e. $\xi$ represents a direction of symmetry of spacetime.
