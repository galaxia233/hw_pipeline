Problem 19.20. For a closed $(k = 1)$ Friedmann universe in which radiation dominates for only a negligibly short fraction of the life of the universe, how many times can a photon encircle the universe from the moment of the creation of the universe to the moment of its death?

## Solution

Solution 19.20. Measuring time in terms of development angle $\eta$ defined by $\mathrm{d}\eta = \mathrm{d}t / \mathbb{R}(t)$ , the Friedmann metric for a radial $(\mathrm{d}\theta = \mathrm{d}\phi = 0)$ photon becomes
$$
0 = \mathrm {d} s ^ {2} = \mathbb {R} ^ {2} (\eta) (- \mathrm {d} \eta^ {2} + \mathrm {d} \chi^ {2})
$$
where $\mathrm{d}\chi^2 = \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}^2 /(1 - \mathbf{r}^2)$ is the "trigonometric" radial coordinate on the 3-sphere (see Problem 19.5). From Problem 19.18 we see that the universe lives an interval $\Delta \eta = 2\pi$ (time between two zeros of $\mathbb{R}$ ), so in this time a photon can propagate $\Delta \chi = 2\pi$ , i.e. exactly once around the universe.
