Problem 17.19. Calculate the Gaussian curvature of the horizon of a Kerr black hole and show that it becomes negative for $a > 3^{\frac{1}{2}}\mathbf{M} / 2$ . (This shows that the horizon cannot be globally embedded in a Euclidean 3-space if $a > 3^{\frac{1}{2}}\mathbf{M} / 2$ .) Use the Gauss-Bonnet theorem to check that the horizon is topologically a 2-sphere.

## Solution

Solution 17.19. In Boyer-Lindquist coordinates, for $t =$ constant and $r = r_+$ , the Kerr metric gives the metric of the horizon:
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = \left(\mathrm {r} _ {+} ^ {2} + \mathrm {a} ^ {2} \cos^ {2} \theta\right) \mathrm {d} \theta^ {2} + \left(2 \mathrm {M r} _ {+}\right) ^ {2} \sin^ {2} \theta \left(\mathrm {r} _ {+} ^ {2} + \mathrm {a} ^ {2} \cos^ {2} \theta\right) ^ {- 1} \mathrm {d} \phi^ {2}. \tag {1}
$$
(We have used the relation $\mathbf{r}_+^2 + \mathbf{a}^2 = 2\mathbf{M}\mathbf{r}_+$ in $\mathbf{g}_{\phi \phi}$ .) The Gaussian curvature $K$ of a 2-surface is the same as the Riemannian curvature of Problem 9.23, where the Riemann tensor is computed from the
2-dimensional metric. It is convenient to use an orthonormal basis dyad. For a metric of the form
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = \mathrm {e} ^ {2 a _ {1}} \left(\mathrm {d x} ^ {1}\right) ^ {2} + \mathrm {e} ^ {2 a _ {2}} \left(\mathrm {d x} ^ {2}\right) ^ {2} \tag {2}
$$
we let
$$
\tilde {\boldsymbol {\omega }} ^ {\hat {1}} = \mathrm {e} ^ {\alpha_ {1}} \widetilde {\mathrm {d x} } ^ {1}, \quad \tilde {\boldsymbol {\omega }} ^ {\hat {2}} = \mathrm {e} ^ {\alpha_ {2}} \widetilde {\mathrm {d x} } ^ {2} \tag {3}
$$
and we have
$$
\mathrm {K} = \mathrm {R} _ {\hat {1} \hat {2} \hat {1} \hat {2}} = \mathrm {R} _ {1 2 1 2} \mathrm {e} ^ {- 2 \left(\alpha_ {1} + \alpha_ {2}\right)}. \tag {4}
$$
The quickest way to find the Riemann tensor is with forms as in Problems 8.27 and 9.20. Since
$$
\mathbf {d} \widetilde {\boldsymbol {\omega }} ^ {\widehat {1}} = \mathrm {e} ^ {\alpha_ {1}} \widetilde {\mathbf {d}} _ {\alpha_ {1}} \wedge \widetilde {\mathbf {d x} } ^ {1} = \mathrm {e} ^ {\alpha_ {1}} \alpha_ {1, 2} \widetilde {\mathbf {d x} } ^ {2} \wedge \widetilde {\mathbf {d x} } ^ {1} = \alpha_ {1, 2} \mathrm {e} ^ {- \alpha_ {2}} \widetilde {\boldsymbol {\omega }} ^ {\widehat {2}} \wedge \widetilde {\boldsymbol {\omega }} ^ {\widehat {1}}
$$
and a similar expression with 1 and 2 interchanged, we find
$$
\tilde {\boldsymbol {\omega }} _ {\hat {2}} ^ {\hat {1}} = \mathrm {e} ^ {- a _ {2}} a _ {1, 2} \tilde {\boldsymbol {\omega }} ^ {\hat {1}} - \mathrm {e} ^ {- a _ {1}} a _ {2, 1} \tilde {\boldsymbol {\omega }} ^ {\hat {2}}. \tag {5}
$$
(Recall that $\mathbf{d}\tilde{\pmb{\omega}}^{\hat{\alpha}} = -\tilde{\pmb{\omega}}^{\hat{\alpha}}\hat{\beta} \wedge \tilde{\pmb{\omega}}^{\hat{\beta}}$ and $\tilde{\pmb{\omega}}_{\hat{\alpha}}\hat{\beta} = -\tilde{\pmb{\omega}}_{\hat{\beta}}\hat{\alpha}$ .) The only nontrivial curvature form is
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathcal {R} _ {\widehat {\widehat {\mathbf {2}}}} ^ {\widehat {\widehat {\mathbf {1}}}} = \mathbf {d} \tilde {\boldsymbol {\omega }} _ {\widehat {\widehat {\mathbf {2}}}} ^ {\widehat {\widehat {\mathbf {1}}}} + \tilde {\boldsymbol {\omega }} _ {\widehat {\widehat {\boldsymbol {\alpha}}}} ^ {\widehat {\widehat {\mathbf {1}}}} \wedge \tilde {\boldsymbol {\omega }} _ {\widehat {\widehat {\mathbf {2}}}} ^ {\widehat {\widehat {\boldsymbol {\alpha}}}} \\ = \mathrm {d} \left(a _ {1, 2} \mathrm {e} ^ {a _ {1} - a _ {2}} \widetilde {\mathrm {d x} } ^ {1} - a _ {2, 1} \mathrm {e} ^ {a _ {2} - a _ {1}} \widetilde {\mathrm {d x} } ^ {2}\right) + 0 \\ = \left. \left(a _ {1, 2} e ^ {a _ {1} - a _ {2}}\right) _ {, 2} \widetilde {d x} ^ {2} \wedge \widetilde {d x} ^ {1} - \left(a _ {2, 1} e ^ {a _ {2} - a _ {1}}\right) _ {, 1} \widetilde {d x} ^ {1} \wedge \widetilde {d x} ^ {2} \right. \\ = - \mathrm {e} ^ {- (\alpha_ {1} + \alpha_ {2})} \left[ \left(\mathrm {e} _ {, 1} ^ {\alpha_ {2}} \mathrm {e} ^ {- \alpha_ {1}}\right) _ {, 1} + \left(\mathrm {e} _ {, 2} ^ {\alpha_ {1}} \mathrm {e} ^ {- \alpha_ {2}}\right) _ {, 2} \right] \tilde {\omega} ^ {\hat {1}} \wedge \tilde {\omega} ^ {\hat {2}}. \\\ \end{array}
$$
Since $\mathcal{R}_{\hat{1}\hat{2}} = \mathbf{R}_{\hat{1}\hat{2}\hat{1}\hat{2}}\tilde{\pmb{\omega}}^{\hat{1}}\wedge \tilde{\pmb{\omega}}^{\hat{2}}$ we have
$$
\mathrm {K} = \mathrm {R} _ {\hat {1} \hat {2} \hat {1} \hat {2}} = - \mathrm {e} ^ {- \left(\alpha_ {1} + \alpha_ {2}\right)} \left[ \left(\mathrm {e} _ {, 1} ^ {\alpha_ {2}} \mathrm {e} ^ {- \alpha_ {1}}\right) _ {, 1} + \left(\mathrm {e} _ {, 2} ^ {\alpha_ {1}} \mathrm {e} ^ {- \alpha_ {2}}\right) _ {, 2} \right]. \tag {6}
$$
Using the explicit form of the metric in Equation (1), with $\theta = \mathbf{x}^1$ and $\phi = \mathbf{x}^2$ , we find from Equation (6) that
$$
K = 2 M r _ {+} \frac {r _ {+} ^ {2} - 3 a ^ {2} \cos^ {2} \theta}{\left(r _ {+} ^ {2} + a ^ {2} \cos^ {2} \theta\right) ^ {3}}. \tag {7}
$$
Note that if $r_+^2 - 3a^2 < 0$ , then $K$ is negative for a range of $\theta$ around the poles $(\theta = 0, \pi)$ . But when $K$ is negative, the surface cannot be globally embedded in a Euclidean 3-space. Since $r_+ = M + (M^2 - a^2)^{\frac{1}{2}}$ , the condition that $r_+^2 - 3a^2$ be less than zero is equivalent to $a > 3^{\frac{1}{2}}M / 2$ .
The Gauss-Bonnet theorem says that
$$
\int \mathrm {K d} ^ {2} \mathrm {S} = 2 \pi \chi
$$
where $\chi$ is the Euler characteristic of the surface ( $\chi = 2$ for a sphere, 0 for a torus, etc.). Since
$$
\mathrm {d} ^ {2} \mathrm {S} = (\mathrm {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \mathrm {d} \theta \mathrm {d} \phi = 2 \mathrm {M r} _ {+} \sin \theta \mathrm {d} \theta \mathrm {d} \phi
$$
we have
$$
\begin{array}{l} \chi = (2 \pi) ^ {- 1} \int_ {0} ^ {2 \pi} d \phi \int_ {0} ^ {\pi} (2 M r _ {+}) ^ {2} \frac {r _ {+} ^ {2} - 3 a ^ {2} \cos^ {2} \theta}{\left(r _ {+} ^ {2} + a ^ {2} \cos^ {2} \theta\right) ^ {3}} \sin \theta d \theta \\ = (2 \mathrm {M r} _ {+}) ^ {2} 2 \int_ {0} ^ {1} \frac {\mathrm {r} _ {+} ^ {2} - 3 a ^ {2} x ^ {2}}{\left(\mathrm {r} _ {+} ^ {2} + a ^ {2} x ^ {2}\right) ^ {3}} d x \\ = (2 \mathrm {M r} _ {+}) ^ {2} 2 \left[ \frac {\mathrm {x}}{\left(\mathrm {r} _ {+} ^ {2} + \mathrm {a} ^ {2} \mathrm {x} ^ {2}\right) ^ {2}} \right] _ {0} ^ {1} \\ = 2 \\\ \end{array}
$$
so the surface is topologically a 2-sphere. (For further details see L. Smarr, Phys. Rev. D. 7, 289, (1973).)
