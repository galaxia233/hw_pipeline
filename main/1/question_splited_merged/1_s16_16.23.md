Problem 16.23. Consider a stationary, axisymmetric star. There are two Killing vectors, $\pmb{\xi}_{(t)}$ and $\pmb{\xi}_{(\phi)}$ . Show that
$$
\mathsf {M} = - \int (2 \mathrm {T} _ {\nu} ^ {\mu} - \delta_ {\nu} ^ {\mu} \mathrm {T}) \boldsymbol {\xi} _ {\mathrm {(t)}} ^ {\nu} \mathrm {d} ^ {3} \Sigma_ {\mu}
$$
is the mass of the star as measured from infinity. Here $\mathrm{d}^3\Sigma_\mu$ is the volume element of the star at some instant of time $t$ (the time coordinate $t$ is chosen such that $\xi_{(t)} = \partial/\partial t$ ). Similarly, show that
$$
\mathrm {J} = \int \mathrm {T} _ {\nu} ^ {\mu} \xi_ {(\phi)} ^ {\nu} \mathrm {d} ^ {3} \Sigma_ {\mu}
$$
is the angular momentum as measured from infinity.

## Solution

$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = - \left(1 - \frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {r}}\right) \mathrm {d t} ^ {2} - \frac {4 \mathrm {J} \sin^ {2} \theta}{\mathrm {r}} \mathrm {d t} \mathrm {d} \phi + \left(1 + \frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {r}}\right) \left(\mathrm {d r} ^ {2} + \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \mathrm {d} \theta^ {2} + \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \sin^ {2} \theta \mathrm {d} \phi^ {2}\right).
$$
Since the only nonzero component of $\pmb{\xi}_{(t)}$ is $\pmb{\xi}^{\mathrm{t}} = 1$
$$
\begin{array}{l} \xi^ {t; r} \approx \xi_ {r} ^ {t} \\ = \xi_ {, r} ^ {t} + \Gamma_ {a r} ^ {t} \xi^ {\alpha} = 0 + \Gamma_ {t r} ^ {t} \\ \approx - \frac {1}{2} g _ {t t, r} = M / r ^ {2}. \\ \end{array}
$$
For the mass integral then
$$
I = (8 \pi) ^ {- 1} \int \frac {2 M}{r ^ {2}} r ^ {2} \sin \theta d \theta d \phi = M
$$
as required. Similarly, we have
$$
\begin{array}{l} S = \int T _ {\nu} ^ {\mu} \xi_ {(\phi)} ^ {\nu} d ^ {3} \Sigma_ {\mu} \\ = (8 \pi) ^ {- 1} \int \mathrm {R} _ {\nu} ^ {\mu} \xi_ {(\phi)} ^ {\nu} \mathrm {d} ^ {3} \Sigma_ {\mu} - (1 6 \pi) ^ {- 1} \int \mathrm {R} \xi_ {(\phi)} ^ {\mu} \mathrm {d} ^ {3} \Sigma_ {\mu}. \\ \end{array}
$$
Since $\mathrm{d}^3 \Sigma_{\phi} = 0$ the second term vanishes and
$$
\begin{array}{l} S = - (8 \pi) ^ {- 1} \int \xi^ {\mu ; \nu} _ {; \nu} d ^ {3} \Sigma_ {\mu} - 0 = - (1 6 \pi) ^ {- 1} \int \xi^ {\mu ; \nu} d ^ {2} \Sigma_ {\mu \nu} \\ = - (1 6 \pi) ^ {- 1} \oint 2 \xi^ {t; r} r ^ {2} \sin \theta d \theta d \phi . \\ \end{array}
$$
But
$$
\begin{array}{l} \xi^ {t; r} \approx \xi_ {, r} ^ {t} + \Gamma_ {a r} ^ {t} \xi^ {a} = 0 + \Gamma_ {\phi_ {r}} ^ {t} \\ = \mathbf {g} ^ {\mathrm {t t}} \Gamma_ {\mathbf {t} \phi \mathbf {r}} + \mathbf {g} ^ {\mathrm {t} \phi} \Gamma_ {\phi \phi \mathbf {r}} \\ \approx - \frac {1}{2} \mathrm {g} _ {\mathrm {t}} \phi , \mathrm {r} + \left(\frac {- 2 \mathrm {J}}{\mathrm {r} ^ {3}}\right) \frac {1}{2} \mathrm {g} _ {\phi \phi , \mathrm {r}} \\ = - 3 J \sin^ {2} \theta / r ^ {2}, \\ \end{array}
$$
and thus
$$
S = (1 6 \pi) ^ {- 1} \int 6 J \sin^ {3} \theta d \theta d \phi = J.
$$
