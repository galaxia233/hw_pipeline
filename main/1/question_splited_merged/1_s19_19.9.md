Problem 19.9. In a Robertson-Walker metric show that angular diameter distance $(\mathbf{d}_{\mathbf{A}})$ , luminosity distance $(\mathbf{d}_{\mathbf{L}})$ and proper motion distance $(\mathbf{d}_{\mathbf{M}})$ are related by
$$
(1 + z) ^ {2} \mathrm {d} _ {\mathbf {A}} = (1 + z) \mathrm {d} _ {\mathbf {M}} = \mathrm {d} _ {\mathbf {L}}.
$$

## Solution

Solution 19.9. If $D$ is the physical size of an object, and $\delta$ is its angle subtended, $d_A \equiv D / \delta$ . From the figure, and the Robertson-Walker metric, we see that $D = R(t)r_1\delta$ , so $d_A = r_1R(t_1)$ . If the object is moving transversely at a proper velocity $V$ and with an apparent angular motion

$\mathrm{d}\delta /\mathrm{d}t$ , then $\mathbf{d}_{\mathbf{M}}\equiv \mathbf{V} / (\mathbf{d}\delta /\mathbf{d}t)$ . Let $t^\prime$ denote time measured at the emission of photons. Since $\mathrm{V} = \mathrm{d}(\mathrm{R}(t_1)\mathrm{r}_1\delta) / \mathrm{dt}'$ , and since $\mathrm{dt}' / \mathrm{dt} = \mathrm{R}(t_1) / \mathrm{R}_0$ because of the cosmological redshift, and noting that $\mathbb{R}(t_1)$ can be considered constant since its change induces no transverse motion, we get $\mathbf{d}_{\mathbf{M}} = \mathbf{R}_{0}\mathbf{r}_{1}$ . If the object has an intrinsic luminosity L, and we receive a flux $\ell$ , then $\mathtt{d_L}\equiv (\mathtt{L} / 4\pi \ell)^{\frac{1}{2}}$ . In a time dt' it emits an energy Ldt'. This energy is redshifted to the present by a factor $\mathbb{R}(t_1) / \mathbb{R}_0$ , and is now distributed over a sphere of proper area $4\pi (\mathfrak{r}_1\mathbb{R}_0)^2$ (see figure). Thus, $\ell = (\mathrm{Ldt'}\mathrm{R} / \mathrm{R}_0)(4\pi \mathrm{r}_1\mathrm{R}_0)^{-2} / \mathrm{dt}$ and $\mathtt{d}_{\mathtt{L}} = \mathtt{R}_{0}^{2}\mathtt{r}_{1} / \mathtt{R}(\mathtt{t}_{1})$ . Using $\mathbb{R}_0 / \mathbb{R}(\mathfrak{t}_1) =$ $1 + z$ , we have now obtained $(1 + z)^2\mathtt{d}_{\mathtt{A}} = (1 + z)\mathtt{d}_{\mathtt{M}} = \mathtt{d}_{\mathtt{L}}$
