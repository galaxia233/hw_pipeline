Problem 13.16. In linearized theory a plane gravitational wave propagating in empty spacetime can be represented as the real part of a complex expression
$$
\overline {{\mathrm {h}}} _ {\mu \nu} = \operatorname {R e} \left[ \mathrm {A} _ {\mu \nu} \mathrm {e} ^ {\mathrm {i k} _ {\alpha} \mathrm {x} ^ {\alpha}} \right]
$$
where $\mathbf{A}_{\mu \nu}$ is a constant tensor. Show that $\mathbf{k}$ must be a null vector, and that $\mathbf{A}$ is orthogonal to $\mathbf{k}$ .
For a particular observer with 4-velocity $\mathbf{u}$ the "transverse-traceless" gauge (a further specialization of the Lorentz gauge) is defined in the observer's unperturbed rest frame ( $u^0 = 1$ , $u^i = 0$ ) such that
$$
\overline {{{\mathbf {h}}}} _ {\mu 0} = 0, \qquad \overline {{{\mathbf {h}}}} _ {\mu} ^ {\mu} = 0.
$$
Find the gauge transformation which accomplishes this. Does A remain orthogonal to k?

## Solution

Solution 13.16. For $\overline{\mathbf{h}}_{\mu \nu}$ to satisfy the Lorentz gauge condition (see Problem 13.14), and the linearized field equations, we must have
$$
\begin{array}{l} 0 = \overline {{\mathrm {h}}} _ {\mu \nu} ^ {\nu} = \operatorname {R e} \left[ \mathrm {i k} ^ {\nu} \mathrm {A} _ {\mu \nu} \mathrm {e} ^ {\mathrm {i k} _ {\alpha} \mathrm {x} ^ {\alpha}} \right] \\ 0 = \overline {{\mathrm {h}}} _ {\mu \nu , \sigma} ^ {\prime \sigma} = \operatorname {R e} \left[ - \mathrm {k} _ {\sigma} \mathrm {k} ^ {\sigma} \mathrm {A} _ {\mu \nu} \mathrm {e} ^ {\mathrm {i k} _ {\alpha} \mathrm {x} ^ {\alpha}} \right]. \\ \end{array}
$$
So the 4-vector $\mathbf{k}$ must be null and must be orthogonal to $\mathbf{A}$ .
The gauge transformation of $\overline{\mathfrak{h}}_{\mu \nu}$ is given in Problem 13.14 as
$$
\overline {{\mathsf {h}}} _ {\mu \nu} ^ {\mathsf {n e w}} = \overline {{\mathsf {h}}} _ {\mu \nu} ^ {\mathsf {o l d}} - 2 \xi_ {(\mu , \nu)} + \eta_ {\mu \nu} \xi_ {, \sigma} ^ {\sigma}.
$$
Clearly the gauge transformation must be of a plane wave $\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\mathbf{k}_{\alpha}x^{\alpha}}$ form. With $\xi_{\mu} = -\mathrm{iC}_{\mu}\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\mathbf{k}_{\alpha}x^{\alpha}}$ we have
$$
\mathbf {A} _ {\mu \nu} ^ {\mathrm {n e w}} = \mathbf {A} _ {\mu \nu} ^ {\mathrm {o l d}} - (\mathbf {C} _ {\mu} \mathbf {k} _ {\nu} + \mathbf {C} _ {\nu} \mathbf {k} _ {\mu}) + \eta_ {\mu \nu} \mathbf {C} ^ {\alpha} \mathbf {k} _ {\alpha}.
$$
If this equation is contracted with $\mathbf{k}^{\mu}$ we see that
$$
A _ {\mu \nu} ^ {n e w} k ^ {\mu} = - k ^ {\nu} C _ {\mu} k ^ {\mu} + k ^ {\nu} C ^ {a} k _ {a} = 0
$$
so A remains orthogonal to k. We can now apply the transversetraceless condition, $(\mathbf{A}^{\mathrm{new}})_{\mu}^{\mu} = 0$ and $A_{\mu 0}^{\mathrm{new}} = 0$ , to our equation to get
$$
\begin{array}{l} 0 = \left(\mathbf {A} ^ {\circ 1 \mathrm {d}}\right) _ {\mu} ^ {\mu} + 2 \mathbf {C} \cdot \mathbf {k} \\ 0 = A _ {\mu 0} ^ {o l d} - \left(C _ {0} k _ {\mu} + C _ {\mu} k _ {0}\right) + \eta_ {\mu 0} C \cdot k. \\ \end{array}
$$
These can be combined into
$$
0 = \mathbf {A} _ {\mu 0} ^ {\mathrm {o l d}} - (\mathbf {C} _ {0} \mathbf {k} _ {\mu} + \mathbf {C} _ {\mu} \mathbf {k} _ {0}) - \frac {1}{2} \eta_ {\mu 0} \mathbf {A} _ {\mu} ^ {\mathrm {o l d}} \mathbf {\Gamma} _ {\mu} ^ {\mu}.
$$
Taking $\mu = 0$ we have $\mathbf{C}_0 = (2\mathbf{k}_0)^{-1}\left(\mathbf{A}_{00}^{old} + \frac{1}{2} (\mathbf{A}^{old})_\mu^\mu\right)$ . The spatial components are then easily found from
$$
0 = A _ {i 0} ^ {\mathrm {o l d}} - \left(C _ {0} k _ {i} + C _ {i} k _ {0}\right).
$$
