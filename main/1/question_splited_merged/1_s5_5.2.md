Problem 5.2. What is the stress energy of a gas with a proper number density (i.e. number density as measured in the local rest frame of the gas) $\mathbf{N}$ of noninteracting particles of mass $m$ , if the particles all have the same speed $v$ but move isotropically? (Do not assume $v << c$ .)

## Solution

Solution 5.2. In Problem 5.1 we saw that for particles with 4-velocity $\mathbf{u}$ , the stress-energy is proportional to $\mathbf{u} \otimes \mathbf{u}$ . For an isotropic collection with $\mathbf{u} = \gamma (1, \mathrm{v} \underline{\mathrm{n}})$ we have
$$
\mathbf {T} = \kappa <   \mathbf {u} \otimes \mathbf {u} >, \quad \kappa = \text {s o m e c o n s t a n t},
$$
where the average is taken over the orientations of $\underline{\mathfrak{n}}$ . By symmetry, off-diagonal components average to zero:
$$
T ^ {0 i} = \kappa \gamma^ {2} v <   n ^ {i} > = 0
$$
$$
\mathrm {T} ^ {\mathrm {i j}} = \kappa \gamma^ {2} \mathrm {v} ^ {2} <   \mathrm {n} ^ {\mathrm {i}} \mathrm {n} ^ {\mathrm {j}} > = 0, \quad \mathrm {i} \neq \mathrm {j}.
$$
To evaluate the diagonal, spatial, components note that $< \mathfrak{n}^{\mathbf{x}}\mathfrak{n}^{\mathbf{x}} > = < \mathfrak{n}^{\mathbf{y}}\mathfrak{n}^{\mathbf{y}} > = < \mathfrak{n}^{\mathbf{z}}\mathfrak{n}^{\mathbf{z}} >$ and that
$$
<   \mathbf {n} ^ {\mathbf {x}} \mathbf {n} ^ {\mathbf {x}} > + <   \mathbf {n} ^ {\mathbf {y}} \mathbf {n} ^ {\mathbf {y}} > + <   \mathbf {n} ^ {\mathbf {z}} \mathbf {n} ^ {\mathbf {z}} > = <   \mathbf {n} ^ {2} > = 1
$$
so that
$$
<   n ^ {i} n ^ {i} > = \frac {1}{3} \quad T ^ {i i} = \kappa \gamma^ {2} v ^ {2} / 3.
$$
We know that the moving particles each have energy my so the density of mass energy must be
$$
\mathrm {m} \gamma \mathrm {N} = \mathrm {T} ^ {0 0} = \kappa \gamma^ {2} <   1 > = \kappa \gamma^ {2}.
$$
Thus $\kappa = \mathbf{mN} / \gamma$ and the nonvanishing components are
$$
\mathbf {T} ^ {0 0} = \mathrm {m y N} \quad \mathbf {T} ^ {\mathrm {i j}} = (\mathrm {m y N v} ^ {2} / 3) \delta^ {\mathrm {i j}}.
$$
For photons $\mathbf{v} \rightarrow \mathbf{c}$ , $\mathfrak{m}\gamma \rightarrow \mathfrak{h}\nu$ . For cold dust $\mathbf{v} = 0$ so $\mathbf{T}^{00} = \mathfrak{m}\mathbf{N}$ is the only nonvanishing component.
