Problem 19.6. Show that the spacelike 3-surfaces of a closed, isotropic, homogeneous universe possess a translation symmetry which leaves no points fixed. (Notice that this is not true in 2 dimensions; a 2 sphere cannot be combed smooth!)

## Solution

Solution 19.6. From the Robertson-Walker metric (in "trigonometric" form), the metric for the spacelike 3-surfaces is
$$
\mathrm {d} \sigma^ {2} = \mathrm {a} ^ {2} \left[ \mathrm {d} \chi^ {2} + \sin^ {2} \chi \left(\mathrm {d} \theta^ {2} + \sin^ {2} \theta \mathrm {d} \phi^ {2}\right) \right].
$$
By analogy with the metric for 2-spheres, it is easy to guess that this metric can be represented as a 3-sphere embedded in a Euclidean 4-space with cartesian coordinates $W$ , $X$ , $Y$ , $Z$ . In particular, if we define
$$
\begin{array}{l} W = a \cos x \\ Z = a \sin \chi \cos \theta \\ \mathbf {X} = \mathbf {a} \sin \chi \sin \theta \cos \phi \\ \mathbf {Y} = \mathbf {a} \sin \chi \sin \theta \sin \phi \\ \end{array}
$$
(which satisfy $\mathbf{W}^2 +\mathbf{Z}^2 +\mathbf{X}^2 +\mathbf{Y}^2 = \mathbf{A}^2)$ , the metric becomes
$$
\mathrm {d} \sigma^ {2} = \mathrm {d} W ^ {2} + \mathrm {d} Z ^ {2} + \mathrm {d} X ^ {2} + \mathrm {d} Y ^ {2}.
$$
In Euclidean 4-space one can find two planes (e.g. X-Y and W-Z) which intersect only at the origin $(a = 0)$ . Thus, a rotation of the X-Y plane changes all X and Y coordinates except the origin and leaves all W-Z coordinates fixed; and vice versa for W-Z rotations. Therefore, a combination of both rotations leaves no points fixed except the
origin and (for nonzero a) takes all points of the 3-sphere into different points.
