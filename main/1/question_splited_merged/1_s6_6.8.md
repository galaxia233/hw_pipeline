Problem 6.8. One can put a metric on the velocity space of a particle by defining the distance between two nearby velocities as their relative velocity. Show that the metric can be written in the form
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = \mathrm {d} \chi^ {2} + \sinh^ {2} \chi (\mathrm {d} \theta^ {2} + \sin^ {2} \theta \mathrm {d} \phi^ {2}).
$$
where the magnitude of the velocity is $\mathbf{v} = \tanh \chi$ .

## Solution

Solution 6.8. The relative velocity $\mathrm{ds}$ between two velocities $\underline{\mathbf{v}}$ and $\underline{\mathbf{v}} + \mathrm{dy}$ is (by Problem 1.3) given by
$$
d s ^ {2} = \frac {(d v) ^ {2} - (v \times d v) ^ {2}}{(1 - v ^ {2}) ^ {2}},
$$
and
$$
\left(\underline {{v}} \times d \underline {{v}}\right) ^ {2} = v ^ {2} \left(d \underline {{v}}\right) ^ {2} - \left(\underline {{v}} \cdot d \underline {{v}}\right) ^ {2}.
$$
Let $\theta$ and $\phi$ be polar and azimuthal angles about the direction of $\underline{\mathbf{v}}$ (i.e. $\mathbf{v}_{\mathbf{z}} = \mathbf{v}\cos \theta, \mathbf{v}_{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{v}\sin \theta\cos \phi, \mathbf{v}_{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{v}\sin \theta\sin \phi$ ). Then
$$
\begin{array}{l} \left(\mathrm {d} \underline {{v}}\right) ^ {2} = (\mathrm {d} v) ^ {2} + v ^ {2} \left(\mathrm {d} \theta^ {2} + \sin^ {2} \theta \mathrm {d} \phi^ {2}\right) \\ \underline {{\mathbf {v}}} \cdot d \underline {{\mathbf {v}}} = \frac {1}{2} d (\underline {{\mathbf {v}}} \cdot \underline {{\mathbf {v}}}) = v d \mathbf {v} \\ \end{array}
$$
so
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = \frac {\mathrm {d v} ^ {2}}{\left(1 - \mathrm {v} ^ {2}\right) ^ {2}} + \frac {\mathrm {v} ^ {2}}{1 - \mathrm {v} ^ {2}} \left(\mathrm {d} \theta^ {2} + \sin^ {2} \theta \mathrm {d} \phi^ {2}\right).
$$
Introduce the "rapidity parameter" $\mathbf{v} = \tanh \chi$ . Then
$$
\mathrm {d} \mathbf {s} ^ {2} = \mathrm {d} \chi^ {2} + \sinh^ {2} \chi (\mathrm {d} \theta^ {2} + \sin^ {2} \theta \mathrm {d} \phi^ {2}).
$$
Note that for small $\mathbf{v}$ , $\sinh \chi \sim \chi \sim \mathbf{v}$ and velocity space is flat, as we expect it to be in the Newtonian limit.
