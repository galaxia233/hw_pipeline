Problem 4.6. Suppose that $\underline{\mathbf{E}}\cdot \underline{\mathbf{B}} = 0$ . Show that there is a Lorentz transformation which makes $\underline{\mathbf{E}} = 0$ if $\mathbf{B}^2 -\mathbf{E}^2 >0$ , or one that makes $\underline{\mathbf{B}} = 0$ if $\mathbf{B}^2 -\mathbf{E}^2 < 0$ . What if $\mathbf{B}^2 -\mathbf{E}^2 = 0$ in addition to $\underline{\mathbf{E}}\cdot \underline{\mathbf{B}} = 0$ ?

## Solution

Solution 4.6. If $\mathbf{E}^2 - \mathbf{B}^2 > 0$ perform a Lorentz transformation with $\underline{\mathbf{v}} = \underline{\mathbf{E}} \times \underline{\mathbf{B}} / \mathbf{E}^2$ to get $\underline{\mathbf{B}}' = \gamma (\underline{\mathbf{B}} - \underline{\mathbf{v}} \times \underline{\mathbf{E}}) = 0$ . The transformation is analogous for $\mathbf{E}^2 - \mathbf{B}^2 < 0$ and $\underline{\mathbf{v}} = -\underline{\mathbf{B}} \times \underline{\mathbf{E}} / \mathbf{B}^2$ . If $\mathbf{E}^2 - \mathbf{B}^2 = 0$ then clearly $|\mathbf{E}| = |\mathbf{B}|$ after any transformation. A transformation of the form (see
Problem 4.5) $\underline{\mathbf{v}} = \alpha (\underline{\mathbf{E}}\times \underline{\mathbf{B}})$ reduces the magnitude of $\underline{\mathbf{E}}$ or $\underline{\mathbf{B}}$ by a factor $\gamma (1 - \alpha \mathrm{E}^2) = \gamma (1 - \mathrm{v}) = [(1 - \mathrm{v}) / (1 + \mathrm{v})]^{\frac{1}{2}}$ . In the limit $\mathbf{v}\rightarrow 1$ the magnitude of $\underline{\mathbf{E}}$ and $\underline{\mathbf{B}}$ can be made arbitrarily small.
