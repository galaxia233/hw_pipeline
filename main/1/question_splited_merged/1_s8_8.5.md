Problem 8.5. The exterior derivative of a differential form $\Omega$ can be defined axiomatically by the following properties:
(i) If $\Omega$ is a p-form, $d\Omega$ is a $(p + 1)$ -form;   
(ii) $\mathrm{d}(\Omega_1 + \Omega_2) = \mathrm{d}\Omega_1 + \mathrm{d}\Omega_2;$   
(iii) For a zero form $f$ (scalar), $\widetilde{df}$ is defined by $<\widetilde{df}, v> = \nabla_v f$ for any $v$ ;   
(iv) $\mathbf{d}(\Omega_1\wedge \Omega_2) = \mathbf{d}\Omega_1\wedge \Omega_2 + (-1)^{\mathrm{p}}\Omega_1\wedge \mathbf{d}\Omega_2$ where $\Omega_{1}$ is a p-form;
[Note: If $\mathbf{p} = 0$ (i.e. f a scalar) this reads: $\widetilde{\mathbf{d}}(\mathbf{f}\Omega) = \widetilde{\mathbf{d}}\mathbf{f} \wedge \Omega + \mathbf{f}\widetilde{\mathbf{d}}\Omega$ .]
(v) $\mathrm{dd}\Omega = 0$ for any $\Omega$ .
An alternative definition notes that a p-form is a completely antisymmetric covariant tensor of rank $p$ , and defines the exterior derivative as the completely antisymmetrized covariant derivative. Show that these definitions are equivalent.

## Solution

Solution 8.5. We show that the two definitions are equivalent by showing that $\mathbf{d}\Omega$ has the same components in an arbitrary coordinate basis when calculated from either definition. Start with the definition in terms of the covariant derivative. Let the components of $\Omega$ be $\Omega_{\alpha \beta \dots \gamma} = \Omega_{[\alpha \beta \dots \gamma]}$ ( $\mathfrak{p}$ indices). Then the components of $\mathbf{d}\Omega$ are
$$
\begin{array}{l} \Omega_ {[ \alpha \beta \dots \gamma ; \delta ]} = \Omega_ {[ \alpha \beta \dots \gamma , \delta ]} - \Gamma_ {\epsilon} [ \alpha \delta \Omega_ {\beta} ^ {\epsilon} \dots \gamma ] \tag {1} \\ - \Gamma_ {\epsilon} [ \beta \delta \Omega_ {a} ^ {\epsilon} \dots \gamma - \dots - \Gamma_ {\epsilon} [ \gamma \delta \Omega_ {a} \beta \dots \epsilon ] = \Omega_ {[ a} \beta \dots \gamma , \delta ] \\ \end{array}
$$
(The $\Gamma$ terms vanish since $\Gamma_{\mu[\nu \sigma]} = 0$ in a coordinate basis.) Now start with
$$
\Omega = \Omega_ {\alpha} \beta \dots \gamma \widetilde {\mathrm {d x}} ^ {\alpha} \wedge \widetilde {\mathrm {d x}} ^ {\beta} \wedge \dots \widetilde {\mathrm {d x}} ^ {\gamma}.
$$
By property (ii), $\mathbf{d}$ does not disturb the summation convention, so
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathrm {d} \boldsymbol {\Omega} = (\mathrm {d} \Omega_ {\alpha \beta \dots \gamma}) \wedge (\widetilde {\mathrm {d x}} ^ {\alpha} \wedge \widetilde {\mathrm {d x}} ^ {\beta} \wedge \dots \wedge \widetilde {\mathrm {d x}} ^ {\gamma}) \tag {2} \\ + \Omega_ {\alpha \beta \dots \gamma} \mathrm {d} \left(\widetilde {\mathrm {d x}} ^ {\alpha} \wedge \widetilde {\mathrm {d x}} ^ {\beta} \wedge \dots \wedge \widetilde {\mathrm {d x}} ^ {\gamma}\right), \\ \end{array}
$$
where we have used property (iv) with $\mathfrak{p} = 0$ since $\Omega_{\alpha \beta \dots \gamma}$ is simply a function (zero form). Now from property (i) we know that
$$
\widetilde {\mathrm {d f}} = \mathrm {f} _ {, a} \widetilde {\mathrm {d x}} ^ {a}
$$
for any zero form $f$ . From property (iii), $<\widetilde{\mathrm{df}}, \partial/\partial x^{\beta}> = <f_{,a} \widetilde{\mathrm{dx}}^{a}, \partial/\partial x^{\beta}> = f_{,b}$ ; thus $\widetilde{\mathrm{d}\Omega}_{\alpha\beta\dots\gamma} = \Omega_{\alpha\beta\dots\gamma,\delta} \widetilde{\mathrm{dx}}^{\delta}$ . The second term in Equation (2) is zero: According to property (iv) it can be reduced to a sum of terms each of which contains a ddx, which vanishes by property (v). Thus Equation (2) becomes
$$
\mathbf {d} \boldsymbol {\Omega} = \Omega_ {\alpha \beta \dots \gamma , \delta} \widetilde {\mathbf {d x}} ^ {\delta} \wedge \widetilde {\mathbf {d x}} ^ {\alpha} \wedge \widetilde {\mathbf {d x}} ^ {\beta} \dots \wedge \widetilde {\mathbf {d x}} ^ {\gamma}.
$$
Since $\mathbf{d}\Omega$ is a $(p + 1)$ form (property (i)), and the wedge product is antisymmetric, the components of $\mathbf{d}\Omega$ are $\Omega_{[a\beta \dots \gamma ,\delta ]}$ as in Equation (1).
