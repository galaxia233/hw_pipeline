Problem 12.12. By examining the relative acceleration of a family of test-particle trajectories in Newtonian gravity and comparing with the Newtonian limit of the equation of geodesic deviation, derive the correspondence
$$
R _ {j 0 k 0} = \frac {\partial^ {2} \Phi}{\partial x ^ {j} \partial x ^ {k}}
$$
between the Newtonian potential and the Riemann tensor. (A Newtonian test particle is acted on only by gravity; a test particle in a relativistic theory of gravity follows a geodesic.)

## Solution

Solution 12.12. Let the Newtonian test-particle trajectories be
$$
\mathbf {x} ^ { \mathrm {j}} = \mathbf {x} ^ { \mathrm {j}} (t, n).
$$
Here $n$ is a parameter telling which trajectory we are considering. The vector
$$
\underline {{\mathbf {n}}} = \frac {\partial}{\partial \mathbf {n}} = \frac {\partial \mathbf {x} ^ { \mathbf {k}}}{\partial \mathbf {n}} \frac {\partial}{\partial \mathbf {x} ^ { \mathbf {k}}} = \mathbf {n} ^ { \mathbf {k}} \frac {\partial}{\partial \mathbf {x} ^ { \mathbf {k}}}
$$
is a connecting vector between neighboring trajectories. The relative acceleration of neighboring trajectories is
$$
\frac {\partial^ {2} \mathbf {n} ^ { \mathrm {j}}}{\partial t ^ {2}} = \frac {\partial^ {2}}{\partial t ^ {2}} \left(\frac {\partial \mathbf {x} ^ { \mathrm {j}}}{\partial \mathbf {n}}\right) = \frac {\partial}{\partial \mathbf {n}} \left(\frac {\partial^ {2} \mathbf {x} ^ { \mathrm {j}}}{\partial t ^ {2}}\right) = \frac {\partial}{\partial \mathbf {n}} \left(- \frac {\partial \boldsymbol {\Phi}}{\partial \mathbf {x} ^ { \mathrm {j}}}\right) = - \mathbf {n} ^ { \mathrm {k}} \frac {\partial}{\partial \mathbf {x} ^ { \mathrm {k}}} \left(\frac {\partial \boldsymbol {\Phi}}{\partial \mathbf {x} ^ { \mathrm {j}}}\right) = - \mathbf {n} ^ { \mathrm {k}} \frac {\partial^ {2} \boldsymbol {\Phi}}{\partial \mathbf {x} ^ { \mathrm {k}} \partial \mathbf {x} ^ { \mathrm {j}}},
$$
where we have used the equation of motion $\partial^2\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{j}} / \partial t^2 = -\partial \Phi /\partial \mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{j}}$ . The equation of geodesic deviation is
$$
\frac {\mathrm {D} ^ {2} \mathfrak {n} ^ {a}}{\mathrm {d} \tau^ {2}} = - \mathrm {R} _ { \beta \gamma \delta} ^ { a} \mathfrak {n} ^ { \gamma} \mathrm {u} ^ { \beta} \mathrm {u} ^ { \delta } .
$$
In the Newtonian limit (velocities $\sim \epsilon$ , gravitational fields $\sim \epsilon^2$ )
$$
u ^ { 0} = 1 + \mathcal { O} (\epsilon), \quad u ^ { j} = \mathcal { O} (\epsilon), \quad \Gamma_ { \beta \gamma} ^ { \alpha} = \mathcal { O} (\epsilon^ { 2})
$$
and $\mathfrak{n}$ , which connects events of equal proper time, connects events of equal coordinate time to $\mathcal{O}(\epsilon)$ . Thus, $\frac{\mathrm{D}}{\mathrm{d}\tau} = \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t} + \mathcal{O}(\epsilon)$ and so
$$
\frac {d ^ {2} n ^ {j}}{d t ^ {2}} = - R _ { 0 k 0} ^ { j} n ^ { k} + \mathcal { O} (\epsilon).
$$
The Riemann components are then
$$
R _ { 0 k 0} ^ { j} = \frac {\partial^ {2} \Phi}{\partial x ^ {k} \partial x ^ {j}}
$$
in the Newtonian limit. Note that, unless the velocities involved approach the speed of light, the other components of $\mathbf{R}_{\beta \gamma \delta}^{\alpha}$ do not enter into the equation of relative motion of test particles.
