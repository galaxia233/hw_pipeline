## Solution

Solution 3.1. The neatest way to do this problem is by using spacetime diagrams.
First rotate the coordinate system until both events lie on the $x$ -axis and the first event occurs at $x = t = 0$ .
The two events A and B at spacelike separation $(\Delta x, \Delta t)$ are depicted in Figure (i), with the world line of a light ray indicated as the dotted $45^{\circ}$ line. A Lorentz transformation, shown as the $t'$ , $x'$ axes in Figure (ii), can obviously be made to make A and B simultaneous. (The magnitude of the Lorentz boost is $\beta = \Delta t / \Delta x$ and $\tanh^{-1} \tan \theta$ in Figure (ii) is the rapidity parameter.) Since the $t'$ axis cannot be rotated below the light cone line in a Lorentz transformation, the two events cannot be made to occur at the same location.

Figure (iii) depicts events A and B at timelike separation. As shown in Figure (iv), a Lorentz transformation can obviously be made to bring A and B to the same location in the primed frame. The magnitude of the required boost is $\alpha = \Delta x / \Delta t$ . Since the $x'$ axis cannot be rotated above the light cone line, the two events cannot be made simultaneous.
