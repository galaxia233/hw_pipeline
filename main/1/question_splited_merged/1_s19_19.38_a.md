19.38 In cosmology one usually uses coordinates comoving with the galaxies. Let $(r, x^i)$ be such a system, and let the metric take the general form
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = - \mathrm {d} \tau^ {2} + 2 \mathrm {g} _ {0 \mathrm {i}} \mathrm {d} \tau \mathrm {d x } ^ {\mathrm {i}} + \mathrm {g} _ {\mathrm {i j}} \mathrm {d x } ^ {\mathrm {i}} \mathrm {d x } ^ {\mathrm {j}}
$$
where $\mathbf{g}_{0\mathrm{i}}$ and $\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{ij}}$ can be functions of $\pmb{\tau}$ and $\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{i}}$ . Show (a) that $\tau$ is the proper time measured by a galaxy;

## Solution

# Solution 19.38.
(a) A galaxy moves with $\mathbf{x}^{\mathbf{i}} =$ constant implying that $\mathrm{dx}^{\mathrm{i}} = 0$ and hence that $\mathrm{ds}^2 = -\mathrm{dr}^2$ .
(b) For $\mathrm{d}\tau = 0$ , $\mathrm{ds}^2 = \mathbf{g}_{ij} \mathrm{dx}^i \mathrm{dx}^j$ .
(c) If $\mathbf{g}_{0i}$ and $\mathbf{g}_{ij}$ are independent of $\mathbf{x}^i$ , then on constant $r$ hypersurfaces, nothing depends upon $\mathbf{x}^i$ ; the space is homogeneous. On the other hand, a bad choice of coordinates and hence a seemingly distorted spacelike hypersurface may cause the metric functions of a homogeneous space to depend upon position.
(d) In Problem 14.9 we showed that if the spatial connecting vector between any two nearby geodesics is of constant length, then $\sigma_{\alpha \beta} = \theta = 0$ for the geodesic field. If $g_{0i}$ and $g_{ij}$ are independent of $\tau$ , then the connecting vector will have constant length.
(e) Since $\mathbf{u}^0 = +1$ , $\mathbf{u}^i = 0$ , we have
$$
\begin{array}{l} u _ {i; j} = - u ^ {\gamma} \Gamma _ {\gamma i j} = - \Gamma _ {0 i j} = - \frac {1}{2} \left(g _ {i 0, j} + g _ {0 j, i} - g _ {i j, 0}\right) \tag {1} \\ = \frac {1}{2} g _ {i j, 0} = \frac {1}{2} f \bar {g} _ {i j}. \\ \end{array}
$$
$$
\begin{array}{l} \theta \equiv u ^ {a}; a = | g | ^ {- \frac {1}{2}} \left(| g | ^ {\frac {1}{2}} u ^ {a}\right), a = f ^ {- \frac {3}{2}} \left(f ^ {\frac {3}{2}}\right), 0 \tag {2} \\ = \frac {3}{2} \frac {\mathrm {f}}{\mathrm {f}}. \\ \end{array}
$$
Now, from Equations (1), (2), and the formula (see Problem 5.18)
$$
\sigma_ {\alpha ; \beta} = \frac {1}{2} \left(\mathrm {u} _ {\alpha ; \mu} \mathrm {P} ^ {\mu} _ {\beta} + \mathrm {u} _ {\beta ; \mu} \mathrm {P} ^ {\mu} _ {\alpha}\right) - \frac {1}{3} \theta \mathrm {P} _ {\alpha} \beta \tag {3}
$$
we arrive at
$$
\sigma_ {i j} = \frac {1}{2} \left(u _ {i; j} + u _ {j; i}\right) - \frac {1}{3} \theta g _ {i j} = \frac {1}{2} \dot {f} \bar {g} _ {i j} - \frac {1}{2} \dot {f} \bar {g} _ {i j} = 0. \tag {4}
$$
$$
a ^ {\alpha} = u ^ {\alpha}; \beta u ^ {\beta} = u ^ {\alpha}; 0 = \Gamma ^ {\alpha} _ {0 0} \tag {f}
$$
$$
\mathrm {a} _ {\alpha} = \Gamma _ {\alpha 0 0} = \frac {1}{2} \left(\mathrm {g} _ {\alpha 0, 0} + \mathrm {g} _ {\alpha 0, 0} - \mathrm {g} _ {0 0, \alpha}\right) = \mathrm {g} _ {0 \alpha , 0}.
$$
If $\mathbf{a}_{\alpha} = 0$ , then $\mathbf{g}_{0\alpha,0} = 0$ and galaxies fall on geodesics if and only if $\mathbf{g}_{0\mathbf{i},0} = 0$ .
(g) From Problem 7.23 we showed that the necessary condition for a family of hypersurfaces to be orthogonal to $\mathbf{u}$ is $\omega_{\alpha \beta} = 0$ . Since $\mathbf{u} = \partial/\partial \tau$ and the hypersurfaces of constant $\tau$ are spanned by $\partial/\partial \mathbf{x}^{\mathbf{i}}$ , then $\omega_{\alpha \beta} \neq 0$ implies that $(\partial/\partial t) \cdot (\partial/\partial \mathbf{x}^{\mathbf{i}}) \neq 0$ and hence that $\mathbf{g}_{0\mathbf{i}} \neq 0$ . If $\omega_{\alpha \beta,0} \neq 0$ , then $\mathbf{g}_{\alpha \beta,0} \neq 0$ and from part (f), this means that the galaxies do not fall on geodesics.
