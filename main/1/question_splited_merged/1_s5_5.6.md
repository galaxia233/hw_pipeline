Problem 5.6. A rope of mass per unit length $\mu$ has a static breaking strength $F$ . What is the maximum $F$ can be without violating the "weak" energy condition that $T^{00}$ should be positive to all observers? How close is steel cable to this theoretical maximum strength?

## Solution

Solution 5.6. Suppose a force infinitesimally smaller than $\mathbf{F}$ is applied. If the cross section is A and the axis of the rope is in the $z$ direction, the components of $\mathbf{T}$ in the rest frame of the rod are given in Problem 5.5. An observer moving with velocity $\mathbf{u} = (\gamma, \gamma \mathbf{v})$ sees energy density
$$
\mathbf {T} ^ {0 \prime 0 ^ {\prime}} = \mathbf {T} ^ {\alpha \beta} \mathbf {u} _ {\alpha} \mathbf {u} _ {\beta} = (\gamma^ {2} / \mathrm {A}) \left[ \mu - \mathbf {F v} _ {z} ^ {2} \right] .
$$
Clearly the observer with the greatest chance of seeing a negative $\mathbf{T}^{0'0'}$ is the one moving in the $z$ -direction with $v \rightarrow c$ . To guarantee that he sees a positive $\mathbf{T}^{0'0'}$ , the weak energy condition requires $F < \mu$ , which is the required upper limit. In physical units, and for good steel rope this would be
$$
\frac {\mathbf {F}}{\mathbf {A}} <   \frac {\mu}{\mathbf {A}} \mathbf {c} ^ {2} = \rho \mathbf {c} ^ {2} = 7 \times 1 0 ^ {1 3} \mathrm {K g - w t / m m} ^ {2}.
$$
as compared to its actual breaking strength of $\approx 200\mathrm{kg - wt / mm}^2$ . The reason for this difference by a factor of order $10^{11.5}$ is that the strength of steel comes essentially from the energy of the molecular bonds ( $\lesssim$ 1eV per atom), not from the total mass density, which is virtually all in the nucleus ( $\sim 5\times 10^{10}\mathrm{eV}$ ).
