Problem 14.12. An observer in a closed box (not necessarily in free fall) measures positions and times inside his box with rulers and a clock. Show that the equation of motion for a particle, correct to first order in its measured velocity $v << 1$ and position $x^j$ is
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathrm {d} \mathbf {v} ^ {\mathrm {j}} / \mathrm {d} t = 2 (\underline {{\mathbf {v}}} \times \underline {{\omega}}) ^ {\mathrm {j}} - [ (\underline {{\mathbf {x}}} \times \underline {{\omega}}) \times \underline {{\omega}} ] ^ {\mathrm {j}} + (\underline {{\mathbf {x}}} \times \underline {{\mathrm {d}}} \underline {{\omega}} / \mathrm {d} t) ^ {\mathrm {j}} \\ - \mathrm {a} ^ {\mathrm {j}} (1 + \underline {{\underline {{\mathrm {a}}}}} \cdot \underline {{\underline {{\mathrm {x}}}}}) - \mathrm {R} _ {0 \mathrm {k 0}} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \mathrm {x} ^ {\mathrm {k}}. \\ \end{array}
$$
Here $\underline{\omega}$ is the angular velocity of the box, $\underline{\mathbf{a}}$ its acceleration, and $\mathbb{R}^j_{0k0}$ the Riemann tensor evaluated at the origin.

## Solution

Solution 14.12. In geometrical language the observer sets up his local coordinates $\mathbf{x}^{\alpha}$ as follows: If $\mathbf{u}$ is the 4-velocity of the center of his box along its worldline, he chooses $\mathbf{e}_0 = \mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{e}_{\alpha} \cdot \mathbf{e}_{\beta} = \eta_{\alpha \beta}$ along his world line. From each point $\mathbf{P}(\tau)$ on his world-line ( $\tau =$ proper time), he sends out spatial geodesics orthogonal to $\mathbf{u}$ , with affine parameter equal to proper length $\mathbf{s}$ . If $\mathbf{n}$ is the tangent vector to the spatial geodesic through the point $\mathbf{P}$ near his world-line, then he assigns to $\mathbf{P}$ the coordinates $\mathbf{x}^0 = \tau$ , $\mathbf{x}^{\mathbf{k}} = \mathbf{sn}^{\mathbf{k}}$ .
The equation of motion is now
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} ^ {2} \mathbf {x} ^ {\alpha}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda^ {2}} + \Gamma^ {\alpha} \beta_ {\gamma} \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} ^ {\beta}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} ^ {\gamma}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} = 0 \tag {1}
$$
where we can take $\lambda$ to be the proper time of the particle. The 4-velocity of the particle is $\mathrm{dx}^{\alpha} / \mathrm{d}\lambda = (\gamma, \gamma \underline{\mathbf{v}})$ , where $\gamma = (1 - \mathbf{v}^{2})^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ . First change $\mathrm{d} / \mathrm{d}\lambda$ to $\mathrm{d} / \mathrm{d}t$ in Equation (1) using $\mathrm{d} / \mathrm{d}\lambda = \gamma \mathrm{d} / \mathrm{d}t$ :
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} ^ {2} \mathbf {x} ^ {\alpha}}{\mathrm {d t} ^ {2}} + \frac {1}{\gamma} \frac {\mathrm {d} \gamma}{\mathrm {d t}} \frac {\mathrm {d x} ^ {\alpha}}{\mathrm {d t}} + \Gamma^ {\alpha} \beta \gamma \frac {\mathrm {d x} ^ {\beta}}{\mathrm {d t}} \frac {\mathrm {d x} ^ {\gamma}}{\mathrm {d t}} = 0. \tag {2}
$$
Putting $a = 0$ , we get
$$
\frac {1}{\gamma} \frac {\mathrm {d} \gamma}{\mathrm {d} t} + \Gamma^ {0} _ {\beta \gamma} \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} ^ {\beta}}{\mathrm {d} t} \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} ^ {\gamma}}{\mathrm {d} t} = 0.
$$
Substitute this in Equation (2) and get, for $\alpha = j$
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {v} ^ {\mathrm {j}}}{\mathrm {d} t} + (- \mathbf {v} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \Gamma^ {0} _ {\beta \gamma} + \Gamma^ {\mathrm {j}} _ {\beta \gamma}) \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} ^ {\beta}}{\mathrm {d} t} \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} ^ {\gamma}}{\mathrm {d} t} = 0. \tag {3}
$$
To first order in $\mathbf{v}$ , Equation (3) is
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {v} ^ {\mathrm {j}}}{\mathrm {d} t} - \mathbf {v} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \Gamma_ {0 0} ^ {0} + \Gamma_ {0 0} ^ {\mathrm {j}} + 2 \Gamma_ {\mathrm {k} 0} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \mathbf {v} ^ {\mathrm {k}} = 0. \tag {4}
$$
Thus, to first order in $\underline{\mathbf{x}}$ and $\underline{\mathbf{y}}$ , we have
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathrm {v} ^ {\mathrm {j}}}{\mathrm {d} t} = \left. \mathrm {v} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \Gamma_ {0 0} ^ {\mid} \right| _ {\underset {\mathrm {x} = 0} {\mid}} - \left. \Gamma_ {0 0} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \right| _ {\underset {\mathrm {x} = 0} {\mid}} - \left. \mathrm {x} ^ {\mathrm {k}} \Gamma_ {0 0, \mathrm {k}} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \right| _ {\underset {\mathrm {x} = 0} {\mid}} - 2 \mathrm {v} ^ {\mathrm {k}} \Gamma_ {\mathrm {k} 0} ^ {\mathrm {j}} | _ {\underset {\mathrm {x} = 0} {\mid}}. \tag {5}
$$
The Christoffel symbols at $\underline{\mathbf{x}} = 0$ are found using a result of Problem 7.17:
$$
\Gamma_ {\alpha 0} ^ {\beta} \mathbf {e} _ {\beta} = \nabla_ {\mathbf {u}} \mathbf {e} _ {\alpha} = - \Omega_ {\alpha} ^ {\beta} \mathbf {e} _ {\beta}
$$
$$
\Gamma_ {\beta \alpha 0} = - \Omega_ {\beta \alpha} \equiv a _ {\alpha} u _ {\beta} - a _ {\beta} u _ {\alpha} + \varepsilon_ {\alpha \beta \lambda \sigma} u ^ {\lambda} \omega^ {\sigma}.
$$
Since $\mathbf{u}^{\alpha} = (1,\underline{0}),\mathbf{u}_{\alpha} = (-1,\underline{0}),\mathbf{a}_{\alpha} = (0,\underline{\mathbf{a}}),\boldsymbol{\omega}^{\sigma} = (0,\underline{\boldsymbol{\omega}})$ we get
$$
\Gamma_ {\beta a 0} = 0 \quad \text {i f} \quad \beta = a
$$
$$
\Gamma_ {0 j 0} = - \Gamma_ {j 0 0} = - a _ {j}
$$
$$
\Gamma_ {\mathrm {k j} 0} = - \Gamma_ {\mathrm {j k} 0} = \varepsilon_ {\mathrm {j k} 0 \mathrm {m}} \omega^ {\mathrm {m}} = \varepsilon_ {\mathrm {j k m}} \omega^ {\mathrm {m}} \tag {6}
$$
The $\Gamma$ 's with no 0-index are found from the condition that the coordinate lines $x^{\alpha} = (r, \mathfrak{sn}^{\mathbf{k}})$ , where $r$ and $\mathfrak{n}^{\mathbf{k}}$ are independent of $\mathbf{s}$ , are geodesics. Thus,
$$
\begin{array}{l} 0 = \frac {\mathrm {d} ^ {2} \mathbf {x} ^ {\alpha}}{\mathrm {d s} ^ {2}} + \Gamma^ {\alpha} \beta \gamma \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} ^ {\beta}}{\mathrm {d s}} \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} ^ {\gamma}}{\mathrm {d s}} \\ = 0 + \Gamma_ {j k} ^ {\alpha} n ^ {j} n ^ {k}. \\ \end{array}
$$
This is satisfied at $\underline{\mathbf{x}} = 0$ for arbitrary $\underline{\mathbf{n}}$ , so
$$
\Gamma_ {\alpha \mathrm {i k}} = 0. \tag {7}
$$
We find $\Gamma_{00,\mathbf{k}}^{\mathbf{j}}$ from the expression for the Riemann tensor:
$$
\mathsf {R} _ {\beta \gamma \delta} ^ {\alpha} = \Gamma_ {\beta \delta , \gamma} ^ {\alpha} - \Gamma_ {\beta \gamma , \delta} ^ {\alpha} + \Gamma_ {\gamma \mu} ^ {\alpha} \Gamma_ {\beta \delta} ^ {\mu} - \Gamma_ {\delta \mu} ^ {\alpha} \Gamma_ {\beta \gamma} ^ {\mu}.
$$
This gives
$$
\Gamma_ {0 0, \mathbf {k}} ^ {\mathrm {j}} = \mathsf {R} _ {0 \mathbf {k} 0} ^ {\mathrm {j}} + \Gamma_ {0 \mathbf {k}, 0} ^ {\mathrm {j}} - \Gamma_ {\mathbf {k} \mu} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \Gamma_ {0 0} ^ {\mu} + \Gamma_ {0 \mu} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \Gamma_ {0 \mathbf {k}} ^ {\mu}.
$$
At $\underline{\mathbf{x}} = 0$
$$
\Gamma_ {0 k, 0} ^ {j} = - \varepsilon_ {j k m} \omega^ {m},
$$
$$
\Gamma_ {\mathbf {k} \mu} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \Gamma_ {0 0} ^ {\mu} = 0
$$
$$
\begin{array}{l} \Gamma_ {0 \mu} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \Gamma_ {0 \mathrm {k}} ^ {\mu} = \Gamma_ {0 0} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \Gamma_ {0 \mathrm {k}} ^ {0} + \Gamma_ {0 \mathrm {m}} ^ {\mathrm {j}} \Gamma_ {0 \mathrm {k}} ^ {\mathrm {m}} \\ = a _ {j} a _ {k} + \varepsilon_ {\min } \omega^ {n} \varepsilon_ {k m 1} \omega^ {1}, \\ \end{array}
$$
and thus
$$
\begin{array}{l} \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {v} ^ {\mathrm {j}}}{\mathrm {d} t} = - a _ {\mathrm {j}} - \mathbf {x} ^ {\mathrm {k}} \left(\mathrm {R} _ {0 \mathrm {k} 0} ^ {\mathrm {j}} - \varepsilon_ {\mathrm {j k m}} \omega^ {\mathrm {m}} _ {\mathrm {, 0}} + a _ {\mathrm {j}} a _ {\mathrm {k}} + \varepsilon_ {\mathrm {m j n}} \omega^ {\mathrm {n}} \varepsilon_ {\mathrm {k m} 1} \omega^ {\mathrm {1}}\right) + 2 \varepsilon_ {\mathrm {j k m}} \omega^ {\mathrm {m}} \mathbf {v} ^ {\mathrm {k}} \\ = - a _ {j} \left(1 + \underline {{a}} \cdot \underline {{x}}\right) - R ^ {j} _ {0 k 0} x ^ {k} + \left(\underline {{x}} \times \underline {{\omega}}, _ {0}\right) ^ {j} - \left[ \left(\underline {{x}} \times \underline {{\omega}}\right) \times \underline {{\omega}} \right] ^ {j} + 2 \left(\underline {{v}} \times \underline {{\omega}}\right) ^ {j}. \\ \end{array}
$$
The first term is the “‘inertial force’” due to the acceleration of the reference frame. The $1 + \underline{a} \cdot \underline{x}$ is a relativistic correction factor (see MTW Exercise 37-4). The second term is the “‘true’” gravitational force. In the weak-field limit, a Newtonian physicist would separate $\underline{a}$ into a contribution from the local acceleration of gravity, $\partial \Phi / \partial x_{j}$ , plus an “absolute” acceleration $\underline{a}_{\mathrm{abs}}$ . Thus,
$$
\begin{array}{l} a _ {j} + R _ {0 k 0} ^ {j} x ^ {k} = \left(a _ {j}\right) _ {a b s} + \partial \Phi / \partial x ^ {j} | _ {\underline {{x}} = 0} + \partial^ {2} \Phi / \partial x ^ {j} \partial x ^ {k} | _ {\underline {{x}} = 0} x ^ {k} \\ = \left. \left(a _ {j}\right) _ {\text {a b s}} + \partial \Phi / \partial x ^ {j} \right| _ {\underset {\sim} {x} = x \underset {\sim} {p a r t i c l e}}. \\ \end{array}
$$
The terms involving $\omega$ are the same as in nonrelativistic mechanics; the second and third are the centrifugal and Coriolis forces respectively.
