Problem 12.10. From the result of Problem 12.9 show that the equation of hydrostatic equilibrium in a star is
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d p}}{\mathrm {d r}} = - \frac {\mathrm {G M} (\mathrm {r})}{\mathrm {r} ^ {2}} \left(\rho + \mathrm {p} / \mathrm {c} ^ {2}\right)
$$
where $\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{r})$ is defined as the "active" mass interior to the fluid shell at radius $r$ . This shows that in a fluid the "effective inertial mass" density is $\rho + p/c^2$ . Notice that this result does not depend on the field equations of general relativity.

## Solution

Solution 12.10. From the previous problem
$$
\sum \underset {\sim} {F} _ {i} \left(1 - \underset {\sim} {g} \cdot \underset {\sim} {x} _ {i} / c ^ {2}\right) = - M \underset {\sim} {g};
$$
If $p$ is pressure and $dA$ is directed surface area, then $F = pdA$ and the above result becomes
$$
- \int p (x) \left(1 - \underline {{g}} \cdot \underline {{x}} / c ^ {2}\right) d \underline {{A}} = - M g
$$
or
$$
- \int p d \underline {{A}} + \int p g \cdot \frac {\underline {{x}}}{c ^ {2}} d \underline {{A}} = - M g
$$
for $\underline{\mathbf{g}}$ constant. Now with $\underline{\mathbf{g}} = -\frac{\mathbf{GM}}{\mathbf{r}^2}\underline{\mathbf{e}}_{\mathbf{r}},\quad \underline{\mathbf{x}} = \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}\underline{\mathbf{e}}_{\mathbf{r}},\quad \mathrm{d}\underline{\mathbf{A}} = \pm \mathrm{d}\mathbf{A}\underline{\mathbf{e}}_{\mathbf{r}},$ we have
$$
- \mathrm {A} [ \mathrm {p} (\mathrm {r} + \mathrm {d r}) - \mathrm {p} (\mathrm {r}) ] \underline {{\mathrm {e}}} _ {\mathrm {r}} - \mathrm {d r} \frac {\mathrm {G M}}{\mathrm {c} ^ {2} \mathrm {r} ^ {2}} \underline {{\mathrm {e}}} _ {\mathrm {r}} \int \mathrm {p d A} = - \underline {{\mathrm {M g}}} = \mathrm {A d r} \rho \frac {\mathrm {G M}}{\mathrm {r} ^ {2}} \underline {{\mathrm {e}}} _ {\mathrm {r}}
$$
and thus
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {p}}{\mathrm {d} \mathbf {r}} = - \frac {\mathrm {G M}}{\mathrm {r} ^ {2}} \left(\rho + \frac {\mathrm {p}}{\mathrm {c} ^ {2}}\right).
$$
This result holds in any theory of gravity that satisfies $\mathbf{T}^{\mu \nu}$ ; $\nu = 0$ (such theories are called metric theories) and is independent of the field equations. Each theory, however, gives its own prescription for finding $\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{r})$ , which is defined such that the local acceleration of gravity is $\mathbf{g} = -\mathbf{GM} / \mathbf{r}^2$ .
