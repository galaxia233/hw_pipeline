Problem 5.1. Calculate the nonzero components in an inertial frame S of the stress-energy tensor for the following systems:
(b) A ring of $\mathbf{N}$ similar particles of mass $m$ rotating counterclockwise in the $x-y$ plane about some point fixed in $S$ at a radius $a$ and angular velocity $\omega$ . (The width of the ring is much less than $a$ .) Do not include the stress-energy of whatever forces keep them in orbit. Assume $\mathbf{N}$ is large enough that one can treat the particles as being continuously distributed.

## Solution

(b) Let the circle of motion be in the $x - y$ plane. Consider a continuum of rest mass density $\rho_0$ at the point $x = 0$ , $y = a$ moving with velocity $\beta = \omega a$ . From part (a), at $x = 0$ , $y = a$ we have $T^{00} = \rho_0 \gamma^2$ etc. Now if we consider a ring of matter, all points on the ring are equivalent so that in polar coordinates the nonzero components of $T^{\mu \nu}$ are
$$
\mathbf {T} ^ {0 0} = \rho_ {0} \gamma^ {2}, \quad \mathbf {T} ^ {0 \hat {\phi}} = \rho_ {0} \gamma^ {2} \beta = \mathbf {T} ^ {\hat {\phi} 0}, \quad \mathbf {T} ^ {\hat {\phi} \hat {\phi}} = \rho_ {0} \gamma^ {2} \beta^ {2} .
$$
We now must relate $\rho_0$ to the collection of particles. If we assume that $\mathbf{N}$ is large enough so that we can view the particles as continuously distributed around the ring, then $\rho_0 \propto \delta(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{a}) \delta(z)$ . The total energy of the particles must be $\gamma \mathbf{Nm}$ so
$$
\mathrm {T} ^ {0 0} 2 \pi \mathrm {r d r d z} = \gamma \mathrm {N m}
$$
and $\rho_0 = \mathrm{Nm}\delta (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{a})\delta (z) / 2\pi \mathrm{ay}$ . The results can now be expressed in cartesian coordinates by making the usual transformation $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{r}\cos \phi$ , $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{r}\sin \phi$ :
$$
\mathrm {T} ^ {\mu \nu} = \frac {\gamma \mathrm {N m} \delta ([ \mathrm {x} ^ {2} + \mathrm {y} ^ {2} ] ^ {\frac {1}{2}} - \mathrm {a}) \delta (\mathbf {z})}{2 \pi \mathrm {a}} \left[ \begin{array}{c c c} 1 & - \beta \sin \phi & \beta \cos \phi \\ & \beta^ {2} \sin^ {2} \phi & - \beta^ {2} \sin \phi \cos \phi \\ & & \beta^ {2} \cos^ {2} \phi \\ (\text {s y m m e t r i c}) & & 0 \end{array} \right]
$$
