# Problem 7.15.
(a) Show that the geodesics of the velocity space metric defined in Problem 6.8 are paths of minimum fuel use for a rocket ship changing its velocity.

## Solution

# Solution 7.15.
(a) The geodesic is the path between two velocities which minimizes the arc-length between them; but arc-length in the velocity space is just the magnitude of a small change of velocity. Since a rocket expends fuel monotonically for the boost it requires, the geodesics of velocity space are paths of minimum fuel use.
(b) We need to find the velocity geodesic connecting $\underline{\mathbf{V}}_1$ and $\underline{\mathbf{V}}_2$ . In principle, we could solve the geodesic equation for the metric in Problem 6.8, but this is very tedious. An easier way is to notice that, by symmetry, a geodesic passing through the origin of the velocity space coordinates is $\theta = \phi = \text{constant}$ , $\chi = s$ (where $s$ is an affine parameter). To get a more general geodesic let us view this geodesic from a moving frame. Since $\chi = \tanh^{-1}\overline{\mathbf{V}}$ is a monotonic function of $\rho = \gamma \overline{\mathbf{V}}$ [where $\gamma \equiv (1 - \overline{\mathbf{V}}^2)^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ ], we can write the geodesics in terms of the non-affine parameter $\rho(s)$ . In terms of this parameter $\gamma = (1 + \rho^2)^{\frac{1}{2}}$ and, if we choose the geodesic to be in the $x$ -direction,
$$
u = \left[ \left(1 + \rho^ {2}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}, \rho , 0, 0 \right] \quad - \infty <   \rho <   + \infty .
$$
A boost in the $x$ -direction just takes this trajectory onto itself so we need only find its transformation under a perpendicular boost, say in the $y$ -direction. Under a boost by $\beta$ in the $y$ -direction $u$ becomes:
$$
\mathbf {u} ^ {\prime} = \left[ \gamma^ {\prime} \left(1 + \rho^ {2}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}, \rho , \left(1 + \rho^ {2}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \gamma^ {\prime} \beta , 0 \right] \tag {1}
$$
where $\gamma' = (1 - \beta^2)^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ .
The 4-velocity in Equation (1) may be represented now in terms of the corresponding 3-velocity
$$
\underline {{\mathbf {V}}} / (1 - \mathbf {V} ^ {2}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} = \rho \underline {{\mathfrak {n}}} + (1 + \rho^ {2}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \gamma^ {\prime} \beta \underline {{\mathfrak {m}}} \tag {2}
$$
where $\underline{\mathfrak{n}}$ and $\underline{\mathfrak{m}}$ are perpendicular unit vectors, and may be manipulated as if they lived in a cartesian space. We can solve for $\mathbf{V}^2$ and write Equation (2) as
$$
\underline {{\mathbf {V}}} = \left[ \frac {1 - \beta^ {2}}{1 + \rho^ {2}} \right] ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \rho \underline {{\mathbf {n}}} + \beta \underline {{\mathbf {m}}}. \tag {3}
$$
This equation gives us the general geodesic of velocity space. One picks a $\beta (|\beta | < 1)$ , two perpendicular vectors $\mathfrak{m}$ , $\mathfrak{n}$ and gets a geodesic parameterized by $\rho (-\infty < \rho < +\infty)$ . From Equation (3) it is clear that the general geodesic is a "straight line." (This does not mean the space is flat!)
For our problem there are two cases. In case (i), in which either angle $\mathrm{OV}_1\mathrm{V}_2$ or $\mathrm{OV}_2\mathrm{V}_1$ is obtuse, the distance of closest approach to the origin $\mathrm{O}$ of the geodesic (straight line) connecting $\underline{\mathbf{V}}_1$ and $\underline{\mathbf{V}}_2$ is simply $|\underline{\mathbf{V}}_1|$ or $|\underline{\mathbf{V}}_2|$ . In case (ii) in which both $\mathrm{OV}_1\mathrm{V}_2$ and $\mathrm{OV}_2\mathrm{V}_1$ are acute, the distance of closest approach $\beta$ is (by simple geometric arguments)
$$
\beta = \frac {\left| \underset {\sim} {V} _ {1} \times \underset {\sim} {V} _ {2} \right|}{\left| \underset {\sim} {V} _ {1} - \underset {\sim} {V} _ {2} \right|}.
$$
