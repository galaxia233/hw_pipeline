Problem 8.9. Show that the integral $\int_{\mathbf{S}} \mathbf{F}^{\mu} \mathrm{d}^{3}\Sigma_{\mu}$ , where $\mathbf{F}^{\mu}$ is some vector field and $\mathbf{S}$ is some oriented 3-dimensional hypersurface in spacetime, is independent of the parametrization $\mathbf{x}^{\mu} = \mathbf{x}^{\mu}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c})$ used to describe $\mathbf{S}$ . [See Problem 3.30 for the definition of $\mathrm{d}^{3}\Sigma_{\mu}$ .]

## Solution

Solution 8.9. The volume element is
$$
\mathsf {d} ^ {3} \Sigma_ {\mu} (\mathsf {a}, \mathsf {b}, \mathsf {c}) = \frac {1}{3 !} \varepsilon_ {\mu \nu} \beta \sigma \frac {\partial (\mathbf {x} ^ {\nu} , \mathbf {x} ^ {\beta} , \mathbf {x} ^ {\sigma})}{\partial (\mathsf {a} , \mathsf {b} , \mathsf {c})} \mathsf {d a} \mathsf {d b} \mathsf {d c}.
$$
If we change parameterization from $a, b, c$ to $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ then
$$
\mathrm {d} a \mathrm {d} b \mathrm {d} c \rightarrow \left| \frac {\partial (a , b , c)}{\partial (\alpha , \beta , \gamma)} \right| d \alpha \mathrm {d} \beta \mathrm {d} \gamma .
$$
Provided $(a, \beta, \gamma)$ has the same orientation as $(a, b, c)$ , the Jacobian is positive (definition of same orientation!), so we can drop the absolute value signs, and
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathrm {d} ^ {3} \Sigma_ {\mu} (\mathrm {a}, \mathrm {b}, \mathrm {c}) \rightarrow \frac {1}{3 !} \varepsilon_ {\mu \nu \rho \sigma} \frac {\partial \left(\mathrm {x} ^ {\nu} , \mathrm {x} ^ {\rho} , \mathrm {x} ^ {\sigma}\right)}{\partial (\mathrm {a} , \mathrm {b} , \mathrm {c})} \frac {\partial (\mathrm {a} , \mathrm {b} , \mathrm {c})}{\partial (\alpha , \beta , \gamma)} \mathrm {d} \alpha \mathrm {d} \beta \mathrm {d} \gamma \\ = \frac {1}{3 !} \varepsilon_ {\mu \nu \rho \sigma} \frac {\partial \left(\mathbf {x} ^ {\nu} , \mathbf {x} ^ {\rho} , \mathbf {x} ^ {\sigma}\right)}{\partial (\alpha , \beta , \gamma)} d \alpha d \beta d \gamma \\ = \mathrm {d} ^ {3} \Sigma_ {\mu} (\alpha , \beta , \gamma) \\ \end{array}
$$
which completes the proof. With differential forms, the invariance is obvious since $\mathbf{d}^3 \Sigma_{\mu}$ can be viewed as a 3-form
$$
\mathrm {d} ^ {3} \Sigma_ {\mu} = \frac {1}{3 !} \varepsilon_ {\mu \nu \rho \sigma} \widetilde {\mathrm {d x}} ^ {\nu} \wedge \widetilde {\mathrm {d x}} ^ {\rho} \wedge \widetilde {\mathrm {d x}} ^ {\sigma}.
$$
