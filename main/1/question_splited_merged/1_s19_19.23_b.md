19.23 A closed $(\mathbf{k} = 1)$ Friedmann universe has Hubble constant $H_0$ and deceleration parameter $q_0$ . Assume that the universe has always been matter dominated.
(b) What is the total proper volume that we see, looking out into the sky?

## Solution

(b) Our past light cone sweeps outward a proper radial distance $c \, dt$ in a time $dt$ (see figure). From the metric, the proper area of a 2-sphere is $4\pi r^2 R^2$ , where $r$ and $R$ are both functions of $t$ along the light cone. Thus, the visible volume is $(c = 1 \, \text{now})$
$$
\mathbf {V} = \int_ {0} ^ {\mathrm {t} _ {0}} \mathrm {d t} 4 \pi \mathrm {r} ^ {2} (\mathrm {t}) \mathbf {R} ^ {2} (\mathrm {t}). \tag {3}
$$
The differential equation for $\mathbf{r}(t)$ follows from its definition as a null cone:
$$
- \mathrm {d t} = \frac {\mathrm {R} (\mathrm {t})}{\left(1 - \mathrm {r} ^ {2}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}} \mathrm {d r}, \tag {4}
$$
and $\mathbf{r} = 0$ at $t = t_0$ . To solve Equation (4) and evaluate the integral in
Equation (3), we use the solution for $\mathbf{R}(t)$ parameterized by "development angle" $\eta$ (see the solution to Problem 19.18).
$$
R = A (1 - \cos \eta), \quad t = A (\eta - \sin \eta) \tag {5}
$$
where
$$
A = \frac {q _ {0}}{\left(2 q _ {0} - 1\right) ^ {\frac {3}{2}} H _ {0}} \tag {6}
$$
and the current value of $\eta$ is
$$
\eta_ {0} = \cos^ {- 1} \left(\frac {1 - q _ {0}}{q _ {0}}\right). \tag {7}
$$
Equation (4) now becomes $\mathrm{d}(\sin^{-1}\mathbf{r}) = \mathrm{d}\eta$ so that
$$
r = \sin (\eta_ {0} - \eta) \tag {8}
$$
and Equation (3) becomes
$$
\mathbf {V} = \int_ {0} ^ {\eta_ {0}} \mathbf {A} (1 - \cos \eta) \mathrm {d} \eta 4 \pi \sin^ {2} (\eta_ {0} - \eta) \mathbf {A} ^ {2} (1 - \cos \eta) ^ {2} .
$$
This straightforward integral gives
$$
V = 4 \pi A ^ {3} \left(\frac {6 1}{8 0} \sin 2 \eta_ {0} + \frac {5}{4} \eta_ {0} - \frac {5}{2} \sin \eta_ {0} - \frac {3}{8} \eta_ {0} \cos 2 \eta_ {0} + \frac {1}{3 0} \sin 3 \eta_ {0}\right)
$$
which can be reduced with Equations (6) and (7) to a function of $\mathbf{q}_0$ and $\mathbf{H}_0$ .
