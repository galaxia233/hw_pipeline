15.16. Prove that the metric,
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = - \mathrm {d t} ^ {2} + \frac {4}{9} \left[ \frac {9 \mathrm {M}}{2 (\mathrm {r} - \mathrm {t})} \right] ^ {2 / 3} \mathrm {d r} ^ {2} + \left[ \frac {9 \mathrm {M}}{2} (\mathrm {r} - \mathrm {t}) ^ {2} \right] ^ {2 / 3} \mathrm {d} \Omega^ {2}
$$
(which looks dynamical because the metric coefficients depend on $t$ ) is actually static. Show that it is in fact the Schwarzschild geometry.

## Solution

Solution 15.16. To avoid the suggestive connotations of coordinate names, relabel $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{t}$ as follows: $\mathbf{r} \rightarrow \mathbf{z}$ , $\mathbf{t} \rightarrow \mathbf{w}$ . The metric then takes the form
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = - \mathrm {d w} ^ {2} + \frac {4}{9} \left[ \frac {(9 / 2) \mathrm {M}}{z - \mathrm {w}} \right] ^ {\frac {2}{3}} \mathrm {d z} ^ {2} + \left[ (9 / 2) \mathrm {M} (z - \mathrm {w}) ^ {2} \right] ^ {\frac {2}{3}} \mathrm {d} \Omega^ {2}. \tag {1}
$$
Now, define a new coordinate, $\mathbf{r}$ , by
$$
r = \left[ \frac {9}{2} M (z - w) ^ {2} \right] ^ {\frac {1}{3}}. \tag {2}
$$
(We are led to do this because Equation (1) is manifestly spherically symmetric and therefore, the coefficient of $\mathrm{d}\Omega^2$ is a geometrically defined curvature radius.) With r thus defined we have
$$
\begin{array}{l} - \left(\frac {2 r ^ {3}}{9 M}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} + z = w (3a) \\ \mathrm {d} \mathbf {w} = \mathrm {d} \mathbf {z} - \left(\frac {\mathbf {r}}{2 \mathbf {M}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \mathrm {d} \mathbf {r}. (3b) \\ \end{array}
$$
Substituting Equations (2), (3) into Equation (1) gives:
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = - \left(1 - \frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {r}}\right) \mathrm {d z} ^ {2} + 2 \left(\frac {\mathrm {r}}{2 \mathrm {M}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \mathrm {d z} \mathrm {d r} - \frac {\mathrm {r}}{2 \mathrm {M}} \mathrm {d r} ^ {2} + \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \mathrm {d} \Omega^ {2}. \tag {4}
$$
Next we want to try to diagonalize the $\mathbf{dz}^2$ , $\mathbf{dz} \, \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}$ , and $\mathbf{dr}^2$ terms: Define a coordinate $t$ and a function $F(r)$ by
$$
\mathbf {z} = \mathbf {t} + \mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r}), \tag {5}
$$
substitute this into Equation (4):
$$
\begin{array}{l} d s ^ {2} = - \left(1 - \frac {2 M}{r}\right) \left(d t ^ {2} + 2 F ^ {\prime} d t d r + F ^ {\prime 2} d r ^ {2}\right) \\ + 2 \left(\frac {\mathrm {r}}{2 \mathrm {M}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \mathrm {d r} (\mathrm {d t} + \mathrm {F} ^ {\prime} \mathrm {d r}) - \cdot \frac {\mathrm {r}}{2 \mathrm {M}} \mathrm {d r} ^ {2} + \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \mathrm {d} \Omega^ {2}, \tag {6} \\ \end{array}
$$
and choose $\mathbf{F}$ so that metric is diagonal, i.e.
$$
\left(1 - \frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {r}}\right) \mathrm {F} ^ {\prime} = \left(\frac {\mathrm {r}}{2 \mathrm {M}}\right) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}. \tag {7}
$$
With this choice for $\mathbf{F}^{\prime}$ , Equation (6) becomes
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = - \left(1 - \frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {r}}\right) \mathrm {d t} ^ {2} - \left(1 - \frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {r}}\right) ^ {- 1} \left(\frac {\mathrm {r}}{2 \mathrm {M}}\right) \mathrm {d r} ^ {2} \\ + 2 \left(\frac {\mathrm {r}}{2 \mathrm {M}}\right) \left(1 - \frac {2 \mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {r}}\right) ^ {- 1} \mathrm {d r} ^ {2} - \frac {\mathrm {r}}{2 \mathrm {M}} \mathrm {d r} ^ {2} + \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \mathrm {d} \Omega^ {2} \\ \end{array}
$$
or
$$
d s ^ {2} = - \left(1 - \frac {2 M}{r}\right) d t ^ {2} + \left(1 - \frac {2 M}{r}\right) ^ {- 1} d r ^ {2} + r ^ {2} d \Omega^ {2} \tag {8}
$$
which is the Schwarzschild metric in curvature coordinates and is, of course, a static spacetime. The original coordinates of Equation (1) are called “Lemaitre coordinates.” Its time coordinate $w$ measures the proper time of freely-infalling observers; each observer moves along a line $z =$ constant.
