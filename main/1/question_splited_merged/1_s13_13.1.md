Problem 13.1. A somewhat generalized form of the Einstein field equations is
$$
R _ {\mu \nu} - a g _ {\mu \nu} R = 8 \pi T _ {\mu \nu}
$$
where $\alpha$ is some dimensionless constant. Show that if $\alpha$ is not $\frac{1}{2}$ the field equations disagree with experiment, even in the Newtonian limit.

## Solution

Solution 13.1. According to the Bianchi identities the Einstein tensor $\mathbf{R}_{\mu \nu} - \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{g}_{\mu \nu}\mathbf{R}$ is divergenceless so that the divergence of our generalized field equation is
$$
\left(\frac {1}{2} - a\right) \mathrm {R} _ {, \mu} = 8 \pi \mathrm {T} _ {\mu} ^ {\nu} _ {; \nu}.
$$
On the other hand, if we contract our field equations first and then differentiate we find
$$
(1 - 4 a) R _ {, \mu} = 8 \pi T _ {, \mu}
$$
so that the equations of motion must be
$$
\mathrm {T} _ {\mu ; \nu} ^ {\nu} = \kappa \mathrm {T} _ {, \mu}, \quad \kappa \equiv \frac {\frac {1}{2} - a}{1 - 4 a}.
$$
In the Newtonian limit, and for a fluid with density $\rho$ and negligible pressures, the $\mu = 0$ component of this equation is
$$
\frac {\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho \underline {{v}}) = \kappa \frac {\partial \rho}{\partial t}
$$
where $\underline{\mathbf{v}}$ is the velocity of fluid flow. If $\kappa$ were not zero (i.e. if $\alpha$ were not $\frac{1}{2}$ ) this would differ from the Newtonian continuity equation, and violate the conservation of mass in Newtonian order.
