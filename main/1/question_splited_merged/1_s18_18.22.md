Problem 18.22. Consider the metric $\mathrm{ds}^2 = \mathrm{dx}^2 + \mathrm{dy}^2 - \mathrm{dudv} + 2\mathrm{H}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{u})\mathrm{du}^2$ . What form must the function $\mathbf{H}$ have for this to represent a (strong) plane gravitational wave propagating in vacuum?

## Solution

Solution 18.22. The function $H$ is determined by the vacuum field equations $R_{\alpha \beta} = 0$ . We first compute the Christoffel symbols from the Lagrangian for geodesics (see Problem 7.25)
$$
\mathbf {L} = \dot {\mathbf {x}} ^ {2} + \dot {\mathbf {y}} ^ {2} - \dot {\mathbf {u}} \dot {\mathbf {v}} + 2 \mathrm {H} \dot {\mathbf {u}} ^ {2}.
$$
From the Euler-Lagrange equation
$$
\frac {d}{d s} \left(\frac {\partial L}{\partial \dot {\mathbf {x}}}\right) - \frac {\partial L}{\partial \mathbf {x}} = 0
$$
we get
$$
\ddot {\mathbf {x}} + \mathrm {H} _ {, \mathbf {x}} \dot {\mathbf {u}} ^ {2} = 0
$$
and therefore $\Gamma_{uu}^{\mathbf{x}} = H_{,x}$ . Similarly $\ddot{y} + H_{,y} \dot{u}^{2} = 0$ implies $\Gamma_{uu}^{\mathbf{y}} = H_{,y}$ and $u = 0$ implies $\Gamma_{\alpha \beta}^{\mathbf{u}} = 0$ . From
$$
\ddot {\mathrm {v}} - 2 \mathrm {H} _ {, \mathrm {u}} \dot {\mathrm {u}} ^ {2} - 4 \mathrm {H} _ {, \mathrm {x}} \dot {\mathrm {x}} \dot {\mathrm {u}} - 4 \mathrm {H} _ {, \mathrm {y}} \dot {\mathrm {y}} \dot {\mathrm {u}} = 0
$$
we get
$$
\Gamma_ {u u} ^ {v} = - 2 H _ {, u} \quad \Gamma_ {x u} ^ {v} = - 2 H _ {, x} \quad \Gamma_ {y u} ^ {v} = - 2 H _ {, y}.
$$
All other $\Gamma$ 's are zero. In the expression (MTW, Equation 8.51b)
$$
\mathrm {R} _ {\alpha \beta} = \Gamma^ {\mu} _ {\alpha \beta , \mu} - (\log (- \mathbf {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}) _ {, \alpha \beta} + (\log (- \mathbf {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}) _ {, \mu} \Gamma^ {\mu} _ {\alpha \beta} - \Gamma^ {\mu} _ {\sigma \beta} \Gamma^ {\sigma} _ {\alpha \mu}
$$
only the first term contributes (note that $(-g)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 1$ ). We find the only nonzero term to be
$$
\mathrm {R} _ {\mathrm {u u}} = \mathrm {H} _ {, \mathrm {x x}} + \mathrm {H} _ {, \mathrm {y y}}.
$$
Any harmonic function in $\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{y}$ will therefore satisfy the field equations. For a plane wave, $\mathbf{H}$ is a quadratic function in $\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{y}$ , so that (exercise!) the Riemann tensor is a function of $\mathbf{u}$ alone and has no singularities in the $\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}$ plane.
