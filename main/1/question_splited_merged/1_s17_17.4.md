Problem 17.4. Show that Kepler's law
$$
\Omega^ {2} = \mathrm {M} / \mathrm {r} ^ {3}
$$
holds exactly for circular orbits around a Schwarzschild black hole, if $r$ is the curvature coordinate radius, and $\Omega$ is the angular frequency as measured from infinity. Derive an analogous law for equatorial orbits around a Kerr black hole of specific angular momentum $a$ .

## Solution

Solution 17.4. We derive the case for a Kerr black hole and then obtain the Schwarzschild case by setting $\mathbf{a} = 0$ . The Kerr metric is (in the usual Boyer-Lindquist coordinates)
$$
\begin{array}{l} d s ^ {2} = - \left(1 - \frac {2 M r}{\Sigma}\right) d t ^ {2} - \left(4 M a r \frac {\sin^ {2} \theta}{\Sigma}\right) d t d \phi + \frac {\Sigma}{\Delta} d r ^ {2} \tag {1} \\ + \Sigma d \theta^ {2} + \left(r ^ {2} + a ^ {2} + 2 M a ^ {2} r \frac {\sin^ {2} \theta}{\Sigma}\right) \sin^ {2} \theta d \phi^ {2} \\ \end{array}
$$
where
$$
\Delta \equiv r ^ {2} - 2 M r + a ^ {2}, \quad \Sigma \equiv r ^ {2} + a ^ {2} \cos^ {2} \theta .
$$
The geodesic equation is
$$
\mathrm {d} ^ {2} \mathrm {x} ^ {\mu} / \mathrm {d} \tau^ {2} + \Gamma_ {\alpha \beta} ^ {\mu} \mathrm {u} ^ {\alpha} \mathrm {u} ^ {\beta} = 0 \tag {2}
$$
but the first term vanishes since $\mathbf{u}^{\mathrm{t}}$ and $\mathbf{u}^{\phi}$ , the nonvanishing components of $\mathbf{u}$ , are both constant. The $r$ component of Equation (2) is
$$
0 = \Gamma_ {\mathrm {r} \alpha \beta} \mathrm {u} ^ {\alpha} \mathrm {u} ^ {\beta} = \left(\Gamma_ {\mathrm {r t t}} \mathrm {d t} ^ {2} + 2 \Gamma_ {\mathrm {r t}} \phi \mathrm {d t} \mathrm {d} \phi + \Gamma_ {\mathrm {r}} \phi \phi \mathrm {d} \phi^ {2}\right) (\mathrm {d} \tau) ^ {- 2}. \tag {3}
$$
Since the metric coefficients depend only on $\mathbf{r}$ and $\theta$ , the relevant $\Gamma$ 's in the equatorial $(\theta = \pi / 2)$ plane are
$$
\Gamma_ {\mathrm {r t t}} = - \frac {1}{2} \mathrm {g} _ {\mathrm {t t}, \mathrm {r}} = \mathrm {M} / \mathrm {r} ^ {2}
$$
$$
2 \Gamma_ {\mathrm {r t} \phi} = - \mathrm {g} _ {\mathrm {t} \phi , \mathrm {r}} = - 2 \mathrm {M a} / \mathrm {r} ^ {2} \tag {4}
$$
$$
\Gamma_ {\mathbf {r} \phi \phi} = - \frac {1}{2} g _ {\phi \phi , \mathbf {r}} = \frac {\mathrm {M a} ^ {2}}{\mathrm {r} ^ {2}} - \mathrm {r}.
$$
So if $\Omega \equiv \mathrm{d}\phi/\mathrm{d}t$ , Equations (3) and (4) give
$$
0 = \Omega^ {2} \left(\frac {\mathrm {M a} ^ {2}}{\mathrm {r} ^ {2}} - \mathrm {r}\right) - \frac {2 \mathrm {M a}}{\mathrm {r} ^ {2}} \Omega + \frac {\mathrm {M}}{\mathrm {r} ^ {2}}, \tag {5}
$$
a quadratic whose two roots (corresponding to direct and retrograde orbits) are
$$
\Omega = \mathrm {M} ^ {\frac {1}{2}} / \left(\pm \mathrm {r} ^ {\frac {3}{2}} + \mathrm {a M} ^ {\frac {1}{2}}\right). \tag {6}
$$
For $a = 0$ this becomes $\Omega^2 = \mathbf{M} / \mathbf{r}^3$ which (by coincidence in these coordinates) is exactly the Newtonian Kepler's law.
