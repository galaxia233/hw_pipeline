Problem 13.17. Show that in linearized theory there is no attractive gravitational force between two thin parallel beams of light.

## Solution

Solution 13.17. For a beam of light moving in the $x$ -direction nonzero components of the stress-energy tensor are $\mathbf{T}_{00} = \mathbf{T}_{xx} = -\mathbf{T}_{0x}$ . From the form of the linearized field equations
$$
\square \bar {h} _ { \alpha \beta } \equiv \bar {h} _ { \alpha \beta , \gamma }, ^ { \gamma } = - 1 6 \pi T _ { \alpha \beta }
$$
and from the fact that all components of $\mathbf{h}_{\alpha \beta}$ should be retarded integrals over the source, the solutions must be related in the same way that the sources are:
$$
\overline {{\mathbf {h}}} _ { 0 0 } = \overline {{\mathbf {h}}} _ { \mathbf {x x } } = - \overline {{\mathbf {h}}} _ { 0 \mathbf {x } },
$$
with all other components zero. Since the trace of $\overline{\mathbf{h}}_{\alpha \beta}$ is zero, $\overline{\mathbf{h}}_{\alpha \beta} = \mathbf{h}_{\alpha \beta}$ and the above relation is also true if we remove the bars on the h's.
For a photon moving in the $x$ -direction with affine parameter $\lambda$ ,
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} ^ {2} \mathbf {y}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda^ {2}} = - \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} ^ {\alpha}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} ^ {\beta}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda} \Gamma_ {\alpha \beta} ^ {\mathbf {y}} = - \left(\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {t}}{\mathrm {d} \lambda}\right) ^ {2} \left(\Gamma_ {\mathbf {\Lambda} _ {0 0}} ^ {\mathbf {y}} + \Gamma_ {\mathbf {\Lambda} _ {\mathbf {x x}}} ^ {\mathbf {y}} + 2 \Gamma_ {\mathbf {\Lambda} _ {0 \mathbf {x}}} ^ {\mathbf {y}}\right).
$$
But the right hand side vanishes
$$
2 \Gamma^ {\mathbf {y}} _ {0 \mathbf {x}} + \Gamma^ {\mathbf {y}} _ {0 0} + \Gamma^ {\mathbf {y}} _ {\mathbf {x x}} = - \frac {1}{2} \left(h _ {0 0, \mathbf {y}} + h _ {\mathbf {x x}, \mathbf {y}} - 2 h _ {0 \mathbf {x}, \mathbf {y}}\right) = 0
$$
so that $\mathrm{d}^2\mathrm{y} / \mathrm{d}\lambda^2 = 0$ and similarly $\mathrm{d}^2 z / \mathrm{d}\lambda^2 = 0$ . Thus the photon continues to move parallel to the beam of light and therefore two thin beams of light initially moving parallel will not attract each other to the lowest order in each of their mass-energies. (In fact, it turns out that there is no attraction even in higher order.)
