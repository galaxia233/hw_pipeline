Problem 18.4. Calculate the gravitational radiation luminosity of a spinning thin metal rod of mass M and length $\ell$ , spinning at frequency $\omega$ around a symmetrical perpendicular axis. Estimate the electromagnetic luminosity which would arise from the slight excess of electrons pushed toward the ends by centrifugal force. If the rod has a reasonable density $(10\mathrm{gm} / \mathrm{cm}^3)$ and is rotating at a reasonable frequency (1 kHz) will electromagnetic or gravitational radiation be more important in slowing the rotation?

## Solution

Solution 18.4. Assume the rod to be in the xy plane rotating about the $z$ axis. If $\mu$ is the mass per unit length of the rod the time dependent parts of the reduced quadrupole tensor are
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbf {T} _ {\mathbf {x x}} = \mu \cos^ {2} (\omega t) \cdot 2 \int_ {0} ^ {\ell / 2} \mathbf {x} ^ {2} d \mathbf {x} \\ = \frac {\mu \ell^ {3}}{1 2} \cos^ {2} \omega t = \frac {M \ell^ {2}}{2 4} \cos (2 \omega t) + \text {c o n s t a n t} \\ \end{array}
$$
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbf {I} _ {\mathrm {y y}} = - \frac {\mathrm {M} \ell^ {2}}{2 4} \cos 2 \omega t + \text {c o n s t a n t} \\ \mathbf {F} _ {\mathrm {x y}} = \mathbf {F} _ {\mathrm {y x}} = \frac {M \ell^ {2}}{2 4} \sin 2 \omega t, \\ \end{array}
$$
so that
$$
\begin{array}{l} L _ {G W} = \frac {1}{5} <   \ddot {F} _ {j k} \dot {F} _ {j k} > = \frac {(2 \omega) ^ {6}}{5} \left(\frac {M l ^ {2}}{2 4}\right) ^ {2} <   2 \cos^ {2} 2 \omega t + 2 \sin^ {2} 2 \omega t > \\ = \frac {2}{4 5} \omega^ {6} M ^ {2} \ell^ {4}. (\text {N o t e u n i t s w i t h} G = c = 1!) \\ \end{array}
$$
To calculate the charge centrifuged to the ends of the bar, balance electrostatic and centripetal forces:
$$
\left| e \nabla \Phi \right| = r m \omega^ {2},
$$
where $\Phi$ is the electrostatic potential and $e$ and $m$ are the charge and mass of an electron. Thus the charge density induced will be of order
$$
\rho \sim - \nabla^ {2} \Phi \sim - (\mathrm {m} / \mathrm {e}) \omega^ {2}.
$$
This charge depletion in the main part of the rod will of course be balanced by electrons squashed against the ends with charge $(\mathfrak{m} / \mathfrak{e})\omega^{2}\ell \mathbf{A}$ where $\mathbf{A} =$ rod cross section. The rod thus becomes an electric quadrupole with quadrupole moment of order $(\mathrm{m / e})\omega^{2}\ell^{3}\mathrm{A}$ . The electric quadrupole radiation due to this induced moment will be roughly $\omega^6$ times the square of the quadrupole moment, or roughly $\omega^{10}(\mathrm{m / e})^{2}\ell^{6}\mathrm{A}^{2}$ . The ratio of electromagnetic damping to gravitational damping is then
$$
\frac {\mathrm {L} _ {\mathrm {E M}}}{\mathrm {L} _ {\mathrm {G W}}} \sim \frac {\omega^ {1 0} (\mathrm {m / e}) ^ {2} \ell^ {6} \mathrm {A} ^ {2}}{\omega^ {6} \rho^ {2} \mathrm {A} ^ {2} \ell^ {6}} = \left[ \frac {(\mathrm {m / e}) ^ {2} \omega^ {2}}{\rho} \right] ^ {2}.
$$
$$
\begin{array}{l} \begin{array}{l} \text {I n G = c = 1 u n i t s (m / e) \approx \frac {1}{2} \times 1 0 ^ {- 2 1} a n d \rho (1 0 g / c m ^ {3}) \approx 1 0 ^ {- 2 7} a n d} \\ \omega (1 k H _ {z}) \approx \frac {1}{3} \times 1 0 ^ {- 7} \text {s o t h a t} \end{array} \\ \mathrm {L} _ {\mathrm {E M}} / \mathrm {L} _ {\mathrm {G W}} \sim 1 0 ^ {- 1 8}. \\ \end{array}
$$
For once gravitational radiation wins out.
