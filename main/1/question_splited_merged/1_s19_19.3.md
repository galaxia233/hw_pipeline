Problem 19.3. An object emits black body radiation of temperature $T$ in its own rest frame; we see it at a redshift $z$ and subtending a solid angle $\Omega$ . What flux do we measure? What if the redshift is due to doppler motion of a local object instead of a cosmological redshift?

## Solution

Solution 19.3. By Liouville's theorem the number density of photons in phase space $\mathbf{I}_{\nu} / \nu^3$ is a constant along a light ray, and (see Problem 5.10)
it is also a Lorentz invariant. Using $(\nu_{\text{emitted}} / \nu_{\text{observed}}) = 1 + z$ , We have then
$$
\begin{array}{l} \text {F l u x o b s e r v e d} = \Omega \int I _ {\nu} ^ {\mathrm {o b s}} \mathrm {d} \nu_ {\mathrm {o b s}} = \Omega \int \left(\frac {I _ {\nu} ^ {\mathrm {o b s}}}{\nu_ {\mathrm {o b s}} ^ {3}}\right) \nu_ {\mathrm {o b s}} ^ {3} \mathrm {d} \nu_ {\mathrm {o b s}} \\ = \Omega \int \frac {\mathrm {I} _ {\nu} ^ {\mathrm {e m i t}}}{\nu_ {\mathrm {e m i t}} ^ {3}} \frac {\nu_ {\mathrm {e m i t}} ^ {3} \mathrm {d} \nu_ {\mathrm {e m i t}}}{(1 + z) ^ {4}} = \frac {\Omega}{(1 + z) ^ {4}} \int \mathrm {I} _ {\nu} ^ {\mathrm {e m i t}} \mathrm {d} \nu_ {\mathrm {e m i t}} \\ = \frac {\Omega}{(1 + z) ^ {4}} \frac {1}{\pi} \sigma \mathrm {T} ^ {4} \\ \end{array}
$$
where $\sigma$ is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant. Note that since a redshifted black body spectrum is also a black body spectrum, the flux and spectrum observed is just that which would come from a nearby stationary object of solid angle $\Omega$ and temperature $T / (1 + z)$ .
In all the above it doesn't matter whether the redshift is due to doppler motion, gravitational redshift, or cosmological redshift, because Liouville's theorem holds in general.
