Problem 14.1. Write the stress-energy tensor for a single free particle, and show that the equation of geodesic motion follows from $\mathbf{T}^{\mu \nu}_{,\nu} = 0$ .

## Solution

Solution 14.1. First we determine what is the appropriate $\mathbf{T}^{\mu \nu}$ for a point particle, say of mass m. In the momentarily comoving frame it should have only a $\mathbf{T}^{00}$ component, and this should be a $\delta$ -function of
position, so
$$
\mathbf {T} ^ {\mu \nu} \propto \int \delta^ {4} (\mathbf {x} ^ {\alpha} - \mathbf {x} ^ {\alpha} (\tau)) \mathbf {u} ^ {\mu} \mathbf {u} ^ {\nu} d \tau
$$
where $\mathbf{x}^{\alpha}(r)$ is the particle's trajectory in spacetime as a function of its proper time $r$ . Now we want $\mathbf{T}^{\mu \nu}$ to transform like a tensor. The product $\mathbf{u}^{\mu}\mathbf{u}^{\nu}$ already transforms as a tensor, but $\delta^{4}(\mathbf{x}^{\alpha} - \mathbf{x}^{\alpha}(r))$ is not a scalar; $\delta^{4}(\mathbf{x}^{\alpha} - \mathbf{x}^{\alpha}(r))(-\mathbf{g})^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ , however, is a scalar:
$$
1 = \operatorname {s c a l a r} = \int \delta^ {4} \left(\mathrm {x} ^ {a} - \mathrm {x} ^ {a} (\tau)\right) \mathrm {d} ^ {4} \mathrm {x} = \int \frac {\delta^ {4}}{(- \mathrm {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}} (- \mathrm {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}} \mathrm {d} ^ {4} \mathrm {x}. \tag {1}
$$
Thus, normalizing to mass $\mathfrak{m}$ , we have
$$
\mathrm {T} ^ {\mu \nu} = \mathrm {m} \int \delta^ {4} \left(\mathrm {x} ^ {\alpha} - \mathrm {x} ^ {\alpha} (\tau)\right) \frac {\mathrm {u} ^ {\mu} \mathrm {u} ^ {\nu}}{(- \mathrm {g}) ^ {\frac {1}{2}}} \mathrm {d} \tau \equiv \int \rho \mathrm {u} ^ {\mu} \mathrm {u} ^ {\nu} \mathrm {d} \tau \tag {2}
$$
where $\rho$ contains all the terms except the u's. Now
$$
0 = \mathrm {T} ^ {\mu \nu} _ {; \nu} = \int [ (\rho \mathrm {u} ^ {\nu}) _ {; \nu} \mathrm {u} ^ {\mu} + (\rho \mathrm {u} ^ {\nu}) \mathrm {u} _ {; \nu} ^ {\mu} ] \mathrm {d} \tau . \tag {3}
$$
Dotting with $\mathfrak{u}^{\mu}$ gives
$$
0 = \int \left[ - \left(\rho \mathbf {u} ^ {\nu}\right) _ {; \nu} + \rho \left(\nabla_ {\mathbf {u}} \mathbf {u}\right) \cdot \mathbf {u} \right] d \tau . \tag {4}
$$
The second term vanishes because 4-acceleration $\nabla_{\mathbf{u}}\mathbf{u}$ is orthogonal to 4-velocity. We conclude therefore that the first term vanishes and hence, from Equation (3)
$$
0 = \int (\rho u ^ {\nu}) u _ {; \nu} ^ {\mu} d \tau .
$$
This implies that $\mathfrak{u}^{\nu}\mathfrak{u}_{;\nu}^{\mu} = 0$ wherever the (delta function) $\rho$ is nonzero, that is, wherever the particle is! But $\mathfrak{u}^{\nu}\mathfrak{u}_{;\nu}^{\mu} = 0$ is just the geodesic equation.
