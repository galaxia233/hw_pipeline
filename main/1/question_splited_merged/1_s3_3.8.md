Problem 3.8. In a coordinate system with coordinates $\mathbf{x}^{\mu}$ , the invariant line element is $\mathrm{ds}^2 = \eta_{\alpha \beta} \mathrm{dx}^\alpha \mathrm{dx}^\beta$ . If the coordinates are transformed $\mathbf{x}^{\mu} \rightarrow \overline{\mathbf{x}}^{\mu}$ , show that the line element is $\mathrm{ds}^2 = \mathbf{g}_{\overline{\mu} \overline{\nu}} \mathrm{d}\overline{\mathbf{x}}^\mu \mathrm{d}\overline{\mathbf{x}}^\nu$ , and express $\mathbf{g}_{\overline{\mu} \overline{\nu}}$ in terms of the partial derivatives $\partial \mathbf{x}^{\mu} / \partial \overline{\mathbf{x}}^{\nu}$ . For two arbitrary 4-vectors $\mathbf{U}$ and $\mathbf{V}$ , show that
$$
\mathbf {U} \cdot \mathbf {V} = \mathbf {U} ^ {\alpha} \mathbf {V} ^ {\beta} \eta_ {\alpha \beta} = \mathbf {U} ^ {\overline {{\alpha}}} \mathbf {V} ^ {\overline {{\beta}}} \mathbf {g} _ {\overline {{\alpha}}} \overline {{\beta}}.
$$

## Solution

Solution 3.8. We express the differentials in terms of the new coordinates:
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = \eta_ {\alpha \beta} \mathrm {d x} ^ {\alpha} \mathrm {d x} ^ {\beta} \\ = \eta_ {\alpha} \beta \left(\partial \mathbf {x} ^ {\alpha} / \partial \overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\mu}\right) d \overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\mu} \left(\partial \mathbf {x} ^ {\beta} / \partial \overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\nu}\right) d \overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\nu} \\ = \left(\eta_ {a} \beta \frac {\partial \mathbf {x} ^ {a}}{\partial \overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\mu}} \frac {\partial \mathbf {x} ^ {\beta}}{\partial \overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\nu}}\right) d \overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\mu} d \overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\nu}. \\ \end{array}
$$
If we write the line element as $\mathrm{ds}^2 = \mathbf{g}_{\overline{\mu}\overline{\nu}}\mathrm{d}\overline{\mathbf{x}}^\mu \mathrm{d}\overline{\mathbf{x}}^{\nu '}$ it follows that
$$
\mathbf {g} _ {\overline {{{\mu}}} \overline {{{\nu}}}} \equiv \eta_ {\alpha \beta} \frac {\partial \mathbf {x} ^ {\alpha}}{\partial \mathbf {x} ^ {\mu}} \frac {\partial \mathbf {x} ^ {\beta}}{\partial \mathbf {x} ^ {\nu}} .
$$
A vector transforms by the following rule
$$
\mathbf {U} ^ {\alpha} = \frac {\partial_ {\mathbf {x}} \mathbf {\Lambda} ^ {\alpha}}{\partial_ {\overline {{\mathbf {x}}}} \beta} \mathbf {U} ^ {\overline {{\beta}}} ,
$$
therefore
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbf {U} \cdot \mathbf {V} = \mathbf {U} ^ {\sigma} \mathbf {V} ^ {\lambda} \eta_ {\sigma \lambda} \\ = \left(\mathrm {U} ^ {\overline {{\alpha}}} \frac {\partial_ {\mathbf {X}} ^ {\sigma}}{\partial \overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\alpha}}\right) \left(\mathrm {V} ^ {\overline {{\beta}}} \frac {\partial_ {\mathbf {X}} ^ {\lambda}}{\partial \overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\beta}}\right) \eta_ {\sigma \lambda} = \mathrm {U} ^ {\overline {{\alpha}}} \mathrm {V} ^ {\overline {{\beta}}} \frac {\partial_ {\mathbf {X}} ^ {\sigma}}{\partial \overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\alpha}} \frac {\partial_ {\mathbf {X}} ^ {\lambda}}{\partial \overline {{\mathbf {x}}} ^ {\beta}} \eta_ {\sigma \lambda} \\ = \mathrm {U} ^ {\overline {{\alpha}}} \mathrm {V} ^ {\overline {{\beta}}} \mathrm {g} _ {\overline {{\alpha}} \overline {{\beta}}} . \\ \end{array}
$$
