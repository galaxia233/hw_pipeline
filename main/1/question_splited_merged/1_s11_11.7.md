Problem 11.7. Thomas Precession: Consider a ("classical") spinning electron which Fermi-Walker transports its spin angular momentum, S, as it moves in a circular orbit around an atomic nucleus. As seen in the laboratory frame, the electron moves in a circular orbit of radius r in the x-y plane with constant angular velocity, $\omega$ . Calculate S(t), the spin as a function of laboratory time.

## Solution

Solution 11.7. The basic equation (see Problem 11.4) is
$$
\mathrm {d} \mathbf {S} / \mathrm {d} \tau = \mathbf {u} (\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {S}).
$$
In the lab frame, the world line of the particle is
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbf {x} = \mathbf {r} \cos \omega t \\ \mathbf {y} = \mathbf {r} \sin \omega t \\ \end{array}
$$
(where $\omega$ and $\mathbf{r}$ are constants) and hence
$$
u ^ {0} = \gamma , \quad u ^ {x} = \gamma \frac {d x}{d t} = - \omega r \gamma \sin \omega t, \quad u ^ {y} = \gamma \omega r \cos \omega t, \quad u ^ {z} = 0
$$
where $\gamma \equiv (1 - r^2\omega^2)^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ . Now $\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{du} / \mathbf{dt}$ so
$$
\mathsf {a} ^ {0} = 0 (\gamma = \mathsf {c o n s t}), \quad \mathsf {a} ^ {\mathbf {x}} = \gamma \frac {\mathsf {d u} ^ {\mathbf {x}}}{\mathsf {d t}}, \quad \mathsf {a} ^ {\mathbf {y}} = - \gamma^ {2} \omega^ {2} \mathbf {r} \sin \omega \mathsf {t}, \quad \mathsf {a} ^ {\mathbf {z}} = 0
$$
and hence
$$
\mathbf {S} \cdot \mathbf {a} = \mathbf {S} ^ {\mathbf {x}} \mathbf {a} ^ {\mathbf {x}} + \mathbf {S} ^ {\mathbf {y}} \mathbf {a} ^ {\mathbf {y}} = - \omega^ {2} \mathbf {r} \gamma^ {2} \cos \omega t \mathbf {S} ^ {\mathbf {x}} - \omega^ {2} \mathbf {r} \gamma^ {2} \sin \omega t \mathbf {S} ^ {\mathbf {y}}.
$$
We have therefore
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {S} ^ {0}}{\mathrm {d} \tau} = \gamma \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {S} ^ {0}}{\mathrm {d} t} = \mathbf {u} ^ {0} (\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {S}) = \gamma (\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {S}) \tag {1}
$$
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} S ^ {\mathbf {x}}}{\mathrm {d} r} = \gamma \frac {\mathrm {d} S ^ {\mathbf {x}}}{\mathrm {d} t} = u ^ {\mathbf {x}} (\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {S}) = - \omega r \gamma \sin \omega t (\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {S}) \tag {2}
$$
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {S} ^ {\mathbf {y}}}{\mathrm {d} \tau} = \gamma \frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {S} ^ {\mathbf {y}}}{\mathrm {d} t} = \mathbf {u} ^ {\mathbf {y}} (\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {S}) = \omega r \gamma \cos \omega t (\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {S}) \tag {3}
$$
$$
\frac {\mathrm {d} S ^ {z}}{\mathrm {d} r} = 0. \tag {4}
$$
Introduce radial and tangential components:
$$
S ^ {x} = S ^ {r} \cos \omega t - S ^ {\theta} \sin \omega t
$$
$$
\mathbf {S} ^ {\mathbf {y}} = \mathbf {S} ^ {\mathbf {r}} \sin \omega t + \mathbf {S} ^ {\theta} \cos \omega t.
$$
Equations (2) and (3) imply:
$$
\mathrm {d} \mathbf {S} ^ {\mathbf {r}} / \mathrm {d} t = \omega \mathbf {S} ^ {\theta} \tag {5}
$$
$$
\mathrm {d} \mathbf {S} ^ {\theta} / \mathrm {d t} = - \omega \gamma^ {2} \mathbf {S} ^ {\mathbf {r}}. \tag {6}
$$
Equations (5) and (6) imply:
$$
\mathrm {d} ^ {2} \mathbf {S} ^ {\mathrm {r}} / \mathrm {d t} ^ {2} = - \omega^ {2} \gamma^ {2} \mathbf {S} ^ {\mathrm {r}} \Longrightarrow \mathbf {S} ^ {\mathrm {r}} = \mathrm {A} \cos (\omega \gamma t + \alpha), \mathrm {A}, \alpha \text {c o n s t .} \tag {7}
$$
and from Equation (5)
$$
\mathrm {S} ^ {\theta} = - \gamma \mathrm {A} \sin (\omega \gamma t + \alpha). \tag {8}
$$
From Equations (4), (7), (8) we obtain
$$
S ^ {x} = A \left[ \cos \omega t \cos (\omega y t + a) + \gamma \sin \omega t \sin (\omega y t + a) \right] \tag {9a}
$$
$$
\mathbf {S} ^ {\mathcal {Y}} = \mathbf {A} [ \sin \omega t \cos (\omega \gamma t + a) - \gamma \cos \omega t \sin (\omega \gamma t + a) ] \tag {9b}
$$
$$
S ^ {z} = \text {c o n s t .} \tag {9c}
$$
Consider initial conditions $S^{\mathbf{x}} = \hbar (2)^{-\frac{1}{2}}, S^{\mathbf{y}} = 0, S^{\mathbf{z}} = \frac{1}{2} \hbar$ . (Although we are not treating the electron spin quantum mechanically, we want $S^2 = 3\hbar^2 / 4$ .) These conditions imply $\alpha = 0$ , $A = \hbar (2)^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ ; and Equations (9) can be written as
$$
S ^ {x} + i S ^ {y} = 2 ^ {- \frac {1}{2}} \hbar [ e ^ {- i (\gamma - 1) \omega t} + i (1 - \gamma) \sin (\omega \gamma t) e ^ {i \omega t} ]. \tag {10}
$$
The first term on the right hand side of Equation (10) and Equation (9c) indicate a precession around the $z$ -axis with angular velocity
$$
\omega_ {\text {T h o m a s}} = (\gamma - 1) \omega \approx \frac {1}{2} v ^ {2} \omega \tag {11}
$$
while the second term in Equation (10) is small $(1 - \gamma \approx -\frac{1}{2} v^2)$ for an electron in an atom.
