Problem 17.9. Show that there are negative energy particle trajectories inside the ergosphere of a Kerr black hole (and outside the horizon!). Show that it is possible for a rocket ship to increase its total energy by firing a bullet into the hole during an orbital passage through the ergosphere.

## Solution

Solution 17.9. The conserved energy of a particle is the dot product of its 4-momentum with the time Killing vector $\pmb{\xi}_{(t)} = \partial/\partial t$ :
$$
\mathbf {E} = - \mathbf {p} \cdot \left(\frac {\partial}{\partial t}\right) = - \mathbf {p} _ {t}. \tag {1}
$$
The Kerr metric is of the form
$$
\mathrm {d s} ^ {2} = - \mathrm {e} ^ {2 \nu} \mathrm {d t} ^ {2} + \mathrm {e} ^ {2 \psi} (\mathrm {d} \phi - \omega \mathrm {d t}) ^ {2} + \mathrm {e} ^ {2 \mu_ {1}} \mathrm {d r} ^ {2} + \mathrm {e} ^ {2 \mu_ {2}} \mathrm {d} \theta^ {2}. \tag {2}
$$
The contravariant components of $\mathbf{g}^{\alpha \beta}$ are
$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbf {g} ^ {\mathrm {t t}} = - \mathbf {e} ^ {- 2 \nu} \quad \mathbf {g} ^ {\mathrm {t} \phi} = - \omega \mathbf {e} ^ {- 2 \nu} \quad \mathbf {g} ^ {\phi \phi} = \mathbf {e} ^ {- 2 \psi} - \omega^ {2} \mathbf {e} ^ {- 2 \nu} \tag {3} \\ \mathbf {g} ^ {\mathrm {r r}} = \mathbf {e} ^ {- 2 \mu_ {1}} \qquad \mathbf {g} ^ {\theta \theta} = \mathbf {e} ^ {- 2 \mu_ {2}}. \\ \end{array}
$$
If $\mu$ is the mass of the particle, then
$$
\begin{array}{l} - \mu^ {2} = \mathbf {p} \cdot \mathbf {p} = - \mathrm {e} ^ {- 2 \nu} \mathrm {p} _ { \mathrm {t}} ^ {2} - \mathrm {e} ^ {- 2 \nu} 2 \omega \mathrm {p} _ { \mathrm {t}} \mathrm {p} _ { \phi} \tag {4} \\ + \left(\mathrm {e} ^ {- 2 \psi} - \mathrm {e} ^ {- 2 \nu} \omega^ {2}\right) \mathrm {p} _ { \phi} ^ {2} + \mathrm {e} ^ {- 2 \mu_ {1}} \mathrm {p} _ { \mathrm {r}} ^ {2} + \mathrm {e} ^ {- 2 \mu_ {2}} \mathrm {p} _ { \theta} ^ {2}. \\ \end{array}
$$
Solving the quadratic equation (4) for $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{t}}$ we get
$$
E = \omega p _ {\phi} + \left[ e ^ {2 \nu - 2 \psi} p _ { \phi} ^ {2} + e ^ {2 \nu} \left(e ^ {- 2 \mu_ {1}} p _ { r} ^ {2} + e ^ {- 2 \mu_ {2}} p _ { \theta} ^ {2} + \mu^ {2}\right) \right] ^ {\frac {1}{2}}. \tag {5}
$$
The sign of the square root in Equation (5) is positive so that $\mathbf{E} = +\mu$ for a particle at rest at infinity. If $\mathbf{E}$ is to be negative, we must have $\mathsf{p}_{\phi}$ negative and
$$
\left[ \mathrm {e} ^ {2 \nu - 2 \psi} \mathrm {p} _ { \phi} ^ {2} + \mathrm {e} ^ {2 \nu} \left(\mathrm {e} ^ {- 2 \mu_ {1}} \mathrm {p} _ { \mathrm {r}} ^ {2} + \mathrm {e} ^ {- 2 \mu_ {2}} \mathrm {p} _ { \theta} ^ {2} + \mu^ {2}\right) \right] ^ {\frac {1}{2}} <   - \omega \mathrm {p} _ { \phi}. \tag {6}
$$
The boundary of the region of negative $\mathbf{E}$ can be found by letting $\mathfrak{p}_{\mathbf{r}} = \mathfrak{p}_{\theta} = 0$ , and $\mu \to 0$ (i.e. highly relativistic particle). This gives $e^{2\nu - 2\psi} < \omega^2$ which is equivalent to $\mathbf{g}_{\mathbf{tt}} > 0$ . That is, the orbit must be inside the ergosphere. (This result also follows from Problem 10.15.)
When a rocket ship fires a bullet we have conservation of 4-momentum
$$
\mathbf {p} _ {\text {b e f o r e}} = \mathbf {p} _ {\text {a f t e r}} + \mathbf {p} _ {\text {b u l l e t}}. \tag {7}
$$
Dotting this into the time Killing vector gives
$$
E _ {\text {b e f o r e}} = E _ {\text {a f t e r}} + E _ {\text {b u l l e t}}. \tag {8}
$$
Since the rocket ship can fall into the ergosphere from infinity, we have $\mathbf{E}_{\text{before}} > \mu$ . In the ergosphere it can fire a bullet sufficiently fast with negative $p_{\phi}$ so that $\mathbf{E}_{\text{bullet}} < 0$ . Then, by Equation (7), $\mathbf{E}_{\text{after}} > \mathbf{E}_{\text{before}}$ and the rocket coasts off to infinity with increased total energy. Note that since both the rocket's trajectory and the bullet's negative energy trajectory are timelike, the bullet can be fired from the rocket with a locally measured velocity less than $c$ .
